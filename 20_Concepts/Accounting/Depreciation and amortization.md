# Depreciation and amortization

> **TL;DR.** Depreciation and amortization (D&A) are non-cash accounting methods used to gradually recognize the expense of a long-term asset over its useful life, rather than recording the entire cost when the asset is initially purchased.

## When you'd use this

You encounter depreciation and amortization when analyzing a company's income statement and cash flow statement. They are crucial for matching the cost of long-term assets to the revenue they generate over time, ensuring a more accurate representation of a company's profitability in any given period.

## The 30-second version

Depreciation applies to tangible fixed assets (like property, plant, and equipment), reflecting their physical wear and tear or obsolescence. Amortization applies to intangible assets with an identifiable life (like patents or intellectual property). Both methods spread the initial purchase cost over the asset's estimated useful life. Because they don't represent a cash outflow in the current period (the cash was spent when the asset was bought), D&A is added back to net income on the cash flow statement.

## The full explanation

Depreciation and amortization are accrual accounting concepts designed to allocate the cost of an asset over the periods it provides economic value. This aligns with the matching principle in accounting, which states that expenses should be recognized in the same period as the related revenues they help generate.

**Depreciation of Tangible Assets**
When a company purchases a physical asset, such as a factory or machinery, it doesn't immediately expense the full amount. Instead, the asset is capitalized on the balance sheet. Over time, as the asset is used, a portion of its cost is recognized as depreciation expense on the income statement. This systematically reduces the asset's carrying value on the balance sheet. Land is a notable exception; it is not depreciated because it's assumed to have an infinite useful life.

**Amortization of Intangible Assets**
Amortization functions identically to depreciation but applies to intangible assets. When a company acquires an intangible asset, such as a patent, the cost is capitalized and then gradually expensed over the asset's expected useful life.

**Different Methods**
The straight-line method is the most common approach for both, where the asset's cost (minus any estimated salvage value) is divided equally across its useful life. However, other accelerated depreciation methods exist (like declining balance or sum-of-the-years' digits), which recognize higher expenses in the early years of an asset's life.

**Impact on Financial Statements**
- **Income Statement:** D&A is an expense that reduces Pre-Tax Income (and thus, income taxes owed). It is typically embedded within Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) or Operating Expenses (OpEx), rather than being broken out as a separate line item.
- **Cash Flow Statement:** Since D&A are non-cash expenses, they are added back to Net Income at the top of the Cash Flow Statement to arrive at Cash Flow from Operations.
- **Balance Sheet:** Accumulated depreciation reduces the carrying value of Property, Plant, & Equipment (PP&E), while accumulated amortization reduces the value of intangible assets.

## Excel and modeling notes

When building an integrated 3-statement financial model, D&A are often embedded within COGS or OpEx on the income statement. To find the exact D&A amount for modeling purposes (like building the Cash Flow Statement add-back or forecasting PP&E), you usually need to look at the Cash Flow Statement or the footnotes in the financial statements.

## Interview-ready answer

Depreciation and amortization are both non-cash expenses that allocate the cost of an asset over its useful life. The key difference is that depreciation applies to tangible assets, like machinery and buildings, to reflect wear and tear, whereas amortization applies to intangible assets, like patents or trademarks. Because they are non-cash, they are added back to net income on the cash flow statement.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- **Confusing it with cash flow:** D&A reduces net income but it is a non-cash expense; the actual cash left the business when the capital expenditure (CapEx) was originally made.
- **Assuming it's always straight-line:** While straight-line is common for book purposes, companies often use accelerated depreciation for tax purposes to reduce their near-term tax burden.
- **Depreciating land:** Land is not depreciable under U.S. GAAP because it is considered to have an indefinite useful life.
- **Confusing asset amortization with loan amortization:** Loan amortization refers to the scheduled paydown of a loan's principal balance over time, which is a cash outflow, entirely separate from the non-cash amortization of intangible assets.
