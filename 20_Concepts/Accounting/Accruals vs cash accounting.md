# Accruals vs cash accounting

> **TL;DR.** Cash basis accounting records revenue and expenses when money actually changes hands, whereas accrual accounting records them at the moment they occur or are earned, regardless of payment.

## When you'd use this

You need to decide between these two methods when setting up a company's financial operations. Cash accounting is simple and ideal for sole proprietors and small businesses that operate with a lean team and don't offer credit. Accrual accounting is smarter for high-revenue businesses, companies that invoice customers with payment terms (like net 30), or companies preparing for significant growth and seeking funding from venture capital or investment partners.

## The 30-second version

The biggest difference between cash and accrual accounting essentially boils down to timing. Specifically, it dictates when your revenue and expenses are recognized and recorded.

Cash basis accounting is a reactive form of accounting that only notes completed transactions. It is easy to manage but can give an inaccurate picture of your business's overall financial health, especially if there are significant delays between taking orders and receiving payment. Accrual accounting provides a more accurate view of financial position by recording transactions when they happen, though it can be more complicated to manage and doesn't provide direct visibility into current cash-on-hand.

## The full explanation

Accrual accounting means you record your business transactions at the moment they occur, instead of when you receive a payment or make a payment. For example, if you use credit to make a purchase, accrual accounting records the expense at that moment, even if you aren't required to pay it back for weeks.

Similarly, sales are recorded the moment they occur, even if customers have 30, 60, or 90 days to pay. Accrual accounting also applies to prepayments and noncash assets like real estate, investments, and utilities.

### Differences in Financial Snapshots
Because cash accounting only notes completed transactions, it offers a less accurate view of your business's current financial standing. If many customers buy on credit, cash accounting fails to paint a clear picture of your upcoming revenue and expenses.

### Benefits and Drawbacks
The best benefit of accrual accounting is the accuracy it offers decision-makers, giving you granular information about exactly which days are the most profitable. It matches your payments and earnings to the season they occurred in, offering better understanding of seasonal fluctuations.

However, using the accrual accounting method alone doesn't provide much visibility into your company's current cash-on-hand, and it is more challenging to set up and manage than cash accounting, often complicating filings at tax time.

## Worked example

Say your business bills a customer in January for a $1,000 order they placed that month. Your bill terms are net 30, meaning they have a full 30 days to pay for their order. You've sent the shipment and they received the products. Finally, in February, your customer pays their invoice.

Under **cash basis accounting**, you record the revenue in February, when the payment hits your account.

Under **accrual accounting**, you record that $1,000 of revenue in your income statement for January. You also mark that $1,000 in your January "accounts receivable" report as money currently owed to you. Once your customer pays their bill in February, it's simply deducted from your accounts receivable to balance out your books.

## Interview-ready answer

The main difference between cash and accrual accounting is timing. Cash basis accounting records transactions only when cash actually enters or leaves the bank account, while accrual accounting records revenues when they are earned and expenses when they are incurred, offering a more accurate picture of a company's financial standing and upcoming obligations.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- **Inaccurate health metrics:** Cash accounting can oversimplify records and distort your actual financial health if your business has significant delays between taking orders and receiving payments.
- **Lack of cash visibility:** While accrual accounting accurately reflects earned revenue and incurred expenses, it can obscure your actual cash-on-hand, making it necessary to supplement with other financial reports.
