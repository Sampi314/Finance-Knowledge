# Non-current assets

> **TL;DR.** Non-current assets are long-term resources owned by a company that are expected to provide economic value for more than one year.

## When you'd use this

You will look at non-current assets when analyzing the long-term investment profile of a business, assessing its capital intensity, or projecting capital expenditures and depreciation over a multi-year horizon in a financial model. Understanding a company's non-current assets provides insight into how it allocates capital to support long-term growth and generate future revenues.

## The 30-second version

Assets are resources a company owns that provide future economic value. Unlike current assets, which are expected to be converted into cash or used up within 12 months, non-current assets (also known as long-term assets) have a useful life of more than one year. They are relatively illiquid, meaning they cannot be easily or quickly converted into cash.

These assets form the backbone of a company's long-term operations. Common examples include Property, Plant, and Equipment (PP&E) like land, machinery, and buildings; long-term investments; goodwill; and other intangible assets like patents and trademarks. Because they provide value over many years, their costs are typically spread out over time through depreciation (for tangible assets) or amortization (for intangible assets).

## The full explanation

Non-current assets represent the long-term investments a company makes to operate, grow, and maintain a competitive advantage. The assets section of the balance sheet is ordered by liquidity, meaning non-current assets are listed below current assets.

### Tangible vs. Intangible Assets

Non-current assets fall into two broad categories:

1. **Tangible Assets:** These are physical items. Creditors generally prefer tangible assets because they can serve as collateral; if a borrower defaults, the lender can seize and sell the physical asset. Examples include land, buildings, and vehicles.
2. **Intangible Assets:** These lack physical substance but hold economic value. They represent legal rights or competitive advantages. Examples include intellectual property (patents, trademarks) and goodwill. Because there is no physical "thing" to liquidate, creditors often view intangibles as less secure collateral.

### Key Types of Non-Current Assets

*   **Property, Plant & Equipment (PP&E):** Often referred to as fixed assets, PP&E includes physical items crucial for operations, such as factories, heavy machinery, office furniture, and computer equipment. PP&E is usually the primary destination for capital expenditures (CapEx).
*   **Investments:** Companies with surplus cash might invest in debt or equity instruments of other companies, or in joint ventures. If these investments are intended to be held for more than a year, they are classified as non-current assets.
*   **Goodwill:** This arises during mergers and acquisitions. When a company acquires another business for a price higher than the fair market value of the target's net assets, the premium paid is recorded as goodwill. It represents intangible value like brand reputation, customer lists, or expected synergies. Goodwill is not amortized but is tested annually for impairment.
*   **Other Intangible Assets:** This includes patents, trademarks, copyrights, and franchises. Like PP&E, the cost of these assets is systematically reduced over their useful life, but this process is called amortization rather than depreciation.

### Capitalizing and Expensing

A key accounting principle (the matching principle) dictates that costs should be recognized in the same period as the revenues they help generate. Therefore, when a company buys a non-current asset (e.g., a $500,000 piece of machinery with a 5-year useful life), it does not record a $500,000 expense immediately.

Instead, the purchase is **capitalized**—recorded as an asset on the balance sheet. Over the next 5 years, the cost is gradually shifted from the balance sheet to the income statement as a depreciation expense. The asset is then shown on the balance sheet net of accumulated depreciation, representing its remaining "book value." Land is a notable exception; it is assumed to have an indefinite useful life and is not depreciated.

### Financing Non-Current Assets

Because non-current assets are long-term investments, they are typically financed using long-term sources of capital. This alignment of asset life with liability life reduces liquidity risk. For example, a company might use a 10-year commercial mortgage to buy a warehouse, or issue equity to fund a major acquisition, rather than relying on a short-term revolving line of credit.

## Excel and modeling notes

*   **Depreciation Schedules:** In a financial model, non-current assets (specifically PP&E) are modeled using a depreciation schedule. You will need to forecast new CapEx additions, calculate the depreciation on both existing and new assets based on their useful lives, and subtract the depreciation to arrive at the ending PP&E balance.
*   **Impairment Checks:** Be mindful that goodwill and other intangibles are subject to impairment. While difficult to forecast, a historical pattern of impairment charges might warrant adjusting future earnings models to account for this non-cash expense.

## Interview-ready answer

"Non-current assets are a company's long-term investments that are expected to provide economic benefit for more than one year. They include tangible items like Property, Plant, and Equipment (PP&E), as well as intangibles like goodwill and patents. Because they provide value over multiple years, their costs are capitalized on the balance sheet and gradually expensed through depreciation or amortization, matching the expense to the revenue the asset helps generate."

## Pitfalls and gotchas

*   **Assuming all CapEx is PP&E:** While PP&E is the most common form of capital expenditure, purchasing intangible assets or making long-term investments can also consume cash flows in similar ways.
*   **Forgetting Land doesn't depreciate:** A common mistake in modeling or accounting exercises is applying a depreciation rate to the total value of real estate, failing to separate the building (which depreciates) from the land (which does not).
*   **Confusing Book Value with Market Value:** The value of a non-current asset on the balance sheet (its book value) is its historical cost minus accumulated depreciation. This rarely equals what the asset could actually be sold for in the open market today.
