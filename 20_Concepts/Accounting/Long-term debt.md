# Long-term debt

> **TL;DR.** Long-term debt refers to loans or other financial obligations that a company does not have to pay off within the next 12 months.

## When you'd use this

You encounter long-term debt when analyzing a company's balance sheet to understand its capital structure, solvency, and leverage. It is a critical component when calculating metrics like the Debt-to-Equity ratio or assessing how a company funds major investments like property, plant, and equipment (PP&E) or acquisitions.

## The 30-second version

Long-term debt (LTD) represents a company's long-term financial commitments with maturities exceeding one year. On the balance sheet, it is classified under non-current liabilities. Common examples include bank loans, mortgages payable, bonds, and debentures. Companies use long-term debt to create financial leverage—borrowing money to finance the purchase of assets or expansion, which is often cheaper than issuing equity and doesn't dilute ownership.

## The full explanation

Long-term debt is an essential component of a company's capital structure, providing a source of financing for growth and expansion. However, taking on excessive long-term debt can increase a company's financial risk and limit its flexibility.

The time to maturity for long-term debt can range from anywhere between 12 months to 30+ years. Long-term debt may be consolidated into a single line-item on the balance sheet or broken out into separate items, depending on the company's accounting policies.

### Types of Long Term Debt
Long term debt is a catch-all phrase that includes various types of loans:
- **Bank Debt:** Loans issued by a bank or financial institution that are not tradable or transferable.
- **Mortgages:** Loans backed by a specific piece of real estate, like land and buildings.
- **Bonds:** Publicly tradable securities issued by a corporation with a maturity longer than a year.
- **Debentures:** Loans that are not backed by a specific asset and therefore rank lower than other types of debt in priority for repayment.
- **Capital lease obligations:** Lease agreements that transfer ownership of an asset to the lessee at the end of the lease term.

### The Current Portion of Long-Term Debt
When all or a portion of the long-term debt becomes due within a year's time, that value moves from the non-current liabilities section to the current liabilities section of the balance sheet. This reclassification is typically called the "current portion of long-term debt."

### Use of Leverage
When companies take on debt, they create financial leverage. This increases both the risk and the expected return on the company's equity. Owners and managers often use leverage to finance asset purchases because it is cheaper than equity and does not dilute their ownership percentage. Financial analysts evaluate a company's leverage using ratios like Debt/Equity, Debt/Capital, Debt/Assets, and Interest Coverage Ratio.

## Excel and modeling notes

When modeling long-term debt, such as a bank loan that equally amortizes over a period of time, you must split the debt into non-current and current portions on the projected balance sheets. For example, if a company takes out a $500,000 bank loan that amortizes equally over 5 years (so $100,000 is due each year):
- In Year 1, $400,000 is recorded as long-term debt (non-current liabilities) and $100,000 is recorded under the current portion of long-term debt (current liabilities).
- In Year 2, the $100,000 current portion from Year 1 is paid off, and another $100,000 of long-term debt moves down to become the new current portion.
- This process repeats until the loan is fully paid off.

## Interview-ready answer

Long-term debt represents a company's financial obligations that are due in more than one year. It's found under non-current liabilities on the balance sheet and includes items like bonds, mortgages, and bank loans. Any portion of this debt that becomes due within the next 12 months is reclassified to current liabilities as the "current portion of long-term debt."

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Failing to move the portion of long-term debt that becomes due within the next 12 months to current liabilities (current portion of long-term debt).
- Assuming all long-term debt is the same; it's a catch-all category that includes various instruments with different terms, collateral (like mortgages vs. debentures), and seniorities.
