# Accounts payable and DPO

> **TL;DR.** Accounts payable represents short-term debts a company owes to its creditors and suppliers for goods or services received, and Days Payable Outstanding (DPO) measures the average number of days the company takes to pay these bills.

## When you'd use this

You would use accounts payable and DPO metrics when analyzing a company's working capital, liquidity, and operational efficiency. Managing accounts payable effectively is critical for maximizing a business's chance of survival by ensuring cash flow is optimized without damaging relationships with key suppliers.

## The 30-second version

Accounts payable are recorded as a current liability on a company's balance sheet, representing short-term debts owed to third parties for items like raw materials, products, services, and transportation. Essentially, these are unpaid company expenditures.

Days Payable Outstanding (DPO) and Accounts Payable Turnover are two primary metrics used to assess how efficiently a company manages these debts. A higher DPO indicates that the company is taking longer to pay its bills, which allows it to hold onto its cash longer (good for short-term liquidity) but might strain supplier relationships. Conversely, a higher accounts payable turnover ratio means the company pays its suppliers quickly.

## The full explanation

Accounts payable (often abbreviated as AP) is a crucial component of a company's working capital. It falls under the liabilities side of the balance sheet—the opposite of assets—and represents what your company owes other parties. When a business purchases goods or services on credit, the transaction creates an account payable.

To understand how effectively a company is managing these obligations, analysts look at specific metrics:

**1. Accounts Payable Turnover:** This ratio measures how quickly a company pays its suppliers. It provides insight into the company's short-term liquidity and its payment practices. A high turnover ratio suggests that the company pays off its accounts payable relatively quickly, while a low ratio may indicate that the company is struggling to meet its obligations or is intentionally taking advantage of favorable credit terms.

**2. Accounts Payable Days (DPO):** Also known as Days Payable Outstanding, this metric translates the turnover ratio into an average number of days it takes the company to pay its suppliers. DPO is a fundamental piece of the cash conversion cycle. Companies often try to maximize their DPO (delaying payment) to keep cash on hand for other investments or operational needs. However, stretching payables too far can result in late fees, missed early-payment discounts, and damaged relationships with essential vendors.

Understanding these financial terms can help maintain a sharp focus on costs and simplify cash flow measures. It also helps determine an organization's operational efficiency and short-term financial health.

## Excel and modeling notes

When forecasting a 3-statement model, accounts payable is typically projected based on the historical DPO and projected Cost of Goods Sold (COGS). The formula is rearranged to forecast the AP balance:
$\text{Accounts Payable} = (\text{DPO} / 365) \times \text{COGS}$

Ensuring accurate DPO projections is vital because changes in accounts payable directly impact the cash flow from operations on the cash flow statement.

## Interview-ready answer

**Question:** What are accounts payable and how do we measure how efficiently a company manages them?

**Answer:** Accounts payable are short-term liabilities representing money a company owes to its suppliers and creditors for goods or services purchased on credit. To measure how efficiently a company manages its payables, we look at Accounts Payable Turnover, which is COGS divided by Average Accounts Payable, and Days Payable Outstanding (DPO), which is (Accounts Payable / COGS) multiplied by 365. DPO tells us the average number of days a company takes to pay its bills. While delaying payment (higher DPO) can improve short-term cash flow and working capital, doing so excessively risks harming supplier relationships.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- **Supplier relationships:** Focusing too heavily on increasing DPO to boost short-term liquidity can backfire if suppliers respond by increasing prices, changing credit terms, or withholding essential goods.
- **Accrual vs. Cash Accounting:** Accounts payable exist because of accrual basis accounting, where expenses are recorded when incurred, not when paid. It's important to remember that AP represents projected cash outflows.
