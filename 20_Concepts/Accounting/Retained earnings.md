# Retained earnings

> **TL;DR.** Retained earnings represent the portion of a company's net income that is not distributed to shareholders as dividends, but rather kept to be reinvested into the business or saved for future use.

## When you'd use this

You would use the concept of retained earnings when analyzing a company's balance sheet to understand its financial health, examining how it funds its growth (internal financing vs. external debt/equity), or evaluating its dividend policy over time.

## The 30-second version

Retained earnings, also referred to as accumulated earnings or profits, are the cumulative net profits a company has generated since its inception minus any dividends it has paid out to shareholders. They sit on the balance sheet under shareholders' equity and represent the assets financed by the company's own profits rather than by outside investors or debt. Positive and growing retained earnings generally indicate a healthy, profitable company, while negative retained earnings (an accumulated deficit) indicate that historical losses have outweighed past profits.

## The full explanation

Retained earnings act as a financial reservoir for a business. When a company earns a profit (net income), the management and board of directors must decide what to do with that cash. They generally have two choices: distribute it to shareholders as a dividend (a reward for their investment) or keep it within the company (retained earnings).

### Why Retained Earnings Matter

1. **Financial Stability:** A consistently growing retained earnings balance indicates stable profitability over time. It provides a cushion to absorb losses during economic downturns without needing to immediately seek external funding.
2. **Fueling Growth:** These accumulated profits are a primary source of internal financing. Companies can use them to invest in research and development, fund marketing campaigns, pay down debt, acquire other businesses, or purchase capital assets.
3. **Dividend Capacity:** Companies with strong retained earnings have a larger surplus from which to pay future dividends, which can make the company more attractive to income-focused investors.

### Factors Influencing the Balance

Several key factors impact whether retained earnings increase or decrease:
- **Profitability:** Higher net income directly increases retained earnings.
- **Dividend Policy:** A company that pays aggressive dividends will accumulate retained earnings slower than a company with a conservative dividend policy.
- **Capital Expenditures:** Heavy investment in the business often requires utilizing these retained funds.

If a company consistently operates at a loss, those losses will reduce retained earnings. If the accumulated losses exceed all previous retained earnings, the account balance becomes negative, signaling potential financial trouble or a need for restructuring.

## Interview-ready answer

Retained earnings are the cumulative net profits a company has kept since inception, rather than paying out as dividends. They are recorded under shareholders' equity on the balance sheet and are used to reinvest in the business, pay down debt, or act as a financial buffer.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Assuming retained earnings equal cash. Retained earnings are an accounting figure representing equity, not a bank account balance; the actual cash generated by those earnings may have already been spent on inventory, equipment, or debt reduction.
- Interpreting low retained earnings as purely negative. While it could mean the company is unprofitable, it could also just mean the company pays out very large dividends or is relatively new.
