# Shareholders equity

> **TL;DR.** Shareholders' equity represents the remaining value of a company's assets after deducting all its liabilities.

## When you'd use this

You use shareholders' equity to evaluate a company's financial health, measure its net worth or book value, and assess the proportion of a business financed by the owners' investments or accumulated profits compared to debt. It serves as a key metric for investors and analysts to determine stability and valuation.

## The 30-second version

Shareholders' equity, also known as owner's equity or book value, is the residual interest in a company's assets after paying off all liabilities. It is the core component of the accounting equation ($Assets = Liabilities + Equity$). If a company were liquidated, this equity would be the amount returned to shareholders. It is primarily composed of funds contributed by owners (share capital or additional paid-in capital) and retained earnings, which are the profits reinvested into the company rather than distributed as dividends.

## The full explanation

Shareholders' equity forms the foundation of the balance sheet alongside assets and liabilities. The fundamental accounting equation states that a company's total assets must equal the sum of its total liabilities and shareholders' equity. Therefore, equity can be derived by subtracting total liabilities from total assets.

### Key Components of Equity

While simple in concept, shareholders' equity can be broken down into several parts on the balance sheet:

- **Outstanding shares / Share capital:** The total amount of stock sold to investors that has not been repurchased.
- **Additional paid-in capital (APIC):** The amount shareholders paid for stock above its stated par value.
- **Retained earnings:** The portion of net income that a company retains for reinvestment in the business instead of distributing to shareholders as dividends. For mature companies, this often forms the largest part of equity.
- **Treasury stock:** Shares that the company has bought back from investors. This is a contra-equity account, meaning it reduces the total shareholders' equity.

### Positive vs Negative Equity

Equity is an indicator of financial stability:
- **Positive equity** means the company has enough assets to cover all its liabilities.
- **Negative equity** means liabilities exceed assets, which can be a red flag, indicating the company is a high-risk investment or potentially insolvent.

## Formula

The core formula derived from the accounting equation is:

$$ \text{Shareholders' Equity} = \text{Total Assets} - \text{Total Liabilities} $$

Another common metric derived from this is the Shareholder Equity Ratio, which shows what proportion of assets are financed by equity:

$$ \text{Shareholder Equity Ratio} = \frac{\text{Shareholders' Equity}}{\text{Total Assets}} $$

## Worked example

Imagine ABC Limited owns total assets amounting to $1,000,000. Its total liabilities amount to $250,000.

Using the primary formula:
$$ \text{Shareholders' Equity} = \$1,000,000 - \$250,000 = \$750,000 $$

If we want to calculate the shareholder equity ratio for ABC Limited:
$$ \text{Shareholder Equity Ratio} = \frac{\$750,000}{\$1,000,000} = 0.75 \text{ or } 75\% $$

This indicates that 75% of the company's assets are financed by shareholder equity, making it a conservatively financed company with low leverage risk.

## Excel and modeling notes

When building financial models in Excel, ensure that the balance sheet balances by checking that Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity. The Retained Earnings line item is the linchpin that connects the income statement to the balance sheet. In your model, ending retained earnings must equal beginning retained earnings plus net income (linked from the bottom of the income statement), minus any dividends issued.

## Interview-ready answer

Shareholders' equity is the net value of a company, calculated as total assets minus total liabilities. It represents the residual amount that would be left for shareholders if all assets were liquidated to pay off all debts. The main components are share capital, retained earnings, and additional paid-in capital, less any treasury stock.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Confusing market value with book value: Shareholders' equity on the balance sheet represents the "book value" of the company based on historical accounting, which often differs significantly from its current market value or market capitalization.
- Missing the linkage: In a 3-statement model, failing to link net income from the income statement correctly to the retained earnings account in shareholders' equity will cause the balance sheet to go out of balance.
