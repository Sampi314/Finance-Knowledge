# Cash from investing

> **TL;DR.** Cash from investing represents the cash generated or spent from buying and selling long-term assets, such as property, equipment, or other businesses.

## When you'd use this

You use cash from investing to evaluate how much a company is spending on its future growth (like buying new equipment) versus how much cash it is generating from selling off its long-term assets. This metric is a key indicator of a company's investment strategy and its potential for long-term expansion.

## The 30-second version

Cash from investing activities is the second section of the cash flow statement. It tracks the cash inflows and outflows related to a company's long-term investments. Cash outflows occur when a company buys fixed assets, like property, plant, and equipment (often referred to as capital expenditures or CapEx), or when it acquires other businesses. Cash inflows occur when a company sells these assets. A negative number in this section is common for growing companies, as it indicates they are heavily investing in their future operations.

## The full explanation

The cash flow statement is divided into three sections: operations, investing, and financing. Cash from investing activities focuses exclusively on the cash exchanges related to long-term assets and investments.

When a company wants to grow, it often needs to purchase new assets. For a startup or a manufacturing firm, this might mean buying computers, machinery, or real estate. The cash spent on these purchases is recorded here as an outflow (a negative number). These purchases are commonly known as capital expenditures (CapEx). Additionally, if a company acquires another business or purchases securities (like stocks or bonds of another company), these are also recorded as investing cash outflows.

Conversely, if a company decides to sell a piece of equipment, a building, or its investments in other companies, the cash received from that sale is recorded as an inflow (a positive number).

Analysts pay close attention to this section. While positive cash flow from operations is generally the goal, a negative cash flow from investing is not necessarily a bad sign. In fact, for many healthy, growing businesses, this number is consistently negative because they are reinvesting their cash into new equipment and technology to drive future growth. However, if a company is consistently selling off its assets to generate cash (resulting in positive investing cash flow but negative operating cash flow), it could be a warning sign of financial distress.

## Formula

The formula for calculating net cash flow from investing activities is:

$\text{Cash Inflows from Investing Activities} - \text{Cash Outflows from Investing Activities} = \text{Net Cash Flow from Investing Activities}$

## Excel and modeling notes

When modeling a cash flow statement, the investing section is typically driven by the capital expenditure (CapEx) schedule and the depreciation schedule. The purchase of new assets links directly to the additions line in the PP&E (Property, Plant, and Equipment) roll-forward on the balance sheet.

## Interview-ready answer

Cash from investing activities shows the cash a company spends on or receives from long-term assets and investments. The most common item here is capital expenditures, or CapEx, which represents cash spent on buying property, plant, and equipment. A negative investing cash flow is typical for growing companies as they reinvest in their business.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- **Confusing it with operating expenses:** Buying a new factory is an investing cash outflow (CapEx), whereas paying the electricity bill for that factory is an operating expense that affects net income and operating cash flow.
- **Viewing negative cash flow as purely bad:** A negative investing cash flow often signals that a company is investing in its future growth, which is generally viewed positively by investors if the core operations are healthy.
