# Cash from financing

> **TL;DR.** Cash from financing tracks the net change in cash related to a company raising capital, repurchasing shares, paying dividends, and repaying debt.

## When you'd use this

You look at cash from financing when trying to understand how a business is funding its operations and growth, or how it is returning capital to its investors. It tells you whether a company is issuing new equity, taking on new debt, paying down existing debt, or distributing dividends to shareholders.

## The 30-second version

Cash flow from financing activities (CFF) is the third and final section of a company's cash flow statement. While the operating section tracks cash from day-to-day business and the investing section tracks cash used for long-term assets, the financing section focuses exclusively on the movement of cash between the company and its owners or creditors. By netting the inflows from raising capital against the outflows from debt repayments, share buybacks, and dividends, it provides a clear picture of the company's external financing activities during a given period.

## The full explanation

### The role of financing activities

The cash flow statement reconciles net income to the actual cash a company generated or used over a specific period. The financing section represents the net cash impact of activities related to debt and equity. It is crucial for understanding changes in a company's capital structure.

### Cash Inflows

When a company raises capital, it brings cash into the business:
- **Debt Issuances:** Borrowing funds from lenders. This represents a cash inflow since the company receives the loan proceeds.
- **Equity Issuances:** Raising external financing by issuing shares to investors in exchange for capital.

### Cash Outflows

When a company returns capital to investors or repays its obligations, cash leaves the business:
- **Debt Repayment:** Repaying the principal amount of a loan or bond. This is an outflow.
- **Share Buybacks:** Repurchasing previously issued shares from the open market, reducing the number of shares outstanding.
- **Dividends:** Making cash payments to equity shareholders as a form of return on their investment.

### Netting it all together

The net cash flow from financing activities is the sum of these inflows and outflows. A positive number means the company brought in more capital than it paid out, which typically happens when a company is growing rapidly and needs external funding. A negative number means the company is paying down debt or returning capital to shareholders, which is more common for mature, cash-generating businesses.

## Excel and modeling notes

When forecasting cash flow from financing activities in a financial model, it requires projecting future inflows and outflows related to debt and equity.

- **Debt:** To forecast debt, assess existing loan agreements, repayment schedules, and potential new borrowing based on the company’s capital needs and leverage strategy. Consider interest rates, maturity dates, and any planned refinancing.
- **Equity:** For equity, review past trends in stock issuance or buybacks and management’s plans for future actions, like raising capital or repurchasing shares.
- **Dividends:** Forecast dividends based on the company's historical dividend policy, payout ratio, and future earnings expectations.

## Interview-ready answer

Cash flow from financing is the third section of the cash flow statement, tracking the cash a company generates from raising capital and the cash it spends on paying down debt, buying back shares, and issuing dividends. A common misconception is that interest expense belongs here, but it actually appears on the income statement and affects the starting point (net income) in the operating section.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- **Interest expense is excluded:** A common mistake is to think interest expense belongs in the financing section because it's related to debt. However, interest expense is an operating expense already accounted for on the income statement, meaning its cash impact is captured within the starting net income figure in the cash from operations section.
- **Principal vs. Interest:** Only the principal repayment of debt is recorded as an outflow in the financing section.
