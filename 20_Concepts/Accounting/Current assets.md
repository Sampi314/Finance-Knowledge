# Current assets

> **TL;DR.** Current assets are the resources on a company's balance sheet that are expected to be converted into cash, sold, or consumed within one year or one operating cycle.

## When you'd use this

You look at a company's current assets when evaluating its short-term financial health and liquidity. Current assets are key variables used by investors and creditors to determine whether a business can meet its immediate financial obligations, fund its day-to-day operations, and survive near-term economic shocks without having to raise more capital or default on its debt.

## The 30-second version

Current assets are the most liquid assets a business owns, listed at the very top of the assets section on the balance sheet. They are resources that the company plans to use up or turn into cash within the next twelve months. The total value of current assets gives a snapshot of a company's short-term resources. Common examples include cash in the bank, money owed by customers (accounts receivable), goods sitting in a warehouse waiting to be sold (inventory), and expenses paid in advance like rent.

## The full explanation

Current assets represent a company's short-term economic resources. They are placed at the top of the balance sheet because assets are generally ordered from most liquid (easiest to convert to cash) to least liquid. They contrast directly with non-current assets (or long-term assets), like property, plant, and equipment, which provide economic value over a period longer than a year and are not easily converted to cash.

The primary role of current assets is to cover current liabilities—the debts and obligations a company owes within the same one-year timeframe. The balance between these two categories determines a company's working capital.

### Order of liquidity

Current assets are typically listed on the balance sheet in the following order of liquidity:

1. **Cash and Cash Equivalents:** The most liquid of all assets, including physical currency, checking accounts, and highly liquid short-term investments like Treasury bills that mature in three months or less.
2. **Marketable Securities:** Short-term investments that can quickly be sold for cash, such as certificates of deposit or money market funds.
3. **Accounts Receivable (A/R):** Money owed to the company by customers who purchased goods or services on credit.
4. **Inventory:** Raw materials, work-in-progress, and finished goods that the company intends to sell. Inventory is often considered the least liquid of the major current assets because selling it requires finding a buyer.
5. **Prepaid Expenses:** Payments made in advance for goods or services to be received in the near future, such as prepaid insurance or rent. While they don't convert to cash, they free up cash that would otherwise be spent.

### Liquidity analysis

Investors and analysts use current assets to calculate various liquidity ratios. These ratios help assess if a company is "liquid" (has enough short-term assets to cover short-term debts) or "illiquid" (at risk of defaulting on near-term obligations).

## Excel and modeling notes

When forecasting a balance sheet in financial models, current assets (specifically accounts receivable, inventory, and prepaid expenses) are usually projected based on their historical relationship to the income statement. For example, Accounts Receivable is often forecasted using a "Days Sales Outstanding" (DSO) metric tied to projected revenue, while Inventory might be forecasted using "Days Inventory Outstanding" (DIO) tied to Cost of Goods Sold (COGS). Cash is typically the "plug" or output of the cash flow statement, not forecasted directly on the balance sheet.

## Interview-ready answer

Current assets are short-term resources on the balance sheet expected to be converted into cash or used up within one year. They are listed in order of liquidity, starting with cash and cash equivalents, followed by marketable securities, accounts receivable, inventory, and prepaid expenses. We look at current assets primarily to evaluate a company's liquidity and working capital position, ensuring it has enough near-term resources to cover its current liabilities.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- **Overstating true liquidity with inventory:** Including inventory in liquidity calculations (like the Current Ratio) can sometimes paint an overly rosy picture. If a company is struggling to sell its products or if the inventory becomes obsolete, it cannot easily be converted to cash to pay off debts. This is why analysts also look at the Quick Ratio, which excludes inventory.
- **Deteriorating quality of receivables:** A growing accounts receivable balance might look like a strong current asset position, but if those receivables represent customers who are failing to pay, they may eventually need to be written off as a bad debt expense, destroying value.
