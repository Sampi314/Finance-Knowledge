# Deferred revenue

> **TL;DR.** Deferred revenue is cash received by a company before it has fulfilled its obligation to deliver the associated goods or services, representing a liability until earned.

## When you'd use this

You encounter deferred revenue when analyzing businesses that require upfront payments for services rendered over time. This is especially common in software-as-a-service (SaaS) companies with annual subscriptions, insurance companies, or any business where customers pay a deposit before work begins. Understanding it is critical for adjusting cash flows and modeling revenue accurately.

## The 30-second version

Deferred revenue, also called unearned revenue or customer deposits, represents money collected from a customer *before* the product or service is delivered. Under accrual accounting and standards like ASC 606, you cannot recognize this cash as revenue yet because you haven't "earned" it by satisfying the performance obligation. Therefore, it sits on the balance sheet as a liability because the company owes the customer either the service or a refund. As the service is delivered over time, the liability is reduced, and the revenue is formally recognized on the income statement.

## The full explanation

Deferred revenue is a cornerstone of accrual accounting, which matches revenues to the period in which they are actually earned (the revenue recognition principle, codified under ASC 606), rather than when the cash changes hands.

When a company receives an upfront payment, it records an increase in Cash (an asset) and an equal increase in Deferred Revenue (a liability). It is a liability because the company has an unfulfilled obligation—it "owes" the customer the service. If the company fails to deliver, it must refund the cash.

### The Flow Through the Three Statements
1. **Initial Collection:** Cash increases on the balance sheet. To balance, Deferred Revenue (liability) increases by the same amount. The income statement is unaffected because nothing has been delivered yet.
2. **Recognition Over Time:** As the company delivers the service (e.g., each month of a 12-month software subscription), it recognizes a portion of the revenue on the income statement. This increases Net Income. On the balance sheet, Retained Earnings (Equity) increases due to the Net Income, and Deferred Revenue decreases by the same amount to balance.
3. **Cash Flow Impact:** On the Cash Flow Statement, an increase in Deferred Revenue is an inflow of cash (operating activities), representing cash collected but not yet recorded as revenue. When revenue is finally recognized, the decrease in Deferred Revenue is a cash outflow adjustment (since the cash was already collected previously), netting out the non-cash revenue added to Net Income at the top.

### SaaS Context
In a SaaS or subscription context, the mechanics can slightly differ because customers might sign a contract and be invoiced before paying cash. When the invoice is sent, Accounts Receivable (Asset) increases, and Deferred Revenue (Liability) increases. Revenue is still recognized over the delivery period, drawing down Deferred Revenue, while cash collection simply converts the Accounts Receivable into Cash.

## Worked example

Imagine a SaaS company sells a 12-month software subscription for $1,200, paid entirely upfront on January 1st.

1. **January 1 (Cash Collection):**
   - Cash increases by $1,200.
   - Deferred Revenue increases by $1,200.
   - Income Statement: $0 impact.

2. **End of January (Service Delivery):**
   - The company recognizes 1/12th of the contract: $100 in Revenue on the Income Statement.
   - Deferred Revenue decreases by $100 (leaving a balance of $1,100).
   - Assuming no expenses/taxes, Net Income increases by $100, flowing into Retained Earnings to keep the Balance Sheet balanced.

This process repeats monthly until Deferred Revenue reaches $0 and the full $1,200 is recognized as revenue.

## Excel and modeling notes

When modeling, Deferred Revenue is part of the working capital schedule. An increase in the deferred revenue balance year-over-year represents a positive source of cash (you collected more cash than you recognized as revenue). A decrease is a use of cash. It's crucial to project deferred revenue based on bookings/billings rather than just historical revenue growth, especially for fast-growing subscription companies.

## Interview-ready answer

Deferred revenue is a liability created when a company collects cash from a customer before delivering the product or service. It's a liability because it represents an unfulfilled obligation. As the company delivers the service over time, it recognizes the revenue on the income statement and reduces the deferred revenue balance on the balance sheet.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- **Confusing it with Accrued Revenue:** They are opposites. Accrued revenue is when you have delivered the service but haven't yet been paid (an asset). Deferred revenue is when you have been paid but haven't yet delivered the service (a liability).
- **Ignoring Delivery Costs:** While recognizing revenue, companies also incur associated delivery expenses (Cost of Services or COGS). These must be matched and expensed in the same period the revenue is recognized to get accurate Net Income margins.
