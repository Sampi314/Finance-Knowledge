# The accounting equation

> **TL;DR.** The fundamental principle that states a company's total assets must equal the sum of its total liabilities and shareholders' equity at all times.

## When you'd use this

You will use the accounting equation whenever you are building, reading, or analyzing a balance sheet. It is the foundational concept behind double-entry bookkeeping and ensures that all financial transactions are balanced. It helps analysts and executives understand how a company's assets are financed—whether through debt (liabilities) or internal sources (equity).

## The 30-second version

The accounting equation shows that everything a company owns (its assets) must be paid for by either borrowing money (liabilities) or from the owners' investments and retained earnings (shareholders' equity). Because of this, the balance sheet must always remain in balance. In the double-entry accounting system, every transaction has at least one debit and one corresponding credit.

## The full explanation

The accounting equation sets the foundation for the balance sheet and the double-entry bookkeeping system. It consists of three main components:

- **Assets:** Resources with positive economic value owned by a company, which can either be sold for money (like inventory) or expected to produce future monetary benefits (like machinery and patents).
- **Liabilities:** A company's future financial obligations to third parties, such as loans, accounts payable, and accrued expenses.
- **Shareholders' Equity:** The net assets or remaining value after subtracting liabilities. It represents internal funding sources, including contributed capital and retained earnings.

### The Expanded Accounting Equation

The basic equation can be expanded to provide more detail, particularly regarding the components of shareholders' equity. The expanded accounting equation breaks equity into Share Capital and Retained Earnings, which can be further detailed as Contributed Capital (CC) + Beginning Retained Earnings (BRE) + Revenue (R) - Expenses (E) - Dividends (D).

This rearranged formula can be especially useful during scenarios like bankruptcy, where it helps illustrate how short-term and long-term debts will be paid first from liquidated assets before any remaining funds compensate shareholders.

## Excel and modeling notes

When building a three-statement financial model, the accounting equation serves as the ultimate "check" that your balance sheet balances. Financial modelers often include a check row at the bottom of the balance sheet subtracting (Liabilities + Equity) from Total Assets. If the result is not exactly zero, there is an error in the model.

## Interview-ready answer

The accounting equation is Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity. It is the core principle behind the balance sheet and double-entry bookkeeping, meaning that every asset a company acquires must be financed either through borrowing (liabilities) or by owner contributions and accumulated profits (equity).

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Failing to recognize that both sides of a transaction must have an equal and offsetting debit and credit entry, which keeps the equation in balance.
- Mistaking revenue and expenses as standalone accounts rather than components that flow into retained earnings (and therefore equity) in the expanded equation.
