# EBITDA

> **TL;DR.** EBITDA is a proxy for a company's core, recurring operating cash flow, calculated before the impacts of capital structure, taxes, and non-cash expenses like depreciation and amortization.

## When you'd use this

You use EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization) to evaluate a company's core operational profitability and to quickly compare the operating performance of different companies, regardless of how they are financed, the tax environments they operate in, or their past capital expenditure decisions. It is also a fundamental metric for evaluating debt capacity in LBOs and serves as the denominator in the widely used EV/EBITDA valuation multiple.

## The 30-second version

EBITDA measures the cash generated by a company's day-to-day operations. By taking operating income (EBIT) and adding back non-cash expenses like depreciation and amortization, EBITDA isolates the profit directly attributable to core business activities. This metric ignores interest (how the company is funded), taxes (which vary by jurisdiction), and depreciation/amortization (accounting allocations for past capital investments). While it is a convenient and universally known proxy for operating cash flow, it is not a true measure of free cash flow because it ignores actual capital expenditures and working capital needs.

## The full explanation

### The origin and purpose of EBITDA

EBITDA was popularized in the 1970s and 1980s by John Malone, the billionaire investor and operator of Tele-Communications Inc. (TCI). During the nascent days of the cable television industry, Malone recognized that scale was critical: the larger the company, the lower the per-subscriber programming costs.

However, pursuing scale required massive capital investments and acquisitions, which generated high depreciation expenses and interest costs, dragging down Net Income and EPS (Earnings Per Share). Malone introduced EBITDA to shift Wall Street's focus away from EPS and toward the pure cash-generating ability of the business before the impact of interest, taxes, and depreciation. This allowed him to minimize reported earnings (and thus taxes) while using the robust pretax cash flow to fund internal growth and acquisitions.

### Why add back Depreciation and Amortization?

To calculate EBITDA, you start with Operating Income (EBIT) and add back Depreciation and Amortization (D&A). You add back D&A because it represents the allocation of spending on long-term assets (like factories, buildings, or IP) from *previous* periods. The company has already spent the cash on these items in the past; therefore, the D&A expense on the current income statement does not represent an actual cash outflow in the current period.

### Adjusted EBITDA

In practice, EBITDA is often further modified to create "Adjusted EBITDA." This involves adding back other non-recurring or unusual expenses (like restructuring charges, legal settlements, or one-time write-offs) to present a "normalized" view of ongoing profitability.

However, adjusted EBITDA is highly susceptible to manipulation. Management teams and investment bankers sometimes push the envelope to make a company appear more profitable than it is. A famous example is WeWork, which introduced "Community Adjusted EBITDA" ahead of a proposed bond offering, stripping out basic operating expenses like marketing, general and administrative, and development costs to turn a $933 million net loss into a positive $233 million metric. When adjustments go beyond genuine one-time items and start ignoring recurring cash expenses, the metric becomes meaningless.

## Excel and modeling notes

When calculating EBITDA from a company's financial statements, **always source the Depreciation & Amortization figure from the Cash Flow Statement**, not the Income Statement. Many companies embed a portion of their D&A within other line items (such as Cost of Goods Sold or SG&A) on the Income Statement. Pulling it from the Cash Flow Statement ensures you capture the full, unconsolidated non-cash charge.

Additionally, do not add back all non-cash expenses. For instance, Stock-Based Compensation is a non-cash expense, but it is a recurring cost of doing business that dilutes the share count. Standard practice is to leave it as an expense rather than adding it back to EBITDA, although aggressive adjusted EBITDA calculations sometimes try to add it back.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- **It is not Free Cash Flow:** The biggest pitfall is treating EBITDA as a true measure of cash flow. EBITDA ignores Capital Expenditures (CapEx) and changes in Net Working Capital, which are real, required cash outflows for most businesses. "You can't eat EBITDA."
- **Sourcing D&A incorrectly:** Pulling D&A from the Income Statement instead of the Cash Flow Statement often leads to understating total D&A and thus understating EBITDA.
- **Over-adjusting:** Accepting management's "Adjusted EBITDA" at face value without scrutinizing the add-backs. Add-backs should only be for genuinely non-recurring items, not for recurring operating costs disguised as one-time events.
