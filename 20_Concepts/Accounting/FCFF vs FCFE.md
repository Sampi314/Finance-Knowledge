# FCFF vs FCFE

> **TL;DR.** Free Cash Flow to the Firm (FCFF) is cash available to all investors and uses WACC as its discount rate to find Enterprise Value, whereas Free Cash Flow to Equity (FCFE) is cash left over for shareholders after debt obligations are met and uses the Cost of Equity to find Equity Value.

## When you'd use this

You use these concepts in discounted cash flow (DCF) modeling and valuation to determine a company's intrinsic value. You must match the type of cash flow to its appropriate discount rate and valuation multiple. Use FCFF when you want to value the entire core operations of a business (Enterprise Value) regardless of its capital structure. Use FCFE (or dividend discount models) when you want to value just the equity stake, which is preferred if the firm has highly stable dividends or if an investor owns a controlling interest in the firm.

## The 30-second version

Free cash flow represents the cash a business generates after accounting for operating expenses, taxes, and capital reinvestment (like CapEx and working capital changes).

**Free Cash Flow to the Firm (FCFF)**, also known as Unlevered Free Cash Flow, represents the cash available to all capital providers—both debt and equity. Because it ignores interest expense and debt repayments, it shows how much cash the core business operations generate on their own.

**Free Cash Flow to Equity (FCFE)**, or Levered Free Cash Flow, is the cash remaining strictly for equity shareholders after all debt obligations (interest and net debt repayments) have been satisfied.

## The full explanation

To understand FCFF and FCFE, it helps to view the company as a machine funded by two groups of people: debt providers (lenders) and equity providers (shareholders).

### Free Cash Flow to the Firm (FCFF)
FCFF measures the cash flow generated by the firm's operating assets before any financing costs are paid. It is the cash flow available to *all* suppliers of capital once the firm pays operating expenses and investing expenditures (like property, plant, and equipment, intangible assets, and working capital) needed to sustain its existence.

Crucially, FCFF excludes the impact of interest expense and net debt issuance or repayments. Therefore, when discounting FCFF in a DCF model, you must use a discount rate that reflects the risk of all capital providers: the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC). Discounting FCFF yields the **Enterprise Value** of the firm.

### Free Cash Flow to Equity (FCFE)
FCFE measures the cash flow available strictly to the firm's stockholders. It accounts for all the operating and investing expenditures used in FCFF, but goes a step further by integrating net cash outflows to bondholders. This means interest payments are subtracted, and net debt issuance (or repayment) is factored in.

Because FCFE represents cash belonging only to equity holders, it must be discounted using the **Cost of Equity**. Discounting FCFE yields the **Equity Value** of the firm.

### Valuation Multiples
The distinction between FCFF and FCFE is critical when calculating valuation multiples. A core rule of valuation is matching the numerator and denominator:
- If the cash flow metric in the denominator *does not* include interest expense (e.g., FCFF, EBIT, EBITDA), the numerator must be **Enterprise Value**.
- If the cash flow metric in the denominator *does* include interest expense (e.g., FCFE, Net Income), the numerator must be **Equity Value**.

## Formula

Calculating FCFF (Unlevered Free Cash Flow):
$\text{FCFF} = \text{EBIT} \times (1 - \text{Tax Rate}) + \text{Non-Cash Expenses} - \text{Changes in Net Working Capital} - \text{CapEx}$

Calculating FCFE (Levered Free Cash Flow):
$\text{FCFE} = \text{Net Income} + \text{Non-Cash Charges} - \text{Changes in Net Working Capital} - \text{CapEx} + \text{Net Debt Issued (Repaid)}$

Alternatively, starting from Cash Flow from Operations (CFO):
$\text{FCFF} = \text{CFO} + \text{Tax Adjusted Interest Expense} - \text{CapEx}$
$\text{FCFE} = \text{CFO} - \text{CapEx} + \text{Net Debt Issued (Repaid)}$

## Worked example

Imagine a company with the following financials:
- Net Income: $100
- Interest Expense: $20
- Tax Rate: 20%
- Depreciation (Non-Cash): $30
- CapEx: $40
- Increase in Net Working Capital: $10
- Net Debt Issued: $15

To find FCFE:
$\text{FCFE} = 100 \text{ (Net Income)} + 30 \text{ (Depreciation)} - 10 \text{ (NWC)} - 40 \text{ (CapEx)} + 15 \text{ (Net Debt Issued)} = \$95$

To find FCFF, first we need EBIT. Since Net Income = (EBIT - Interest) * (1 - Tax Rate):
$100 = (\text{EBIT} - 20) \times (1 - 0.20)$
$100 = (\text{EBIT} - 20) \times 0.8$
$125 = \text{EBIT} - 20$
$\text{EBIT} = 145$

Now, calculate FCFF:
$\text{FCFF} = 145 \times (1 - 0.20) + 30 - 10 - 40 = 116 + 30 - 10 - 40 = \$96$

*(Alternatively, FCFF = FCFE + Interest*(1-t) - Net Debt Issued = 95 + 20*(0.8) - 15 = 95 + 16 - 15 = $96)*

## Excel and modeling notes

When building a three-statement model to reconcile FCFF with FCFE, you must ensure the underlying assumptions (like tax rates and reinvestment requirements) remain consistent across both methods. The FCFF method arrives at Enterprise Value, from which you subtract net debt to find Equity Value. The FCFE method calculates Equity Value directly. In a perfectly forecasted model with a static capital structure, both approaches will yield the exact same Equity Value.

## Interview-ready answer

"FCFF is Unlevered Free Cash Flow, representing the cash generated by the firm's core operations available to all capital providers. We discount it using WACC to find Enterprise Value. FCFE is Levered Free Cash Flow, which represents the cash available only to equity shareholders after debt obligations are met. We discount FCFE using the Cost of Equity to find Equity Value."

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- **Mismatched discount rates:** A common modeling error is discounting FCFF using the Cost of Equity, or discounting FCFE using WACC. This will result in an incorrect valuation.
- **Mismatched multiples:** Using Enterprise Value divided by a levered metric (like Net Income or FCFE) is analytically inconsistent. Always pair Enterprise Value with unlevered metrics, and Equity Value with levered metrics.
- **Ignoring changing capital structures:** Reconciling FCFF and FCFE correctly requires assuming the firm's capital structure remains static. If the debt-to-equity ratio changes over time, the marginal cost of capital changes, making the reconciliation more complex.
