# Net income

> **TL;DR.** Net income is a company's total profit after deducting all expenses, including cost of goods sold, operating expenses, interest, and taxes, from its total revenue over a specific period.

## When you'd use this

You use net income to evaluate a company's overall profitability and financial health. It is fundamentally important for investors, analysts, and management to determine whether a business is generating enough profit to sustain operations, pay dividends, or reinvest for growth. It is also a key input for calculating earnings per share (EPS) and profitability ratios like net profit margin.

## The 30-second version

Net income, often called the "bottom line" because it sits at the very end of the income statement, represents what is left of a company's revenue after all costs, expenses, and taxes have been paid. While it indicates accounting profit, it does not necessarily equal the cash generated during the period. This is due to the accrual accounting principle, which recognizes non-cash expenses like depreciation and records revenue when earned, not just when cash changes hands.

## The full explanation

Net income is a crucial indicator of a company's financial performance over a given fiscal period. To arrive at net income, you start with total revenue (the "top line") and systematically deduct various categories of expenses:

1. **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS):** The direct costs attributable to the production of the goods sold by a company. Subtracting COGS from revenue yields the gross profit.
2. **Operating Expenses:** These are the indirect costs associated with running the business, such as Selling, General, and Administrative (SG&A) expenses.
3. **Depreciation and Amortization (D&A):** Non-cash expenses representing the gradual wear and tear or loss of value of tangible and intangible assets over time.
4. **Interest Expense:** The cost of borrowing money.
5. **Taxes:** The income taxes owed to the government based on the pre-tax income.

### Accrual Accounting vs. Cash Flows
It is essential to understand that net income does not represent a company's cash flow. Because of accrual accounting, revenues are recorded when earned and expenses are recorded when incurred, regardless of when cash is actually exchanged. Furthermore, net income deducts non-cash expenses (like depreciation) and excludes capital expenditures (like buying a factory) and financing activities (like issuing debt), which do affect cash but are not fully expensed in the current period's income statement.

### Adjusted Net Income
Companies and analysts often use a non-GAAP metric called "Adjusted Net Income" to provide a clearer picture of normalized, ongoing operations. This involves taking the reported net income and adding back non-cash items and adjusting for one-time, non-recurring events (like the sale of an asset or a restructuring charge). The tax implications of these adjustments must also be considered to arrive at the final adjusted figure.

## Formula

The basic formula for Net Income is:
Net Income = Total Revenue - Total Expenses

More specifically, stepping through the income statement:
Net Income = Revenue - COGS - Operating Expenses - Depreciation & Amortization - Interest Expense - Taxes

## Worked example

Suppose a company reports the following financial data for the year:
- Revenue: $1,000,000
- Cost of Goods Sold (COGS): $400,000
- Operating Expenses (SG&A): $200,000
- Depreciation & Amortization: $50,000
- Interest Expense: $20,000
- Tax Rate: 20%

First, calculate Gross Profit:
$1,000,000 - $400,000 = $600,000

Next, calculate Operating Income (EBIT):
$600,000 - $200,000 - $50,000 = $350,000

Then, calculate Earnings Before Tax (EBT):
$350,000 - $20,000 = $330,000

Calculate Taxes (20% of EBT):
$330,000 * 0.20 = $66,000

Finally, calculate Net Income:
$330,000 - $66,000 = $264,000

## Excel and modeling notes

When building a financial model, net income is typically the last line item projected on the income statement. It serves as a crucial link to the other financial statements. Net income flows into the top line of the Cash Flow Statement (where non-cash items and changes in working capital are then adjusted to find cash flow from operations). It also flows into the Retained Earnings account on the Balance Sheet, ensuring that the balance sheet balances.

## Interview-ready answer

Net income is the bottom line of the income statement, representing a company's total accounting profit after deducting all expenses, interest, and taxes from revenue. It's important to note that net income is an accounting metric based on accrual accounting and does not equal cash flow, as it includes non-cash expenses like depreciation and excludes cash outlays for capital expenditures.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Confusing net income with cash flow. Net income includes non-cash items and accruals, meaning a highly profitable company on paper can still run out of cash.
- Taking adjusted net income at face value. Management teams often use adjusted net income (a non-GAAP metric) to exclude certain expenses, which can sometimes be used to obscure true, underlying poor performance. Always scrutinize the add-backs.
- Forgetting that net income is a flow metric measured over a period of time, unlike balance sheet items which are point-in-time snapshots.
