# Current liabilities

> **TL;DR.** Current liabilities are a company's short-term financial obligations that are due within one year or within a normal operating cycle.

## When you'd use this

You look at current liabilities to assess a company's short-term liquidity, working capital, and overall ability to meet upcoming financial obligations without raising additional capital or selling off long-term assets.

## The 30-second version

Current liabilities represent the debt or obligations a business expects to pay off within the next 12 months using its current assets. They appear on the balance sheet and commonly include accounts payable, short-term debt, accrued expenses, and income taxes payable. Because these obligations must be settled soon, monitoring them is critical to ensure a business doesn't run out of cash.

## The full explanation

A liability is a present obligation that requires a future outflow of resources. The primary way accountants classify liabilities is by their due date, dividing them into current and non-current (long-term) liabilities.

**Current liabilities** are obligations due within a year or one normal operating cycle. They are a critical measure of a company's short-term liquidity. When a company incurs a current liability, it essentially means it must generate or have enough cash (or other current assets) in the near term to settle that debt.

Some of the most common current liabilities include:

- **Accounts payable:** The largest current liability for many businesses, representing money owed to suppliers for goods or services purchased on credit.
- **Accrued expenses:** Expenses that have been incurred but not yet paid, such as employee wages or accrued interest.
- **Short-term debt / Notes payable:** Loans or other formal borrowings with a maturity of less than one year.
- **Current portion of long-term debt (CPLTD):** The specific segment of a long-term loan that is due within the next 12 months.
- **Income taxes payable:** Taxes owed to the government that must be paid within the year.
- **Deferred (unearned) revenue:** Money received in advance for goods or services that have not yet been delivered.

### Impact on liquidity

Analysts and management teams closely monitor current liabilities because a failure to manage them can lead to a liquidity crisis or even bankruptcy. Current liabilities are a key component of several liquidity ratios, most notably the current ratio, which compares a company's current assets to its current liabilities.

## Formula

The concept itself doesn't have a specific formula, but it is a critical input in the Current Ratio:

$$ \text{Current Ratio} = \frac{\text{Current Assets}}{\text{Current Liabilities}} $$

## Excel and modeling notes

When modeling a balance sheet, current liabilities are usually forecasted as a function of the income statement. For example:
- **Accounts payable** is often forecasted using Days Payable Outstanding (DPO) and tied to Cost of Goods Sold (COGS).
- **Accrued expenses** might be modeled as a percentage of SG&A or operating expenses.
- **Deferred revenue** might be linked to projected sales growth.

Short-term debt and the current portion of long-term debt are typically driven by a separate debt schedule.

## Interview-ready answer

Current liabilities are short-term financial obligations due within one year or one operating cycle. Common examples include accounts payable, accrued expenses, and short-term debt. We care about them because they measure a company's short-term liquidity and dictate the amount of cash required to keep operations running smoothly.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- **Confusing Notes Payable with Accounts Payable:** Accounts payable relates to goods and services purchased on credit from vendors, while short-term notes payable relates to actual funds or cash borrowed from financial institutions.
- **Ignoring Deferred Revenue:** While it is a liability, deferred revenue doesn't usually require a cash outflow to settle; rather, it requires the delivery of a product or service. This distinction is important when assessing true cash obligations.
- **Overlooking CPLTD:** Don't forget to break out the current portion of long-term debt from the total long-term debt balance when calculating liquidity ratios.
