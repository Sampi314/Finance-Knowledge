# Operating expenses

> **TL;DR.** Operating expenses (OpEx) are the ongoing, day-to-day costs incurred by a business in its normal operations, such as rent, salaries, and utilities, which are not directly tied to the production of goods.

## When you'd use this

You encounter operating expenses whenever you analyze a company's income statement. They are critical for evaluating how efficiently a company is managing its day-to-day operations and determining its operating profit or EBIT. OpEx is a fundamental piece when forecasting the financial performance of a business, distinguishing between the direct costs of producing goods (COGS) and the broader costs of running the enterprise.

## The 30-second version

Operating expenses encompass all the costs required to run a business on a daily basis that do not fall under Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) or Capital Expenditures (CapEx). Common examples include employee wages, office rent, insurance, marketing, and utilities. Unlike CapEx, which represents investments in long-term assets that are depreciated over time, OpEx is expensed immediately on the income statement in the period the cost is incurred. While COGS scales directly with the number of products sold, many operating expenses remain relatively fixed regardless of sales volume.

## The full explanation

Operating expenses, commonly referred to as OpEx, are the indirect costs associated with the core operations of a business. These are necessary to keep the company running ("keeping the lights on") but are not directly linked to the manufacturing or procurement of the specific goods or services a company sells.

### The nature of Operating Expenses

Operating expenses represent immediate cash outflows or short-term obligations that directly impact a company's net income for the current period. According to the matching principle of accounting, expenses should be recognized in the same period as the revenue they help generate. However, because OpEx typically provides benefits only in the short term (usually within a year), they are expensed entirely on the income statement as they occur.

Examples of typical operating expenses include:
- **Selling, General, and Administrative (SG&A):** This broad category includes sales commissions, marketing and advertising costs, legal fees, accounting fees, and executive salaries.
- **Rent and Utilities:** The cost of leasing office space, electricity, water, and internet services.
- **Research & Development (R&D):** Costs associated with developing new products or services. While some argue R&D provides long-term benefits, it is generally treated as an operating expense due to the uncertainty of future economic benefits.

### Differentiating OpEx from COGS

It's crucial to distinguish operating expenses from Cost of Goods Sold (COGS). COGS includes the direct costs attributable to the production of goods sold by a company, such as direct materials and direct labor. COGS tells you how profitable a specific product is (Gross Profit), whereas OpEx tells you how efficiently the overall business is being managed. Operating income (EBIT) is calculated by subtracting operating expenses from gross profit.

### Differentiating OpEx from CapEx

Operating expenses must also be differentiated from Capital Expenditures (CapEx). CapEx involves spending money to acquire, upgrade, or maintain physical assets like property, plants, or equipment (PP&E). These are long-term investments where the benefit extends beyond the current year. Because of their long-term nature, CapEx is capitalized on the balance sheet and slowly expensed over time through depreciation. OpEx, by contrast, hits the income statement immediately.

## Excel and modeling notes

When forecasting an income statement in a financial model, operating expenses are often projected differently from COGS. While COGS is typically forecasted as a percentage of revenue, some operating expenses (like rent or corporate salaries) may be forecasted as fixed amounts that grow at an assumed inflation rate, while others (like marketing) might be tied to revenue growth.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- **Confusing CapEx and OpEx:** A common mistake is expensing a large, long-term asset purchase entirely in the current year as an operating expense, which improperly suppresses net income for that period.
- **Misclassifying direct labor:** Sometimes labor directly involved in manufacturing is incorrectly classified under operating expenses (SG&A) rather than COGS, which skews the gross margin calculation.
