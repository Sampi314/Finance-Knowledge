# Revenue recognition

> **TL;DR.** Revenue recognition is an accounting principle determining that revenue should be recorded when it is earned and realized or realizable, not necessarily when cash is received.

## When you'd use this

You need to understand revenue recognition whenever you analyze a company's income statement, project future revenue growth, or evaluate financial performance under accrual accounting standards. It is fundamental when dealing with companies that receive payments before delivering a product, or that deliver a product before receiving payment.

## The 30-second version

Revenue recognition dictates the specific conditions for recording revenue on the financial statements. The core idea is that revenue is only recognized when the company has fulfilled its obligation to deliver goods or services to the customer. This ensures that the financial performance shown in a given period accurately reflects the actual business activity during that period. If cash is received before the service is provided, it's recorded as deferred revenue; if the service is provided before cash is received, it creates accounts receivable.

## The full explanation

Revenue recognition is a cornerstone of accrual accounting. It separates the timing of cash flows from the reporting of financial performance. According to the revenue recognition principle, revenue must be recognized in the period it was "earned."

### Accrual Accounting Context
The importance of this principle stems from the desire to accurately portray a company's financial state. When paired with the matching principle—which states that expenses should be recognized in the same period as the corresponding revenue they helped generate—revenue recognition ensures that the income statement reflects true operational performance.

### Deferrals and Accruals
Because cash payment often does not perfectly align with the delivery of goods and services, accountants use adjusting entries:
- **Deferrals**: Occur when cash is received *before* the revenue is earned. For example, a customer pays for an annual software subscription upfront. The company cannot immediately recognize all that cash as revenue. Instead, it records it as "deferred revenue" (a liability). The revenue is recognized over the subscription period as the service is delivered.
- **Accruals**: Occur when revenue is earned *before* the cash payment is received. The company has delivered the good but hasn't been paid yet, creating "accounts receivable."

### Standards
Revenue recognition standards can be complex and vary by industry. Companies generally must adhere to standard frameworks, such as GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles) or IFRS (International Financial Reporting Standards), when recognizing revenue to ensure consistency and transparency for investors.

## Excel and modeling notes

When building a three-statement model or a revenue build, you must account for the difference between cash collected and revenue recognized. For a SaaS company, revenue is typically modeled as the beginning deferred revenue balance plus new bookings (cash collected), minus ending deferred revenue.

## Interview-ready answer

Revenue recognition is an accrual accounting principle stating that revenue is recorded when a product or service is actually delivered and earned, regardless of when the cash payment changes hands. For example, if a company collects payment for a one-year subscription upfront, it cannot recognize all that revenue immediately; it must defer it and recognize it proportionally each month as the service is provided.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Equating revenue with cash flow. Remember that a spike in cash collection doesn't mean an immediate spike in revenue if the cash is for future services.
- Misunderstanding the matching principle. Expenses should be matched to the period when the associated revenue is recognized, not necessarily when the expense was paid.
