# Double-entry bookkeeping

> **TL;DR.** Double-entry bookkeeping is an accounting system where every transaction requires at least two offsetting entries—a debit and a credit—ensuring that the accounting equation always remains in balance.

## When you'd use this

You would use double-entry bookkeeping when setting up the accounting system for any business larger than a sole proprietorship. It is the standardized method used globally to accurately record financial transactions and build the three major financial statements (Income Statement, Cash Flow Statement, and Balance Sheet).

## The 30-second version

Unlike a single-entry system that simply lists revenues and expenses, double-entry accounting records the dual effect of every transaction. If a company receives cash (an asset), it must also record where that cash came from—perhaps a loan (a liability) or sales revenue (equity). By requiring that total debits equal total credits for every single event, double-entry bookkeeping inherently creates a system of checks and balances that minimizes administrative errors and ensures the fundamental accounting equation (Assets = Liabilities + Equity) always holds true.

## The full explanation

### The Mechanics of Debits and Credits
In a double-entry system, accounts are increased or decreased using debits and credits. A debit does not universally mean "increase," and a credit does not universally mean "decrease." Instead, their impact depends on the type of account being adjusted:

- **Asset Accounts** (e.g., cash, inventory, equipment): Increased by debits, decreased by credits.
- **Expense Accounts** (e.g., rent, payroll): Increased by debits, decreased by credits.
- **Liability Accounts** (e.g., accounts payable, debt): Increased by credits, decreased by debits.
- **Equity Accounts** (e.g., common stock, retained earnings): Increased by credits, decreased by debits.
- **Revenue Accounts** (e.g., sales): Increased by credits, decreased by debits.

### The DEAD Rule
A common mnemonic to remember the normal balances of accounts is the **DEAD** rule. You should generally **D**ebit **E**xpenses, **A**ssets, and **D**ividends to increase them. Knowing this rule makes it easier to deduce that liabilities, equity, and revenues operate in the exact opposite manner (they are increased by credits).

### Why It Prevents Errors
As a business grows, tracking transactions becomes more complex. While double-entry accounting doesn't eliminate every possible mistake, it makes it mathematically impossible for the balance sheet to go out of balance without the accountant knowing immediately. Because every transaction must have equal debits and credits, an error in one entry will cause the books to fail to balance, alerting the company to trace and fix the issue before finalizing financial statements.

## Interview-ready answer

Double-entry bookkeeping is the standard accounting system where every financial transaction dictates two offsetting entries: a debit and a credit. This dual-sided approach ensures that total debits always equal total credits, acting as an internal check to maintain the integrity of the core accounting equation: Assets = Liabilities + Equity.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- **Confusing debits and credits with bank terminology:** In everyday banking, a "credit" to your account feels like an increase, while a "debit" card purchase reduces your money. In corporate accounting, a debit increases an asset account like cash, while a credit decreases it.
- **Assuming it prevents all fraud:** Double-entry ensures mathematical balance, but it cannot prevent intentional fraud if false but balancing entries (e.g., creating fake revenue and fake receivables) are recorded.
