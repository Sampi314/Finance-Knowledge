# The income statement

> **TL;DR.** The income statement shows a company’s revenue, expenses, and taxes over a specific period, ultimately revealing its profit or loss.

## When you'd use this

You use the income statement to understand the profitability of a business over time (such as a year, quarter, or month). It is the starting point for financial modeling, as projecting future revenues and expenses allows you to forecast net income and eventually free cash flow. In valuation and operations, you rely on the income statement to assess operating efficiency, calculate margins, and gauge whether the core business model is sustainable.

## The 30-second version

The income statement is one of the three core financial statements. It records a company’s financial performance over a discrete period. It starts with revenue at the top, deducts various costs and expenses (such as the cost of goods sold, operating expenses, depreciation, and interest), and ends with net income (or net loss) at the bottom.

It relies on the accrual basis of accounting, meaning it records revenue when it is recognized under the matching principle, rather than when cash actually changes hands. This matching ensures that expenses are aligned with the revenues they help generate.

## The full explanation

### Purpose and Structure
The primary purpose of the income statement is to demonstrate a business's ability to generate profit. It is structured to flow from the top line (revenue) down to the bottom line (net income), capturing different types of expenses along the way.

By categorizing expenses into different sections—such as Cost of Goods Sold (COGS), operating expenses (like Selling, General & Administrative, or SG&A), interest, and taxes—the income statement allows analysts to observe multiple layers of profitability. For example, Gross Profit focuses purely on the direct costs of delivering a product, while Operating Income (or EBIT) shows profitability from the core operations before the impact of capital structure (debt/interest) and taxes.

### Accounting Principles Used
The income statement is deeply tied to key accounting rules, most notably:
- **The Matching Principle:** This mandates that expenses must be recorded in the same period as the revenues they helped produce.
- **Accrual vs. Cash Accounting:** Revenues and expenses are recorded when they are incurred or recognized, not necessarily when cash flows in or out of the bank. Because of this, the net income figure usually does not equal the actual cash generated by the business.

### Connection to Other Statements
The income statement is inextricably linked to the balance sheet and the cash flow statement.
- **Balance Sheet Link:** The net income generated on the income statement flows into the balance sheet by increasing Retained Earnings (a component of Shareholders' Equity), assuming no dividends are paid out.
- **Cash Flow Statement Link:** Net income is the starting point for the cash flow statement (under the indirect method), which then adjusts for non-cash expenses (like depreciation) and changes in working capital to arrive at true cash from operations.

## Excel and modeling notes

When forecasting the income statement in a financial model:
- **Projecting Line Items:** You typically forecast revenue growth as a percentage, and then project many expenses (like COGS and SG&A) as a percentage of sales to maintain historical margins.
- **Flat Assumptions:** A common starting point is holding the most recent historical margins flat over the projected period, unless specific insights (or equity research analyst estimates) justify tweaking them.
- **Depreciation vs. Amortization:** Depreciation is usually projected as a percentage of sales or property, plant, and equipment (PP&E), because recurring capital expenditures generate fixed assets over time. However, the amortization of intangible assets is typically projected in discrete dollar amounts equal to the most recent period, as intangibles often arise from non-recurring events like M&A.
- **Color Coding:** Standard practice is to color inputs and assumptions (like sales margins) in blue font.

## Interview-ready answer

The income statement shows a company's revenues, expenses, and taxes over a specific period, ending with net income. It relies on accrual accounting and the matching principle to pair expenses with the revenue they generate, rather than tracking actual cash flow.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Confusing net income with cash flow. Due to non-cash expenses like depreciation and timing differences in working capital, net income does not represent cash generated.
- Projecting amortization as a percentage of sales in a model. Since intangibles usually come from one-off events (like acquisitions), amortization should generally be hardcoded in discrete amounts rather than scaled with revenue.
