# Time value of money

> **TL;DR.** The time value of money (TVM) states that a dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow because it can be invested to earn a return.

## When you'd use this

You use this concept whenever you need to compare cash flows occurring at different points in time. It is the core foundation for discounted cash flow (DCF) models, bond valuation, net present value calculations, and assessing investment opportunities.

## The 30-second version

Money has earning capacity. If you have $100 today, you can invest it and have more than $100 in a year. Therefore, $100 received in a year is worth less than $100 received right now. To properly value assets or investments that pay out over time, we use the time value of money to "discount" those future cash flows back to their present value using a discount rate.

## The full explanation

The time value of money means that money today is worth more than money tomorrow because you could invest it today and earn a return on it in the future.

It is true that inflation makes uninvested money less valuable over time, but the real reason that future cash flow is less valuable than cash flow today is because of the opportunity cost of capital. By receiving money in the future, you give up the opportunity to invest that money today and earn a return on comparable investments with similar risk profiles.

In valuation models, you have to discount future cash flows to their Present Value based on your opportunity cost. The discount rate represents the expected return on investment given its risk profile. A higher discount rate implies there is more risk, making a company's future cash flows less valuable today.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Confusing inflation with TVM. Many people misunderstand the concept and think that future cash flow is less valuable just because of inflation. While inflation decreases purchasing power, TVM exists primarily because of the opportunity cost of not being able to invest the capital today.
- Directly comparing future cash flows with present cash flows without discounting the future cash flows first.
