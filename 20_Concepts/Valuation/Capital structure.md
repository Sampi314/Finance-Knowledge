# Capital structure

> **TL;DR.** Capital structure refers to the mixture of debt and equity a company uses to fund its operations and finance its assets.

## When you'd use this

You examine a company's capital structure whenever you are analyzing its risk profile, determining its weighted average cost of capital (WACC) for a DCF valuation, or modeling an LBO or M&A transaction. The capital structure impacts how a company handles economic downturns and influences both its enterprise and equity valuation.

## The 30-second version

A company needs capital to operate and grow, and it can raise this capital by taking on debt (borrowing from lenders) or issuing equity (selling ownership stakes). The proportion of debt versus equity forms the company's capital structure. Debt is typically cheaper due to tax-deductible interest and its senior claim on assets, but it comes with mandatory repayment obligations and increases the risk of financial distress. Equity is more flexible with no mandatory payments, but it is more expensive and dilutes ownership.

## The full explanation

### The Core Trade-off

The fundamental decision in determining a capital structure is balancing the benefits of debt against its costs. Debt provides an "interest tax shield"—because interest payments are tax-deductible, the effective after-tax cost of debt is lower. Additionally, debt holders have seniority in a liquidation scenario, so they accept lower returns than equity investors who bear the residual risk.

However, debt introduces significant risks. It requires fixed, mandatory interest and principal payments regardless of the company's operating performance. It also often comes with restrictive covenants that can limit management's flexibility.

### Theoretical Frameworks

Three main theories attempt to explain how companies should structure their capital:

1. **Modigliani-Miller Theorem**: Originally proposed in 1958, this theorem argues that in a perfect market with no taxes or bankruptcy costs, capital structure is irrelevant to a firm's value. Later modified to include taxes, it suggested that a firm should be 100% debt-financed due to the tax shield. However, this ignores real-world distress costs.
2. **Trade-off Theory**: This framework suggests there is an optimal capital structure where the marginal benefit of the tax shield exactly offsets the marginal cost of expected financial distress.
3. **Pecking Order Theory**: Proposes that companies prefer internal financing (retained earnings) first. If external funds are needed, they will issue debt next, and issue equity only as a last resort, because issuing equity sends a negative signal that management believes the stock is overvalued.

### Industry Variations

Capital structure varies significantly across industries based on asset tangibility and cash flow stability. For example, utilities and real estate investment trusts (REITs) can sustain high leverage (high debt-to-equity ratios) because they possess tangible assets to serve as collateral and generate highly predictable cash flows. Conversely, technology and biotech firms typically operate with low leverage, or even negative net debt, as their primary assets are intangible and their cash flows can be volatile.

## Excel and modeling notes

When modeling a company's capital structure, particularly in three-statement models or LBOs, you will often build detailed debt and equity schedules. The debt schedule tracks the beginning balance, interest expense, mandatory amortization, optional prepayments, and ending balance for each tranche of debt. Because interest expense lowers net income and, consequently, retained earnings and cash, modeling debt often involves circular references (especially regarding revolving credit facilities), which need to be managed carefully with circularity breakers or iterative calculations.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- **Ignoring off-balance sheet liabilities**: When analyzing capital structure, analysts sometimes forget to include operating leases or unfunded pension obligations as debt equivalents, leading to an artificially low perception of leverage.
- **Assuming an optimal capital structure is static**: A company's ideal capital structure changes as it moves through its life cycle. A high-growth startup may require all-equity funding, while a mature cash cow can comfortably handle high debt levels.
