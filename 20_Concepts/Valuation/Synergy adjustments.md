# Synergy adjustments

> **TL;DR.** Synergy adjustments modify pro forma financial statements to reflect expected cost savings and revenue gains when combining two companies in an M&A transaction.

## When you'd use this

You apply synergy adjustments when evaluating potential mergers and acquisitions, building merger models, and projecting pro forma financial statements to see how a combined entity will perform. These adjustments directly impact the theoretical value of the target firm and are necessary to justify the acquisition premium.

## The 30-second version

Synergies are the extra value created when two companies combine, making the combined whole more valuable than the sum of its parts. Synergy adjustments are the specific modifications made to the pro forma income statement, typically enhancing revenue (from cross-selling or pricing power) and reducing operating expenses (by eliminating overlapping roles, systems, or facilities). Because cost synergies are more controllable by management, analysts view them as far more reliable than revenue synergies and treat them differently in valuations.

## The full explanation

### Integrating Synergies into Pro Forma Statements

When an acquiring firm buys a target, the combined financials are shown through **pro forma financial statements**. One of the main adjustments made to these theoretical statements are synergy adjustments. Synergies usually come in two forms:

1. **Cost synergies:** Savings that occur when duplicate functions are removed. Examples include laying off redundant management and staff, closing excess office space, streamlining supply chains, or combining IT systems.
2. **Revenue synergies:** Additional sales generated by the combination. This could be from cross-selling the target's products to the acquirer's customer base, accessing new geographical markets, or raising prices due to reduced competition.

In a merger model, these expected synergies are phased in over several years (e.g., Year 1, Year 2) because integration is rarely immediate. The adjustments flow through the income statement, increasing EBITDA and net income, which ultimately impacts accretion/dilution analysis and free cash flow generation.

## Excel and modeling notes

When building a merger model in Excel:
- Model cost synergies at or near management projections, but heavily discount management's revenue synergy projections. (e.g. If management claims $500 million in revenue synergies, base cases might only model $150 to $200 million).
- Include the *cost to achieve* synergies (e.g., severance packages, system integration costs) as a one-time expense in the early periods of the pro forma model.
- Phase synergies in gradually (e.g., 25% realized in Year 1, 75% in Year 2, 100% in Year 3).

## Interview-ready answer

In a transaction, synergy adjustments are applied to the pro forma income statement to reflect the operational benefits of the combination. Cost synergies reflect savings from eliminating redundancies and are generally considered highly reliable since management has direct control over them. Revenue synergies reflect top-line growth from cross-selling or market expansion but are viewed more skeptically by analysts and often heavily discounted, as they rely on external customer behavior.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Applying revenue synergies at 100% of management's estimates, which is often overly optimistic and seen as naive in a banking context.
- Forgetting to deduct the one-time integration costs required to actually achieve the projected cost savings.
- Assuming synergies are realized on Day 1 rather than phasing them in over a realistic integration period (often 12-36 months).
