# Free cash flow projection

> **TL;DR.** Free cash flow projection is the process of forecasting a company's unlevered free cash flows over a given timeframe (typically 5 to 10 years) to determine the present value of its future cash-generating ability as part of a Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) model.

## When you'd use this

You use free cash flow projections primarily when conducting a Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) valuation. It is essential when estimating the intrinsic value of a company or an asset, as it focuses entirely on the cash generated from core operations available to all capital providers (both debt and equity holders) before any financing costs.

## The 30-second version

To value a business using a DCF model, you forecast its future free cash flows, typically starting with projected revenue, deriving Earnings Before Interest and Taxes (EBIT), and removing non-cash charges, capital expenditures, and changes in net working capital. These Unlevered Free Cash Flows (UFCF) represent the core operating cash available to all investors. These flows are usually projected over a 5 to 10-year period—long enough for the business to reach steady-state growth. After that, a terminal value is applied, and everything is discounted back to present value using the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC).

## The full explanation

### The foundation of DCF Valuation

A Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) model estimates a company's intrinsic value based entirely on its ability to generate cash flow in the future, rather than focusing on its current market perception or trading multiples. To do this, analysts must project the core operating cash flows that the business will yield.

These projected cash flows are called Unlevered Free Cash Flows (UFCF), meaning they are generated by a company's operations and are available to all capital providers—including both debt and equity holders. They are "unlevered" because they are calculated before the payment of interest on debt.

### The projection period

Projecting free cash flows usually takes place over a 5- or 10-year period. The goal is to forecast these cash flows until the business attains maturity and reaches a "steady-state" in both growth and profitability. Once the business is in this steady state, predicting its long-term future value becomes a simpler calculation known as the Terminal Value. Using an annual projection interval is typical, though some seasonal businesses might require quarterly projections for more accuracy.

### Calculating Unlevered Free Cash Flow (UFCF)

Forecasting the UFCF requires a few crucial steps derived from a company's projected income statement and balance sheet variables:

1. **Start with EBIT:** Begin with Earnings Before Interest and Taxes (EBIT), which represents GAAP-based operating profit.
2. **Account for Taxes:** Subtract projected taxes by multiplying EBIT by the anticipated tax rate (often based on the historical effective tax rate). This yields Unlevered Net Income or NOPAT (Net Operating Profit After Tax).
3. **Add Back Non-Cash Charges:** Add back Depreciation and Amortization (D&A), because these are accounting expenses that do not actually reduce the cash on hand.
4. **Account for Working Capital:** Factor in the annual changes in Net Working Capital (NWC). Increases in NWC are treated as cash outflows, while decreases are cash inflows.
5. **Subtract Capital Expenditures (CapEx):** Finally, subtract Capital Expenditures. These represent the cash investments the company must make in its fixed assets to sustain the forecasted growth.

## Excel and modeling notes

When building these projections in Excel, it's essential that the various drivers—such as EBIT growth, taxes, Net Working Capital, and CapEx—are linked to a 3-statement model (Income Statement, Balance Sheet, and Cash Flow Statement). Linking them correctly ensures that changing one assumption (like revenue growth) dynamically impacts all relevant components (such as working capital required) accurately.

## Interview-ready answer

In a DCF analysis, projecting unlevered free cash flows involves estimating the cash generated by core operations that's available to all capital providers. You forecast revenue and margins to get to EBIT, subtract taxes to get NOPAT, add back non-cash expenses like depreciation and amortization, and subtract both capital expenditures and the change in net working capital.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- **Overestimating Growth:** Assuming unrealistic revenue growth or operating margins that don't converge to a sustainable long-term rate by the end of the projection period.
- **Ignoring Reinvestment Needs:** Failing to accurately project enough Capital Expenditures to sustain the high revenue growth or EBIT growth assumptions.
- **Inconsistent Tax Rates:** Using a marginal tax rate instead of a historically grounded effective tax rate can skew the cash flow outputs.
- **Shorter vs. Longer Projection Intervals:** While shorter intervals (e.g., monthly) increase accuracy theoretically, they demand many more assumptions which can compound errors and offset the benefits.
