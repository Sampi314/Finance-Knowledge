# Net debt

> **TL;DR.** Net debt measures a company's total debt minus its cash and cash equivalents, representing the debt remaining if the company used all its liquid assets to pay it off.

## When you'd use this

You use net debt to gauge a company's true leverage and liquidity. It is used extensively in business valuation, specifically to transition between Enterprise Value and Equity Value, and by investors or creditors trying to evaluate financial stability.

## The 30-second version

Net debt is calculated by adding up all short-term and long-term financial obligations (like bank loans, commercial paper, notes payable, and bonds) and subtracting highly liquid assets (like cash, treasury bills, and marketable securities). If a company has negative net debt, it means it holds more cash than its debt, putting it in a strong, net-cash position. Conversely, an elevated net debt implies a company is highly leveraged.

## The full explanation

Net debt gives a more accurate picture of a company's debt obligations because it acknowledges that a company's available cash can theoretically be used to pay down its debts immediately.

### Calculating Net Debt
The calculation involves combining all interest-bearing debt:
- **Short-term debts**: Obligations due within 12 months.
- **Long-term debts**: Obligations due beyond 12 months.

From this total, you subtract **Cash and cash equivalents**, which represent the most liquid assets.

### Enterprise Value
Net debt plays a critical role in valuation. Enterprise Value (EV), a core valuation metric, is equal to a company's equity value plus its net debt. Netting out cash from debt assumes this cash can be used to settle creditor obligations, providing a true cost of acquiring the business.

### Net Debt-to-EBITDA Ratio
Credit rating agencies and investors frequently pair net debt with EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization) to determine a company's ability to pay off debt from its operating cash flows.
- A ratio below 3 is generally considered favorable or low, indicating strong capacity to manage and repay debt.
- A ratio greater than 4 is considered high, and over 6 is elevated, serving as red flags for potential financial distress.

## Worked example

Imagine Company A has the following financials:
- Short-term debt: $10,000 line of credit + $30,000 current portion of long-term debt = $40,000
- Long-term debt: $50,000 bank loan + $50,000 in bonds = $100,000
- Cash & equivalents: $15,000 cash + $10,000 Treasury bills + $15,000 marketable securities = $40,000

Net debt = ($40,000 + $100,000) - $40,000 = $100,000.

## Interview-ready answer

Net debt is a company's total short-term and long-term debt minus its cash and cash equivalents. It's an important liquidity metric used to find enterprise value and to assess leverage, often in combination with EBITDA to determine a company's debt-repayment capacity.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Relying exclusively on net debt: Net debt should be considered alongside other liquidity or leverage ratios (like the current ratio or quick ratio) rather than in isolation.
- Assuming all cash can be used to pay down debt: In reality, some cash may be restricted or needed for day-to-day operations and working capital, meaning not all of it is freely available to immediately settle creditor claims.
- Fluctuations: The metric is highly sensitive to short-term changes, such as asset sales or recent, debt-funded capital expenditures, which might temporarily skew the ratio.
