# Enterprise value

> **TL;DR.** Enterprise value is a measure of a company's total value, representing the theoretical takeover price of a company.

## When you'd use this

Enterprise value is used when comparing the valuations of companies with different capital structures, or evaluating acquisition targets since buyers assume both debt and equity obligations.

## The 30-second version

Enterprise Value (EV) or Total Enterprise Value (TEV) is a capital structure-neutral metric that measures the total value of a company's operations to all stakeholders, including equity and debt capital providers. It reflects what a buyer would pay to acquire the entire business.

## The full explanation

Enterprise Value is calculated by taking a company’s market capitalization (the value of its outstanding shares) and adding its total debt, preferred stock, and minority interest. From this sum, cash and cash equivalents are subtracted.

EV is important because simply using equity value can mislead the valuation of companies with high debt. EV accounts for both assets and liabilities, giving a full picture of company value.

## Interview-ready answer

Enterprise Value is the value of a company’s operations to all stakeholders, such as common equity shareholders, preferred stockholders, and lenders of debt capital. You calculate it by taking Equity Value, adding Net Debt, Preferred Stock, and Minority Interest.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Simply using equity value can mislead the valuation. EV accounts for liabilities, giving a more balanced view.
- Ignoring cash reserves. Always subtract cash when calculating EV.
