# DCF valuation

> **TL;DR.** Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) valuation is an intrinsic valuation method that estimates a company's fundamental value by projecting its future free cash flows and discounting them to present value using a risk-adjusted discount rate.

## When you'd use this

You use a DCF valuation when you need to determine the intrinsic or fundamental value of an asset or business based solely on its future cash flow generation capabilities, rather than relying on market-driven multiples. It is heavily utilized in investment banking for M&A advisory, equity research to determine target stock prices, and private equity to evaluate buyout targets. The method is best suited for mature companies with predictable cash flows.

## The 30-second version

A DCF analysis values a business based on the present value of all the future cash it will generate. You first project the company's unlevered free cash flows for a specific forecast period (usually 5 to 10 years). Because a company is expected to operate indefinitely, you then calculate a terminal value to capture all cash flows beyond the forecast period. Finally, you discount both the projected free cash flows and the terminal value back to today's dollars using the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC), which reflects the riskiness of the cash flows. The sum of these present values gives you the enterprise value of the firm.

## The full explanation

The DCF methodology represents a forward-looking, fundamental approach to valuation, relying entirely on the cash flows generated by the business. The process generally follows five main steps:

### 1. Project Unlevered Free Cash Flows (UFCF)
The first step is forecasting the company's unlevered free cash flows, typically for a 5- to 10-year period. UFCF represents the cash generated by the business that is available to all providers of capital (both debt and equity holders) before financing costs are considered.

### 2. Calculate the Terminal Value (TV)
Since projecting cash flows accurately beyond 5-10 years is very difficult, but businesses operate indefinitely, we calculate a Terminal Value. This represents the value of all cash flows beyond the initial forecast period.

### 3. Determine the Discount Rate
We need to discount future cash flows back to present value because money today is worth more than money in the future, and because future projections carry risk. In a standard unlevered DCF, the appropriate discount rate is the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC), which is the blended cost of debt and equity for the company.

### 4. Discount Cash Flows to Present Value
The projected UFCFs and the Terminal Value are then discounted back to today's present value using the calculated WACC. The sum of the present value of the explicit forecast period cash flows and the present value of the terminal value equals the Enterprise Value.

### 5. Calculate Equity Value
If the goal is to find the value of the company's equity (or stock price), you must bridge from Enterprise Value to Equity Value. This is done by subtracting net debt (total debt minus cash) and adjusting for other non-operating assets and liabilities.

## Formula

The core formula for calculating Enterprise Value in a DCF is:

$$Enterprise\ Value = \sum_{t=1}^{n} \frac{UFCF_t}{(1 + WACC)^t} + \frac{Terminal\ Value}{(1 + WACC)^n}$$

## Excel and modeling notes

When building a DCF model in Excel, it's a best practice to set up your assumptions for revenue growth, margins, and capital expenditures in a clear, distinct section so that you can easily run sensitivity analyses. You will typically build a full three-statement model that flows into your UFCF calculation to ensure that your working capital changes and depreciation are accurately captured.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- The DCF is highly sensitive to its assumptions. Small changes in the discount rate (WACC) or terminal growth rate can drastically change the resulting valuation.
- Terminal value often accounts for 60% to 80% or more of the total valuation, making the model heavily dependent on the assumptions used for the period beyond the explicit forecast.
- DCFs are difficult to use for early-stage startups or companies with highly unpredictable cash flows, as forecasting accurate free cash flows 5-10 years out is nearly impossible.
