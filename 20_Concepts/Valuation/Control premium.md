# Control premium

> **TL;DR.** The additional amount an acquirer pays above a target company's current market value to acquire a controlling ownership stake.

## When you'd use this

You use this concept primarily in M&A to explain why acquisition prices consistently exceed trading prices. It's used when valuing acquisitions of controlling stakes, analyzing precedent M&A transactions, negotiating purchase prices, and assessing the fairness of acquisition offers. It's the inverse of a minority discount, which is applied when valuing minority stakes.

## The 30-second version

A control premium is the extra amount paid to gain a controlling ownership stake in a company compared to its public market or standalone value. Control has value because the controlling shareholder can direct the company's future—allocating capital, setting strategy, executing operational changes, and capturing synergies that passive minority investors cannot. Typically ranging from 25% to 40% for public acquisitions, the premium reflects the economic value of controlling cash flows and unlocking synergistic value.

## The full explanation

A control premium captures the additional value a buyer is willing to pay to secure outright control over a business. While the public share price generally reflects a minority, pro-rata share of a company's value, outright ownership provides strategic and operational advantages that a minority position lacks.

### Why acquirers pay a premium

Acquirers pay premiums primarily for three interrelated reasons:

1.  **Controlling cash flows:** Majority owners can determine capital allocation (e.g., choosing to issue dividends, reinvest in the business, or pay down debt), set operational priorities, and direct strategic initiatives (e.g., entering new markets or changing product lines).
2.  **Access to synergies:** Control is typically required to realize both cost synergies (eliminating duplicate corporate functions or consolidating facilities) and revenue synergies (cross-selling products or accessing new distribution channels). The value of these synergies often justifies a substantial premium.
3.  **Strategic and competitive value:** Acquiring control may provide defensive value (preventing a competitor from acquiring the asset), access to proprietary technology, or immediate market entry. A premium may also account for the opportunity cost of *not* acquiring the target.

### Factors influencing the premium

According to historical data, typical control premiums fall between 25% and 35%, though they vary widely. Higher premiums (30-40% or more) are common for strategic buyers who can capture significant synergies, in competitive auctions, or for unique technology assets. Lower premiums (20-30%) are typical for financial buyers (like private equity firms) whose returns depend primarily on financial engineering rather than operating synergies.

## Worked example

If a company is trading at an unaffected price of $40 per share, and a strategic acquirer offers $52 per share to buy the company, the calculation is:

Control Premium = ($52 - $40) / $40 = 30%

The 30% premium represents the extra value the acquirer is paying to gain control, likely reflecting anticipated cost synergies and the strategic value of the target.

## Excel and modeling notes

When analyzing precedent transactions or preparing a fairness opinion, accurately determining the "unaffected price" is critical. You must identify the target's share price *before* any acquisition speculation or rumors inflated the stock. Sometimes analysts use a 30-day or 60-day volume-weighted average price (VWAP) to smooth out volatility and ensure the base price used to calculate the premium reflects the true standalone market value.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- **Using a rumor-inflated price:** Using the market price just prior to a deal announcement, if rumors have already driven the price up, will understate the true control premium paid.
- **Confusing premium with synergies:** While synergies justify a premium, the premium is simply the difference between the offer price and unaffected price. A buyer doesn't always pay the full value of the synergies to the seller.
- **Ignoring industry and buyer types:** Applying a generic 30% premium assumption to a mature, capital-intensive business or a financial sponsor deal will often result in an overvaluation, as average premiums vary heavily by sector and buyer type.
