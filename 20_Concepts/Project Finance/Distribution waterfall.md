# Distribution waterfall

> **TL;DR.** A distribution waterfall is a financial model that defines the sequential order in which profits are allocated between investors (Limited Partners) and managers (General Partners).

## When you'd use this

You use a distribution waterfall when structuring investments involving multiple parties, such as in private equity, real estate, and project finance. It provides a clear, contractual roadmap that converts episodic realizations into cash flows for Limited Partners (LPs) and General Partners (GPs), ensuring incentives align and specifying who gets paid, when, and how much.

## The 30-second version

A distribution waterfall outlines how and when profits are distributed among stakeholders in an investment partnership. It generally prioritizes returning invested capital to the Limited Partners (LPs) first, often alongside a preferred return. Once the LPs have achieved their hurdle rate, the General Partner (GP) may receive a catch-up payment. Finally, remaining profits are typically split according to a carried interest ratio, often 80% to LPs and 20% to GPs.

## The full explanation

Distribution waterfalls define the economic relationship between the equity participants involved in an investment. They ensure structured, fair profit allocation among investors, general partners, and other stakeholders, often serving as the contractual roadmap that converts book gains into cash in investors' pockets.

### Key Components

The core components of a distribution waterfall work together to allocate profits:

- **Return of capital**: Before performance payments are considered, LPs typically receive a dollar‑for‑dollar return of their contributed capital. This step ensures GPs focus on absolute value creation and investors recover their money before profits are shared.
- **Preferred Return**: The preferred return (hurdle rate) acts like a risk‑free floor for LPs. It ensures investors receive a fixed return (often 6% to 8% compound IRR) on their invested capital before the GPs receive profits.
- **Catch-Up Clause**: Once LPs have received their capital and preferred return, the GP may receive all or a majority of incremental proceeds in a catch-up tranche until their share of total profits equals the agreed carry percentage.
- **Carried Interest Allocations**: After the catch-up, any remaining profits are split according to the carried-interest ratio, classically 80% to LPs and 20% to the GP.

### Waterfall Structures

Waterfalls vary by complexity and structure. For example, a European-style (whole-of-fund) waterfall requires aggregate distributions to return all contributed capital plus the preferred return before GP carry crystallizes. In contrast, an American-style (deal-by-deal) waterfall applies the test to each realized investment, allowing carry to flow earlier.

## Excel and modeling notes

When modeling distribution waterfalls, you might see simpler structures like a straight 80/20 split or 50/50 split in real estate. More complex models incorporate a 20% catch-up after a preferred return, requiring careful multi-tier calculations. Real-estate waterfalls often favor the GP more than standard private equity deals.

## Interview-ready answer

A distribution waterfall is a financial mechanism that dictates the order in which investment returns are distributed between Limited Partners (LPs) and General Partners (GPs). It typically starts with the return of capital and a preferred return to LPs, followed by a GP catch-up, and finally splits remaining profits according to a carried interest ratio like 80/20.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Complex multi-tier calculations and changing profit variables can lead to manual errors and stakeholder disputes.
- Drafting the waterfall poorly can risk clawback disputes, over-distribution, and reputational damage.
