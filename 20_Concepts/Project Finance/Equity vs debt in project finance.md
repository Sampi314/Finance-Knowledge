# Equity vs debt in project finance

> **TL;DR.** Debt is a cheaper source of capital due to tax shields and seniority, but comes with mandatory payments and bankruptcy risk, while equity is more expensive but offers financial flexibility and no required repayments.

## When you'd use this

You apply these concepts when determining the optimal capital structure for a project or company, and when evaluating the risk and cost of different funding sources. This trade-off is foundational for calculating the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) and assessing investment feasibility.

## The 30-second version

Every company needs capital to operate, and the primary sources are debt and equity. Debt involves borrowing money that must be repaid with interest, and it is generally cheaper because interest is tax-deductible (the tax shield) and debt holders are paid first in a bankruptcy. Equity involves selling ownership stakes. While more expensive and dilutive, equity does not require mandatory payments and reduces the risk of financial distress. The mix of these two sources—the capital structure—affects a company's overall cost of capital and risk profile.

## The full explanation

### Why Debt Is Cheaper Than Equity

The cost of debt is almost always lower than the cost of equity for two main reasons. First, interest payments on debt are tax-deductible, reducing a company's taxable income and providing a "tax shield". Second, lenders have a senior claim in the event of bankruptcy or liquidation, meaning they take on less risk than equity holders and can thus accept a lower return.

### Why Equity Has Advantages

Despite its higher cost, equity provides significant benefits. Debt creates mandatory obligations (scheduled principal and interest repayments) regardless of business performance. It also typically comes with covenants that restrict management's flexibility. Most importantly, high levels of debt increase bankruptcy risk. Equity, being a residual claim without mandatory repayments, provides financial flexibility and avoids these distress costs.

### Theoretical Frameworks

- **Modigliani-Miller Theorem:** In perfect markets (no taxes, no bankruptcy costs), capital structure is irrelevant to firm value. However, once taxes are introduced, the tax shield of debt implies a firm should maximize leverage.
- **Trade-Off Theory:** Balances the tax benefits of debt against the costs of financial distress. The optimal capital structure is where the marginal benefit of additional debt equals the marginal cost of potential distress.
- **Pecking Order Theory:** Suggests companies follow a financing hierarchy based on information asymmetry: they prefer internal funds first, then debt, and equity as a last resort, avoiding equity issuance which may signal the shares are overvalued.

## Excel and modeling notes

In financial modeling, WACC combines the cost of debt (adjusted for the tax shield) and the cost of equity, weighted by their proportions in the capital structure. WACC is used to discount cash flows in a DCF analysis.

## Interview-ready answer

Debt is cheaper than equity because interest payments are tax-deductible and debt holders have a senior claim in bankruptcy. However, too much debt increases the risk and cost of financial distress, which is why companies balance debt and equity according to Trade-Off Theory, or prioritize funding sources based on the Pecking Order Theory.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Ignoring the indirect costs of financial distress, such as losing customers or suppliers, when evaluating the benefits of high leverage.
- Failing to account for the constraints imposed by debt covenants on management's flexibility.
- Assuming the cost of debt remains constant regardless of the leverage ratio; as debt increases, the risk of default rises, increasing the required interest rate.
