# Public-private partnerships

> **TL;DR.** A public-private partnership (PPP) is an arrangement where private capital steps in to finance, build, and operate essential public infrastructure projects, filling the funding gap left by constrained government budgets.

## When you'd use this

You would use a PPP structure when a government needs to develop or upgrade large-scale infrastructure—such as toll roads, airports, water systems, or student accommodations—but lacks the upfront public capital to do so alone. It allows the public sector to transfer significant financial, construction, and operational risks to private investors and specialized infrastructure funds.

## The 30-second version

Infrastructure projects provide essential services but often suffer from a financing gap because of their massive capital requirements. In a public-private partnership, a private sector entity partners with the government to fund, build, and often run the project over a long term (typically 20 years or more). The private entity earns a return either through direct user charges (like toll road fees) or through government availability payments. Because these assets are essential, they generally offer low-volatility, inflation-protected cash flows that are attractive to private equity infrastructure funds.

## The full explanation

Public-private partnerships represent a fundamental shift from traditional government procurement, introducing private sector efficiencies and capital into public works. The world faces a massive infrastructure financing gap, estimated at $15 trillion by 2040, prompting governments to develop established PPP frameworks.

In these arrangements, private companies—often backed by specialized infrastructure private equity funds—take on large projects. These investments are characterized by absolute returns and inflation protection, as they provide essential services and typically have revenue mechanisms linked to inflation. Furthermore, infrastructure assets generally have a low correlation to standard market cycles, providing resilience and diversification for investors' portfolios.

However, many socially important infrastructure projects might be economically beneficial but financially unviable for private investors seeking typical 12% to 15% returns, because user charges must remain affordable. To resolve this profitability gap, governments may offer Viability Gap Funding (VGF), which provides upfront capital grants to reduce the effective project cost. By covering up to 40% to 80% of costs (depending on the sector), VGF makes the remaining private investment viable while keeping the resulting public service affordable.

## Excel and modeling notes

When modeling PPPs, such as a toll road, revenue projections might rely on manual traffic counts and willingness-to-pay surveys. Cash flow patterns often exhibit a ramp-up period as traffic grows, followed by steady increases tied to inflation. Downside scenarios are typically modeled to evaluate if the project can repay its debt, sometimes using structures like a mini-perm (built-in refinancing) or evaluating government payments that make up deficits.

## Interview-ready answer

A public-private partnership (PPP) is a long-term contract between a private party and a government entity for providing a public asset or service. Private capital covers the massive upfront costs in exchange for long-term, low-volatility returns via user fees or availability payments, and the government may bridge any profitability shortfalls using Viability Gap Funding.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Traffic and usage risks can be severely misjudged; optimistic traffic estimates in toll road PPPs can lead to financial distress or failure.
- Relying on long-term fixed assumptions can be risky without modeling downside scenarios and built-in refinancing options (like mini-perms).
