# Power purchase agreements

> **TL;DR.** A power purchase agreement (PPA) is a long-term contract between an electricity generator and a buyer (off-taker) that secures a revenue stream and often includes penalties for delays or underperformance.

## When you'd use this

PPAs are central to renewable energy and infrastructure project finance. They secure predictable cash flows, which are essential for raising debt, and establish the obligations regarding capacity and energy delivery.

## The 30-second version

In a PPA, the project company (IPP) agrees to sell power to an off-taker (like a utility) at an agreed price. These contracts contain nuanced trade-offs, such as how to set targets, penalties, and incentives for availability, and liquidated damages for delays. To minimize the aggregate cost to both the IPP and the off-taker, penalties are often based on the marginal cost to the off-taker, accounting for energy and capacity charges.

## The full explanation

Power purchase agreements require a delicate balance of risks and costs. For instance, in setting delay liquidated damages, the goal is to minimize total costs. Completing a plant unrealistically fast increases IPP costs (e.g., overtime, rushed equipment orders), while delays cost the off-taker more if their existing energy sources are more expensive than the new plant's cost plus capacity payments.

The target completion date is theoretically optimized where the sum of IPP costs and off-taker costs is minimized. Once the target is established, penalties are calculated based on the off-taker's marginal cost less the avoided energy and capacity charges. Similar trade-offs apply to maintenance and availability penalties, comparing the IPP's cost of achieving higher reliability against the off-taker's benefit of having power available.

## Interview-ready answer

A Power Purchase Agreement (PPA) is a core contract in project finance that secures the revenue stream by dictating the terms under which an independent power producer sells energy to an off-taker. It involves complex risk-return trade-offs, particularly around delay liquidated damages and availability penalties, which should ideally be structured to minimize total costs to both parties.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Incorrectly measuring fixed and variable costs in contracts.
- Economic distortions when contract heat rates differ from actual heat rates.
