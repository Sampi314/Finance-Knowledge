# Take-or-pay contracts

> **TL;DR.** A contract under which an offtaker pays its supplier for a given volume regardless of whether the offtaker accepts that volume.

## When you'd use this

Used when evaluating standalone developments with construction and operational risks. Common in digital infrastructure (e.g., data centers) and carbon dioxide removal (CDR) projects, ensuring contractual reliability and stable cash flows to secure favorable financing terms and project assurance.

## The 30-second version

A take-or-pay contract allows the supplier to cover its fixed costs and be ready to supply in the future. It is a critical element for securing favorable financing terms by providing long-term tenant stability, cash flow predictability, and underwriting project finance (serving as a form of "bankable contracting").

## The full explanation

In Project Finance, take-or-pay agreements underwrite project finance. They guarantee a minimum revenue stream to the project company (supplier), protecting them from volume risk. If the buyer (offtaker) decides not to take the agreed-upon volume, they are still obligated to pay for it. This structure is similar to a capacity payment, which is designed to cover fixed costs. These contracts are evaluated for contractual reliability during corporate and project finance valuations.

## Interview-ready answer

A take-or-pay contract requires an offtaker to pay for a specified volume of a good or service, even if they don't take delivery of it. This provides the supplier with cash flow predictability to cover fixed costs and is essential for securing project financing.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- The absence of binding offtakes, like take-or-pay agreements, can leave projects stranded before a final investment decision.
