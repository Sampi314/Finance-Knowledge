# Concession agreements

> **TL;DR.** A concession agreement is a long-term contract under which a government entity grants a private company the right to build, operate, and maintain a public infrastructure asset for a finite period before it reverts to public ownership.

## When you'd use this

You would use this concept when structuring Public-Private Partnerships (PPPs) for infrastructure projects like toll roads, airports, and public facilities. It defines the revenue mechanism, allocates key risks (like demand and construction risks) between the public and private sectors, and establishes the project's finite lifespan.

## The 30-second version

A concession agreement is the legal foundation for many infrastructure investments. It shifts the burden of upfront construction costs and operational responsibilities from the government to private investors via a Special Purpose Vehicle (SPV). The private operator earns revenue—either from user fees like tolls or from government availability payments—over a fixed term (e.g., 20, 30, or 50 years). After the concession term ends, the asset usually reverts to the government, often at zero residual value.

## The full explanation

Concession agreements are critical for enabling private sector participation in public infrastructure. They clearly lay out the rights and obligations of the private operator and the contracting public authority.

Unlike perpetual corporate entities, infrastructure assets governed by a concession agreement have a finite life defined by the contract term. This directly impacts financial modeling: DCF models for these projects should not include a traditional terminal value based on perpetuity. Instead, cash flows are projected strictly up to the final year of the concession. In many Build-Operate-Transfer (BOT) structures, the asset reverts to the government at the end of the term, meaning any residual value must be explicitly defined by the contract.

The agreement also dictates the project's revenue model and risk allocation. For "user-pays" assets like toll roads, the private operator bears volume or demand risk, requiring models to project traffic and elasticity. Alternatively, for social infrastructure, the government might guarantee availability payments. The contract establishes the competitive bidding process and operational standards the private partner must maintain throughout the concession period.

## Excel and modeling notes

When modeling concession agreements, project cash flows only up to the end of the concession term without a standard perpetuity-based terminal value.

## Interview-ready answer

A concession agreement is a long-term contract in a Public-Private Partnership where a government grants a private operator the right to develop and operate an infrastructure asset. It defines the asset's finite life, allocates demand and operational risks, and dictates how the private sector earns back its investment before the asset reverts to public ownership.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Including a perpetual terminal value in a DCF model for an asset that reverts to the government at the end of the concession.
- Misallocating demand risk when distinguishing between user-pays models and government-backed availability payments.
- Failing to verify that residual or final disposition value is explicitly supported by the contractual terms.
