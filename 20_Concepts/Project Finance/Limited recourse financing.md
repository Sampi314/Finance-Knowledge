# Limited recourse financing

> **TL;DR.** Limited recourse financing is a structure where lenders primarily rely on a project's projected cash flows for repayment, with limited or no claim on the sponsors' other assets.

## When you'd use this

This financing method is typically used for large, complex, and capital-intensive ventures like infrastructure, energy, and natural resource projects. It is applied when sponsors want to ring-fence a project's liabilities, enabling higher leverage and protecting their core corporate assets from project-specific failures.

## The 30-second version

In limited recourse or non-recourse financing, lenders rely on the cash flows generated by the project itself to repay debt and earn returns, rather than the general creditworthiness of the project's sponsors. If the project defaults, the lenders can only seize the project's assets and cash flows. Because the lenders bear more risk, they conduct extensive due diligence and monitor the project intensively, while the sponsors benefit from off-balance sheet treatment and higher leverage potential.

## The full explanation

In project finance, limited recourse (or non-recourse) financing fundamentally shifts the risk from the project sponsors to the project entity itself, typically a Special Purpose Vehicle (SPV). The lenders have either no recourse or only limited recourse to the assets of the sponsors beyond what has been explicitly committed to the project.

This structure necessitates rigorous due diligence—covering technical, financial, legal, and environmental aspects—by the lenders, as their recovery in a default scenario is restricted to the project's cash flows and assets. To mitigate this risk, project finance deals incorporate complex contractual frameworks, such as Engineering, Procurement, and Construction (EPC) contracts, Operation and Maintenance (O&M) agreements, and offtake agreements, to clearly allocate risks to the parties best equipped to manage them.

The non-recourse nature allows projects to achieve high leverage ratios, often between 70:30 and 90:10 debt-to-equity, enhancing the sponsors' equity returns.

## Interview-ready answer

Limited recourse financing is a structure where lenders rely solely on the cash flows and assets of a specific project for repayment, without a general claim on the sponsors' corporate assets. It allows sponsors to undertake large capital-intensive projects with high leverage while shielding their balance sheets from project failure.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- The complexity of the structure leads to high transaction costs and extended timelines for financial close.
- Extensive documentation is required to clearly allocate risks and responsibilities due to the non-recourse nature.
- The rigid structure makes it challenging to adapt to significant changes in project circumstances or market conditions.
