# Linking the three statements

> **TL;DR.** The three financial statements are linked by Net Income flowing from the Income Statement to the Cash Flow Statement, the net change in cash updating the Cash account on the Balance Sheet, and changes in other balance sheet items mapping to cash flow activities.

## When you'd use this

You need to understand these links to build integrated three-statement financial models or to understand the holistic financial mechanics of a business. It is also the single most common technical screening question in investment banking interviews, meant to verify your foundation in basic accounting principles and ability to concisely explain complex concepts.

## The 30-second version

The three financial statements are interlinked and serve different purposes, but must balance together. Net Income from the Income Statement bridges to the Cash Flow Statement as the starting point for Cash Flow from Operations. After non-cash charges and working capital adjustments are made on the Cash Flow Statement, the resulting Net Change in Cash flows to the Balance Sheet to become the ending Cash balance. Simultaneously, Net Income links to Retained Earnings on the Balance Sheet, while capital expenditures and debt activities on the Cash Flow Statement update corresponding asset and liability balances on the Balance Sheet.

## The full explanation

A three-statement model is integrated, meaning it accurately captures the relationships between line items across the income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement. This integration enables you to change an assumption in one part of the model and immediately see its impact on all other parts correctly.

### Start with Net Income

The primary link between the statements is **Net Income**. Net Income is the profit a company earns after subtracting all expenses from its total revenue. It flows from the bottom of the Income Statement to the top of the Cash Flow Statement, serving as the first line item in Cash Flow from Operations.

### Adjust for Cash

Because Net Income is an accrual-based figure rather than a cash figure, it must be adjusted to find actual cash flow. Non-cash charges, such as Depreciation and Amortization, are added back. Additionally, working capital changes from the Balance Sheet—like increases in Accounts Receivable (a use of cash) or increases in Accounts Payable (a source of cash)—are adjusted to arrive at the true Cash Flow from Operations.

### Continue through the Cash Flow Statement

Next, Cash Flow from Investing captures items like Capital Expenditures (CapEx), while Cash Flow from Financing captures debt issuance, repayment, or dividends paid. The sum of all three sections yields the **Net Change in Cash**.

### Connect to the Balance Sheet

The Net Change in Cash flows to the Balance Sheet to update the Cash account for the period. Several other connections ensure the Balance Sheet equation (Assets = Liabilities + Equity) remains balanced:
*   **Net Income** flows from the Income Statement to increase **Retained Earnings** in Shareholders' Equity.
*   **CapEx** from the Cash Flow Statement increases **Property, Plant & Equipment (PP&E)**.
*   **Depreciation** reduces the book value of PP&E.
*   **Debt changes** update the Debt balance.
*   **Dividends paid** reduce Retained Earnings.

Once fully updated, the ending balances serve as the starting point for the next period, continuing the cycle.

## Excel and modeling notes

When building these linkages in Excel, it is critical to adhere to standard formatting practices: color-code inputs blue and formulas black, maintain standard column widths, and format data consistently. Ensure you use roll-forward schedules when forecasting balance sheet items, and avoid hardcoding any numbers on the Cash Flow Statement, as it should be a pure reconciliation of year-over-year changes in the Balance Sheet.

## Interview-ready answer

"The three statements are linked together by several key items. I'll start with Net Income from the Income Statement, which is the starting point for connecting to the other two statements..."
See [[What happens if Depreciation increases by 10]] for an example of a common scenario-based follow-up question.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Skipping the Cash Flow Statement entirely when trying to explain how the Income Statement and Balance Sheet connect.
- Confusing a "use of cash" with a "source of cash" when explaining working capital changes (e.g., mistakenly thinking an increase in accounts receivable is a source of cash).
- Using hardcoded numbers on the Cash Flow Statement instead of properly referencing changes in balance sheet accounts.
