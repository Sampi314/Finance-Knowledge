# Revenue build bottom-up

> **TL;DR.** Bottom-up forecasting is a budgeting method that projects future revenue by starting with fundamental unit-level drivers (like price and quantity) and aggregating them upwards.

## When you'd use this

You would use a bottom-up revenue build when creating detailed operating budgets, forecasting for an individual product line or business segment, and performing detailed FP&A work. This method is preferred when management needs a more accurate, actionable, and granular view of the business than what a top-down, market-share-driven approach provides.

## The 30-second version

A bottom-up revenue build estimates a company's financial performance starting from the lowest level of operations. Rather than assuming the company will capture a certain percentage of a broader market, this approach builds revenue from the ground up using specific operational metrics. For example, it might multiply the total number of expected orders by an average order value. The granular estimates from various departments or product lines are then summed up to calculate the overall company revenue. This methodology leads to setting specific, measurable goals and provides greater accuracy, though it risks getting bogged down in minor details.

## The full explanation

Bottom-up budgeting requires compiling granular cost and revenue estimates from the department or unit level and moving up to the top level. The process generally involves identifying the individual components of the business, establishing unit-level operational assumptions, summing these cost and revenue projections for each department, and finally adding them together to derive the overall budget.

This approach is fundamentally driven by specific operational variables that have a material impact on performance. At its core, the most basic formula it relies on is: `Revenue = Price × Quantity`. However, the exact unit economics are industry-specific:
- **B2B Software:** Active accounts × Average Contract Value.
- **Retail / D2C:** Number of Orders × Average Order Value (AOV).
- **Subscription Services:** Total Active Subscribers × Average Revenue Per User (ARPU).
- **Airlines:** Average Miles Flown × Average Revenue Per Kilometer (RPK).

There are two primary advantages to this method. First, it offers **greater accuracy**. Because estimates are derived from the individuals closest to the operational realities (e.g., department managers), they tend to be closer to reality. Second, it promotes **employee motivation**. By involving departmental employees in the budget-making process, they gain a sense of ownership and are more motivated to achieve the organization's goals.

Conversely, a major drawback of the bottom-up approach is that the specific, granular targets it sets open the door for increased scrutiny and criticism from external stakeholders, as these figures are often interpreted as precise predictions.

## Excel and modeling notes

When building a bottom-up forecast in Excel, ensure that the granular unit metrics used (like Average Order Value, Average Selling Price, or Product Count Per Order) are driven by historical data. It is common practice to project multiple operational scenarios (e.g., Base Case, Upside Case, Downside Case) using variables such as "% Growth in Total Orders" and "Change in Average Selling Price" rather than simply applying a flat growth rate to the top-line revenue figure.

## Interview-ready answer

In an interview, if asked about forecasting revenue, you can explain: "A bottom-up revenue build projects top-line performance by multiplying granular operational drivers—like the expected number of active users or units sold—by an average price or contract value. Unlike a top-down forecast which takes a percentage of total market size, the bottom-up approach provides a more defensible, accurate model grounded in a company's specific unit economics."

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Getting lost in the details: Modeling too many minor variables can create an overly complex, unwieldy model that defeats the purpose of forecasting.
- Ignoring double-counting with refunds: When calculating variables like Average Order Value (AOV) from historical data, use total revenue instead of net revenue so you don't double-count the impact of refunds if you plan to forecast refunds separately.
- Producing off-target budgets: Because departments create their own estimates, the sum of all department budgets may initially misalign with broader company objectives and require modification by top management.
