# Hostile vs friendly takeovers

> **TL;DR.** A friendly takeover occurs with the target board's approval and cooperation, while a hostile takeover is pursued directly against the target board's wishes via tender offers or proxy fights.

## When you'd use this

In M&A, the nature of the transaction dictates the strategy. Friendly takeovers are the standard approach when the target company's board believes the acquisition is in the best interest of shareholders and cooperates in the process. Hostile takeovers are used when the acquiring company believes the target is undervalued or a strong strategic fit, but the target's board rejects the offer, forcing the acquirer to bypass the board and appeal directly to shareholders.

## The 30-second version

Takeovers are broadly classified into two categories: friendly and hostile. In a friendly takeover, the target company's board of directors approves the acquisition and recommends it to shareholders. The process is collaborative, allowing the acquirer to conduct due diligence. In a hostile takeover, the target's board opposes the acquisition. The acquirer must bypass the board and appeal directly to shareholders using tactics like tender offers or proxy fights. Targets often employ defense mechanisms like poison pills or staggered boards to fend off hostile bids.

## The full explanation

### Friendly Takeovers
A friendly takeover begins with the acquiring company making an offer to the target company's board of directors. If the board believes the offer maximizes shareholder value, it will approve the transaction and recommend that shareholders vote in favor. This cooperative approach allows the acquirer to conduct thorough due diligence, reviewing non-public financial and legal information to confirm the target's value. The transaction is typically structured as a merger or acquisition and proceeds smoothly toward a shareholder vote.

Acquirers in friendly takeovers might offer cash, their own shares, or a combination, often at a premium to the current share price.

### Hostile Takeovers
A hostile takeover occurs when the target company's board rejects the acquirer's offer, yet the acquirer continues to pursue the acquisition. Without board approval, the acquirer cannot conduct private due diligence and must rely on public information.

To succeed, the acquirer must employ specific tactics to bypass the board:
1. **Tender Offer:** The acquirer offers to buy shares directly from the target's shareholders at a premium to the current market price. If enough shareholders accept the offer (usually a majority), the acquirer gains control.
2. **Proxy Fight:** The acquirer attempts to persuade existing shareholders to vote out the current board of directors and replace them with a new, acquirer-friendly board that will approve the takeover.

### Defense Strategies
Target companies utilize various defense mechanisms to deter hostile takeovers:
- **Poison Pill (Shareholder Rights Plan):** Triggers when an acquirer crosses a certain ownership threshold (e.g., 15-20%). It allows existing shareholders (excluding the acquirer) to buy newly issued shares at a steep discount, massively diluting the acquirer's stake and making the takeover prohibitively expensive.
- **Staggered Board:** Only a portion of the board is up for election each year, making it impossible for an acquirer to replace the entire board in a single proxy fight.
- **White Knight:** The target finds a friendlier company to acquire it, rescuing it from the hostile bidder.

## Interview-ready answer

Hostile takeovers involve an acquirer bypassing the target's board to appeal directly to shareholders, typically via a tender offer or proxy fight. In response, target companies often deploy defense mechanisms like a poison pill, which is a shareholder rights plan that massively dilutes the acquirer's stake by allowing other shareholders to buy discounted shares if the acquirer crosses a specific ownership threshold.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Assuming a premium offer will always be accepted; boards may reject offers if they believe the company's intrinsic value is higher or if better alternatives exist.
- Underestimating the time and expense of a hostile takeover, especially when strong defenses like a staggered board or poison pill are in place.
