# Hostile vs friendly takeovers

> **TL;DR.** A friendly takeover is approved by the target company's board of directors, whereas a hostile takeover proceeds against their wishes by appealing directly to shareholders.

## When you'd use this

These concepts apply when analyzing mergers and acquisitions strategies. They help determine how a bidder might approach an acquisition and how the target company might respond, directly impacting deal timeline, costs, and feasibility.

## The 30-second version

A takeover is the purchase of a target company by an acquiring company to realize synergies, eliminate competition, or acquire specific assets. The main difference between hostile and friendly takeovers lies in management approval. In a friendly takeover, the target's board approves the deal and recommends it to shareholders. In a hostile takeover, the board rejects the offer, forcing the acquirer to use tactics like tender offers or proxy fights to gain shareholder approval directly.

## The full explanation

A takeover is the process where one company, the acquirer, purchases another, the target. The strategy employed by the acquirer depends heavily on whether the target's leadership is receptive to the acquisition.

### Friendly Takeover Strategies
In a friendly takeover, the target's board of directors and management team are willing participants. They approve the proposal and advise their shareholders to vote in favor of the deal. Acquirers in friendly deals often offer:
- **Cash or Share Offers:** The acquirer can offer cash, their own shares, or a combination of both for each target company share.
- **Share Price Premium:** To incentivize the deal, acquirers often offer a percentage premium over the target's most recent closing share price.

### Hostile Takeover Strategies
When the target's board opposes the intended takeover, it becomes hostile. The acquiring company must bypass management and appeal directly to the target's shareholders to gain control. Common hostile strategies include:
- **Tender Offer:** The acquirer makes a direct offer to target shareholders to buy their shares at a premium above the market price, aiming to acquire a majority stake.
- **Proxy Fight (Proxy Vote):** The acquirer attempts to persuade existing shareholders to vote out the current board of directors during an annual general meeting and replace them with directors more receptive to the takeover.

### Defenses against a Hostile Takeover
Target companies use several defense mechanisms to thwart hostile acquirers:
- **Poison Pill:** Allowing current shareholders to buy new shares at a discount to dilute the acquirer's stake.
- **Crown Jewel Defense:** Selling the most valuable parts of the target company to make it less desirable.
- **Golden Parachute:** Contracts guaranteeing expensive payouts to key management if removed post-takeover, increasing acquisition costs.
- **Greenmail:** The target repurchases shares from the acquirer at a premium to prevent a controlling interest.
- **Pac-Man Defense:** The target company attempts to acquire the acquiring company.

## Interview-ready answer

The difference between a friendly and hostile takeover is whether the target company's board of directors approves the deal. In a friendly takeover, the board supports the acquisition and recommends it to shareholders, while in a hostile takeover, the board opposes the deal, forcing the acquirer to go directly to the shareholders via tender offers or proxy fights.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Assuming all takeovers start hostile; most are friendly and negotiated privately before becoming public.
- Confusing a tender offer with a hostile takeover; a tender offer is simply a tactic that can be used in both friendly and hostile situations, though it is a primary tool in hostile bids.
