# Accretion dilution analysis

> **TL;DR.** Accretion dilution analysis determines whether a merger or acquisition will increase or decrease the acquirer's post-transaction Earnings Per Share (EPS).

## When you'd use this

You would use this analysis during the evaluation phase of a potential M&A transaction. It provides a capital markets lens on how the deal will be perceived by shareholders immediately after closing. It helps in assessing the affordability of the target and measuring the impact of different financing structures (cash, debt, equity) on earnings.

## The 30-second version

Accretion and dilution is a simple test used to assess the EPS impact of a deal. If the combined company's pro forma EPS is higher than the acquirer's standalone EPS before the deal, the transaction is "accretive". If it is lower, it is "dilutive". This comparison effectively weighs the incremental earnings from the target against the incremental cost of financing the transaction. However, accretion is an earnings metric, not a measure of intrinsic value creation.

## The full explanation

Accretion/dilution analysis compares the target's earnings yield against the effective financing cost of the deal. If the earnings yield exceeds the cost of financing, the deal is mechanically likely to be accretive.

The analysis involves several steps:
1. Establish the standalone baseline EPS for the acquirer.
2. Determine the purchase price and deal structure (the mix of cash, debt, and stock).
3. Calculate pro forma net income by combining standalone net incomes and adjusting for incremental interest expense, lost interest income, amortization of acquired intangibles, and post-tax synergies.
4. Calculate pro forma shares outstanding, which include the acquirer's existing diluted shares plus any new shares issued for the deal.
5. Compute pro forma EPS by dividing pro forma net income by pro forma shares.
6. Assess whether the percentage change from standalone EPS is positive (accretive) or negative (dilutive).

A key shortcut for all-stock deals without synergies is the P/E rule: a deal is generally accretive if the acquirer's P/E multiple is higher than the target's implied P/E multiple.

## Excel and modeling notes

When building an accretion/dilution model, it is crucial to accurately forecast both the numerator (earnings pressure from interest or lost cash) and the denominator (share dilution from equity issuance). Synergies often swing a deal from dilutive to accretive in later years, but aggressive synergy assumptions are highly scrutinized. Do not confuse accretion with value creation; highly leveraged deals can be accretive while destroying fundamental value.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Relying solely on accretion/dilution ignores cash flows and the intrinsic long-term value generated by the deal.
- Focusing only on Year 1 EPS might cause the rejection of strategically sound deals that become heavily accretive in later years due to phased synergies.
- Overestimating synergies to artificially inflate pro forma earnings to make a deal look accretive.
