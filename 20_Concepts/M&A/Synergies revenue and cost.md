# Synergies revenue and cost

> **TL;DR.** Synergies represent the additional value created when two companies combine, exceeding the sum of their standalone values, typically achieved through cost savings or revenue increases.

## When you'd use this

You use synergy analysis when evaluating the potential value creation of a merger or acquisition. Projecting the types and amounts of synergies (cost, revenue, or financial) helps justify the acquisition premium an acquirer pays and forms a crucial part of building merger models and pro forma financial statements.

## The 30-second version

Synergies make the equation 2 + 2 = 5 in M&A. They are broadly categorized into cost synergies and revenue synergies. Cost synergies involve eliminating redundant expenses (like laying off duplicate corporate staff or consolidating facilities) and are generally considered highly reliable because management controls them. Revenue synergies involve increasing sales through cross-selling complementary products or expanding into new geographies, but they are viewed skeptically by analysts and heavily discounted because they depend on unpredictable customer behavior and take longer to materialize.

## The full explanation

Synergies are the fundamental justification for most M&A transactions. They represent the incremental value that neither company could achieve operating independently.

### Cost Synergies
Cost synergies arise when the combined entity can operate more efficiently. Because a combined company doesn't need duplicate corporate functions (like two CEOs, two finance departments, or redundant headquarters), these synergies yield immediate and quantifiable savings. Common sources include:
- **Headcount reduction:** Eliminating overlapping management and staff.
- **Facilities consolidation:** Closing redundant manufacturing plants, warehouses, or offices.
- **Procurement savings:** Leveraging increased buying power to negotiate better prices with suppliers.
- **IT and systems consolidation:** Standardizing technology platforms to reduce licensing and maintenance costs.

### Revenue Synergies
Revenue synergies are top-line enhancements resulting from the combination. While theoretically valuable, they are much harder to realize. Common sources include:
- **Cross-selling:** Selling one company's products to the other's existing customer base.
- **Complementary geographies:** Expanding market access by combining regional strengths.
- **Pricing power:** Potentially raising prices due to reduced competition or better product bundling.

### Financial Synergies
Beyond cost and revenue, financial synergies occur when the merged firm improves its capital structure. This might involve lowering the cost of capital, increasing debt capacity, or accessing better tax benefits.

Analysts treat these synergies very differently in valuation models. Because cost synergies are controllable and typically realize within 12 to 24 months, analysts often model them near management's projected levels. In contrast, revenue synergies face execution risks, integration complexities, and competitor responses, taking three to five years to materialize. Consequently, analysts apply significant "haircuts" (discounts) to management's revenue synergy projections.

## Excel and modeling notes

When modeling M&A transactions, cost synergies are directly modeled to improve EBITDA margins and boost free cash flow. If management projects $500 million in revenue synergies, conservative analysts might apply a massive haircut and only model $150 to $200 million in their base case.

## Interview-ready answer

Cost synergies result from eliminating duplicative expenses like redundant headcount, consolidated facilities, and shared IT systems, and are highly reliable as management can directly control them. Revenue synergies result from top-line growth opportunities such as cross-selling products or expanding into new geographies, but are far less reliable because they depend on unpredictable customer behavior and take years to materialize.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Assuming revenue synergies are guaranteed; they require complex integration and rely on customer behavior that management cannot directly control.
- Failing to account for the costs to achieve synergies, such as severance packages and system integration expenses.
- Overestimating the speed at which synergies will materialize, particularly for revenue synergies which can take years.
