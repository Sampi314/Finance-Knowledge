# Tender offers

> **TL;DR.** A tender offer is an offer to purchase shares of a target company at a premium to the market price.

## When you'd use this

A tender offer is primarily used by an acquiring company during an acquisition attempt. It is a common strategy in hostile takeovers when the target company's board of directors has no desire to be acquired or finds the bid price unacceptable, prompting the acquirer to bypass the board and appeal directly to shareholders.

## The 30-second version

In a tender offer, an acquirer offers to purchase shares from the stockholders of a target company at a premium to the market price. The goal is to obtain a controlling interest in the target company. There are also specialized forms, such as two-tier tender offers, where an initial high price is offered to gain control, followed by a lower price for remaining shares.

## The full explanation

A tender offer is a critical mechanism in the M&A landscape, primarily deployed in hostile takeover scenarios. When an acquiring company seeks to acquire a target company, but the board of directors rejects the bid, the acquirer may issue a tender offer directly to the shareholders. This offer proposes buying their shares at a premium to the market price.

**Two-Tier Tender Offers**
A strategy often used is the two-tier tender offer. The acquirer starts by offering an attractive price (higher price per share or a higher proportion of cash) for a limited number of shares to quickly gain a controlling stake. Once control is achieved, a second offer is made for the remaining shares, but at a reduced price per share compared to the original offer. This strategy benefits the acquirer by reducing the overall cost of the acquisition. To combat this, target companies may implement a "Fair Price Amendment" in their corporate charter, which requires potential acquirers to pay a uniform fair price for all acquired shares.

**Creeping Tender Offers**
Another variation is the creeping takeover or creeping tender offer. Here, an acquirer gradually purchases the target company's shares on the open market instead of making a formal tender offer immediately. This allows the acquirer to obtain shares at current market prices rather than paying the typical tender offer premium. However, in some countries, there are regulations governing this process that require the bidder to offer a formal bid upon holding a certain amount of shares.

## Interview-ready answer

A tender offer is an offer to purchase shares of a target company at a premium to the market price. It's often used as a strategy in hostile takeovers when the target company's board rejects an acquisition bid.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Two-tier tender offers force stockholders to accept an offer immediately or risk having to accept a reduced offer later.
- A failure in the creeping takeover of the target company will leave the acquirer with a large block of shares that it may need to liquidate, possibly at a loss.
- In some countries, creeping tender offers are subject to regulations that require the bidder to offer a formal bid once they hold a certain amount of shares.
