# Section 338 election

> **TL;DR.** A Section 338 election is a tax election that permits a corporation making a qualified stock purchase of a target company to treat the acquisition as an asset purchase for federal tax purposes.

## When you'd use this

You would use this election during a corporate acquisition when an acquirer is technically purchasing the stock of a target, but wishes to realize the tax benefits associated with purchasing assets. This allows the buyer to step up the tax basis of the target's assets, leading to future tax deductions from depreciation and amortization.

## The 30-second version

In a stock acquisition, the buyer typically assumes the seller's historical tax basis in the acquired net assets (carryover basis), meaning no future tax deductions are available for any premium paid above the target's net asset value. However, if an Internal Revenue Code (IRC) Section 338 election is made, the stock sale is treated hypothetically as an asset sale. This entitles the buyer to a stepped-up tax basis in the acquired assets—including tax-deductible goodwill (amortized over 15 years)—while usually triggering a taxable gain on the hypothetical asset sale.

## The full explanation

A Section 338 election bridges the mechanical simplicity of a stock deal with the tax advantages of an asset deal. It requires that a purchaser acquire at least 80% of the vote and value of the target company's stock in a "qualified stock purchase".

There are two primary forms of the election:

**Section 338(g) Election:**
In a Sec. 338(g) election, the target recognizes a gain on the deemed sale of its assets, and the tax impact is borne by the acquirer. Because the seller also recognizes gain on the sale of the stock, this results in two levels of tax. Therefore, it is generally not beneficial in domestic acquisitions. However, it is most useful for foreign acquisitions (where the foreign target has no U.S. operations and is not subject to U.S. tax). The assets are stepped up for U.S. tax purposes, generating higher depreciation and amortization which reduces future earnings and profits.

**Section 338(h)(10) Election:**
Commonly used in domestic acquisitions, the Sec. 338(h)(10) election results in a single level of tax. The target is deemed to have sold all its assets to the new target and distributed the proceeds to the old target shareholders, effectively ignoring the stock sale. The buyer receives the stepped-up tax basis for future depreciation and amortization without paying tax on the full amount of the gain up front. This requires the target to be a subsidiary in a consolidated group, a corporation 80% controlled without filing consolidated, or an S corporation, and requires joint agreement from both buyer and seller.

When an election is made, the purchase price must be allocated to seven specific asset classes under the residual method (Section 1060).

## Excel and modeling notes

In a financial model, a transaction with a Section 338 election requires stepping up the tax basis of the assets and creating a schedule for the newly tax-deductible depreciation and amortization. Because goodwill becomes amortizable for tax purposes over 15 years under IRC Section 197, the acquirer's cash taxes will be lower compared to a standard stock deal. The resulting tax savings increase the overall value and cash flow of the transaction.

## Interview-ready answer

A Section 338 election allows a stock acquisition to be treated as an asset acquisition for tax purposes. This gives the buyer a "stepped-up" basis in the acquired assets—including tax-deductible goodwill—which lowers future cash taxes through higher depreciation and amortization, though it may trigger an immediate taxable gain on the deemed asset sale depending on the specific form of the election.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Sec. 338(g) causes double taxation (tax on the stock sale and tax on the deemed asset sale) and is mostly only beneficial for foreign targets not subject to U.S. tax.
- Assuming all stock deals lack a tax step-up; a joint Section 338(h)(10) election provides the step-up while only imposing a single level of tax.
