# Tax-free reorganizations

> **TL;DR.** Tax-free reorganizations under Section 368 of the Internal Revenue Code allow companies to restructure, merge, or divide without triggering immediate corporate or shareholder-level tax liabilities.

## When you'd use this

You'd use a tax-free reorganization when an acquirer wants to use their stock to buy a target, or when a parent company wants to divest a subsidiary without tax penalties. They enable strategic combinations and divestitures that might otherwise be cost-prohibitive due to capital gains taxes on the transferred assets.

## The 30-second version

Section 368 of the U.S. tax code outlines transactions that qualify for tax-free treatment. For an acquisition, this typically involves a substantial portion of the purchase consideration being paid in the acquirer's stock. There are different types, such as statutory mergers (Type A), stock-for-stock exchanges (Type B), and stock-for-assets (Type C). In addition, complex structures like the Reverse Morris Trust allow a company to spin off an unwanted business tax-free, provided the original shareholders retain control of the merged entity.

## The full explanation

A tax-free reorganization is an M&A transaction that meets the statutory requirements of IRC Section 368(a), allowing it to avoid immediate taxation at the corporate and shareholder levels. To qualify, these transactions generally must satisfy judicial requirements: continuity of interest, continuity of business enterprise, and a valid business purpose.

**Acquisitive Reorganizations (Types A, B, C):**
- **Type A:** Tax-free mergers and consolidations where a subsidiary is absorbed into a parent, or two companies combine to form a new corporation. This category also includes forward triangular mergers and reverse triangular mergers.
- **Type B:** Stock-for-stock exchanges, where the acquirer trades its stock for the target company's stock, leaving target shareholders as minority owners in the acquirer.
- **Type C:** Stock-for-asset exchanges.

**Divisive Reorganizations:**
- **Type D:** Involves the divestiture of a portion of a group's holdings or division into smaller subsidiaries.
- A notable strategy in this category is the **Reverse Morris Trust**. This technique enables a company to sell off unwanted assets tax-free. A parent spins off a subsidiary with the unwanted assets, which is then immediately merged with a third party. Crucially, the new combined company must issue at least 50.1% of its voting stock (control) to the original parent company's shareholders.

**Other Reorganizations:**
- **Type E:** Recapitalizations, restructuring the proportion of debt and equity.
- **Type F:** Relocations or organizational structure changes.
- **Type G:** Insolvency reorganizations.

## Interview-ready answer

A tax-free reorganization under Section 368 allows companies to execute M&A deals or spin-offs without immediate tax consequences. To qualify in an acquisition, a significant portion of the deal must be paid in the buyer's stock, satisfying the "continuity of interest" requirement.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- **Anti-Morris Trust Rules:** Under Section 355(e), if one or more persons acquire 50% or more of the stock of the distributing or spun-off company as part of a plan, the spin-off is taxable at the corporate level. This strictly bounds the Reverse Morris Trust.
- **Continuity of Interest:** Tax-free status can be revoked if target shareholders immediately sell off the acquirer stock they receive, breaking the continuity of interest.
