# M&A overview

> **TL;DR.** Mergers and acquisitions (M&A) is the process of combining two companies into one, which involves strategy, valuation, due diligence, and integration.

## When you'd use this

You would use an M&A process when a company seeks to expand its operations, enter new markets, or gain competitive advantages by acquiring another business or merging with it.

## The 30-second version

The M&A process is a complex, multi-step journey that can take anywhere from 6 months to several years. It starts with developing an acquisition strategy and search criteria, followed by finding targets and initial planning. Then comes valuation, negotiations, and exhaustive due diligence. Finally, the deal is structured, financed, closed, and the two companies are integrated. The goal is often to generate synergies, where the combined company is more valuable than the sum of its parts.

## The full explanation

The standard M&A process typically involves 10 steps:
1. **Develop an acquisition strategy:** Understand the business purpose for acquiring.
2. **Set the M&A search criteria:** Identify key criteria like profit margins or geographic location.
3. **Search for potential acquisition targets:** Evaluate companies meeting the criteria.
4. **Begin acquisition planning:** Make contact to assess amenability to a deal.
5. **Perform valuation analysis:** Evaluate the target using provided financials.
6. **Negotiations:** Construct an offer and negotiate terms in detail.
7. **M&A due diligence:** Exhaustively confirm the target's value, metrics, and operations.
8. **Purchase and sale contract:** Execute a final contract (asset or share purchase).
9. **Financing strategy:** Finalize how the deal will be paid for.
10. **Closing and integration:** Close the deal and merge the management teams and operations.

### Strategic vs. Financial Buyers
- **Strategic buyers** are typically competitors or companies in adjacent industries looking to integrate the target into their core business to realize synergies.
- **Financial buyers** are institutional investors like private equity firms seeking to own the target as an investment, often using leverage (LBO) to finance the deal without operating it directly.

### Synergies
- **Hard (Operating) synergies:** Direct cost savings, such as eliminating redundant personnel.
- **Soft (Financial) synergies:** Revenue increases that are expected but harder to guarantee.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Rival bidders driving up the premium required to acquire the target company.
- Failing to realize expected soft synergies after the integration.
- Complications in deal structuring such as antitrust laws or tax implications.
