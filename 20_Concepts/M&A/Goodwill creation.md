# Goodwill creation

> **TL;DR.** Goodwill is an intangible asset created in accounting when a buyer acquires another company for a price higher than the fair market value of its net identifiable assets.

## When you'd use this

You encounter goodwill creation during mergers, acquisitions, and leveraged buyouts. When the acquirer's purchase price exceeds the target's net assets, the pro forma balance sheet will go out of balance unless an offsetting asset is recorded. Goodwill is calculated and recorded as this accounting "plug" during the purchase price allocation process to keep the combined balance sheet balanced.

## The 30-second version

Goodwill is the accounting recognition of a company's "economic goodwill" or intangible value, which includes assets that cannot be physically touched or precisely quantified, such as a strong brand identity, loyal customer base, proprietary technology, or talented workforce.

To calculate goodwill, you first determine the target's net book value, then adjust those assets and liabilities to their current fair market values. The difference between the actual purchase price paid and the fair market value of those net assets is recorded as goodwill on the acquirer's balance sheet. Once recorded, it stays on the books indefinitely and is not amortized, but it must be tested annually for impairment.

## The full explanation

### The Mechanics of Goodwill Creation
In purchase price accounting, Buyers are required to re-value everything on Sellers' Balance Sheets to their fair market values. If a Seller's factories, inventory, or intellectual property are worth more or less than their book values, they must be adjusted.

Because Buyers usually pay a premium over the Common Shareholders' Equity (or net identifiable assets), the Combined Balance Sheet will go out of balance immediately after the deal closes. Goodwill is created as a "plug" on the Assets side to ensure that Assets = Liabilities + Equity.

### How to Calculate Goodwill
The process to accurately calculate goodwill involves several steps:
1. **Find Book Value:** Determine the book value of the target's assets (current, non-current, fixed, and intangible) and liabilities.
2. **Determine Fair Value:** Have an accountant assess the fair current market value of those assets.
3. **Calculate Adjustments:** Find the difference between the fair value and book value for each asset (e.g. writing up PP&E or writing down obsolete inventory).
4. **Find Excess Purchase Price:** Calculate the difference between the purchase price paid and the net book value of the company's assets.
5. **Deduct Adjustments:** Subtract the fair value adjustments from the excess purchase price to arrive at the final goodwill figure.

### Deferred Taxes and Complexities
The calculation in real life can be complex because new Deferred Tax Liabilities (DTLs) often get created due to the write-ups of Fixed Assets and Other Intangible Assets. If an item like a DTL increases liabilities (or reduces Equity), it means more Goodwill is needed to boost Assets and keep the balance sheet balanced.

### Goodwill Impairment
Unlike physical assets or definite-lived intangibles, goodwill has an indefinite life and does not depreciate or amortize under US GAAP and IFRS. Instead, it is subject to an annual impairment test. Goodwill impairment is an accounting charge that occurs when the value of goodwill is determined to be below its carrying amount—typically because the acquired assets are failing to generate their expected future cash flows. The reduction is recorded as a charge on the income statement.

## Excel and modeling notes

When modeling goodwill for an LBO or M&A transaction, it naturally flows from the Sources & Uses table. First, total all sources of capital, then subtract uses to arrive at Seller Proceeds. Goodwill is equal to Seller Proceeds less the net identifiable assets of the target company. Net identifiable assets are equal to identifiable assets less liabilities, which is equal to shareholders' equity.

## Interview-ready answer

Goodwill is an intangible asset created in an M&A transaction when the purchase price exceeds the fair market value of the target's net identifiable assets. It represents the premium paid for unquantifiable assets like brand recognition or customer loyalty, and acts as an accounting plug to ensure the post-deal balance sheet stays balanced. It is not amortized, but is tested annually for impairment.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Confusing historical book value with fair market value. Goodwill is the excess of purchase price over *fair* market value. You must adjust assets to fair value before calculating goodwill.
- Forgetting to account for Deferred Tax Liabilities (DTLs) created by asset write-ups, which effectively increase the amount of goodwill created.
- Thinking goodwill is created in standard asset acquisitions. Goodwill is an intangible asset only recognized for the purchase of a complete entity/business combination.
