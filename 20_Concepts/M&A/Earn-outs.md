# Earn-outs

> **TL;DR.** An earn-out is a contingent payment structure in M&A deals where part of the purchase price is paid only if the acquired business achieves specific performance milestones or financial targets after closing.

## When you'd use this

You would use an earn-out when there is a fundamental disagreement between the buyer and seller about the future prospects and valuation of the target company. They are especially prevalent in private company acquisitions and industries with high uncertainty, such as life sciences and technology, helping to bridge valuation gaps and manage risk.

## The 30-second version

An earn-out is a mechanism in mergers and acquisitions that makes a portion of the purchase price contingent on the target's future performance. Instead of paying the full price upfront, the buyer pays a base amount at closing and agrees to pay additional amounts if the company hits predefined targets (like revenue, EBITDA, or specific milestones) within a set period, typically 1 to 5 years. This aligns incentives, shifts some downside risk to the seller, and allows a deal to proceed despite initial valuation differences.

## The full explanation

Earn-outs serve multiple strategic purposes in M&A beyond simply resolving valuation impasses. They are powerful risk mitigation tools for buyers acquiring early-stage businesses, companies awaiting regulatory decisions, or targets in rapidly evolving markets.

### Structure and Metrics
The foundation of an earn-out is the performance metric. These can be:
- **Financial metrics:** Revenue, EBITDA, gross profit, or net income.
- **Operational metrics:** Customer retention, production levels, or market share.
- **Milestone-based metrics:** FDA approvals, patent grants, or product development completion.

The choice of metric is critical. Buyers often prefer profit-based metrics to ensure they are paying for true value creation, while sellers prefer top-line revenue metrics because they are harder to manipulate through post-close expense allocation.

### Buyer and Seller Perspectives
- **Buyers:** Seek broad discretion to run the business without operational constraints, favor shorter earn-out periods to limit exposure, and prefer profit-based metrics with clear definitions.
- **Sellers:** Seek detailed covenants to ensure the buyer operates the business consistently to allow target achievement. They worry about achievement risk (only about 59% of earn-outs pay out) and potential buyer manipulation of metrics or operations.

### Common Disputes
Earn-outs are sometimes called "litigation magnets" due to disagreements over metric calculations (e.g., revenue recognition or expense allocation) and operating decisions made by the buyer that the seller claims undermined earn-out achievement. Clear drafting and defined dispute resolution mechanisms are essential.

## Worked example

Consider a simplified example:
- The seller believes their company is worth $100 million based on aggressive projected growth.
- The buyer believes it is worth only $70 million based on more conservative assumptions.
- They agree on $70 million upfront plus up to $30 million in earn-outs if the company hits certain revenue or profitability targets.

If the seller's optimistic view proves correct, they receive the full $100 million. If growth disappoints, the buyer pays less than they would have under a higher fixed price.

## Interview-ready answer

An earnout is a contingent payment mechanism where sellers receive additional purchase price if performance targets are met after closing. They are used to bridge valuation gaps when buyers and sellers disagree about future prospects, allowing transactions to proceed by shifting risk to sellers and aligning incentives during transitions.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- **Buyer manipulation:** Sellers risk the buyer loading the business with expenses or changing strategy, intentionally or inadvertently undermining the earn-out targets.
- **Calculation disagreements:** Disputes frequently arise over how to define and calculate metrics like EBITDA or revenue recognition, making precise legal definitions critical.
- **Operating restrictions:** Buyers may find their strategic flexibility limited by covenants required by sellers to protect the earn-out.
