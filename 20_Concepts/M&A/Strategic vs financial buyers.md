# Strategic vs financial buyers

> **TL;DR.** A strategic buyer acquires a company to integrate it into existing operations and realize synergies, whereas a financial buyer acquires a company as an investment to generate a return, typically using leverage.

## When you'd use this

You would use this distinction when a company is being sold in M&A or LBO scenarios to understand the motivations of potential acquirers, as this dictates the purchase price, deal structure, and post-acquisition operations.

## The 30-second version

When a company is sold, buyers fall into two main categories: strategic and financial. Strategic buyers are typically competing or adjacent operating companies looking for horizontal or vertical expansion. They aim to integrate the target's products or services into their own to achieve synergies, and they often pay a higher premium because of this. Financial buyers, like Private Equity firms, view the acquisition as an investment. They use borrowed funds (leverage) to acquire the target, aiming to generate satisfactory returns and make good on their investment over time.

## The full explanation

### Strategic Buyers
Strategic buyers acquire a business because it aligns with their long-term plans. This might be for vertical integration or horizontal expansion. Their primary goal is to realize operational synergies instantly, such as economies of scale. Because they can quickly absorb the target and generate value from these synergies, they are generally willing to pay a higher price than financial buyers. Strategic buyers are often well-established companies with easier access to capital and may finance the deal with cash, stock, or a mix of both.

### Financial Buyers
Financial buyers, such as Private Equity firms, treat an acquisition as an investment. They look to increase revenues and cash flow through techniques like reducing expenses and creating economies of scale, before ultimately exiting the company (e.g., taking it public or selling it) once maximum returns are realized. Since they don't necessarily integrate the business into existing operations, they don't benefit from initial operational synergies in the same way. Financial buyers heavily rely on borrowed funds (leverage) to finance the acquisition. When choosing targets, they look for consistency in financial statements.

### Which Buyer is Best?
The ideal buyer depends on the seller's specific goals:
- **Seller looking only for returns:** If the goal is purely the highest price, a strategic buyer is usually best, as they are likely to offer more for the firm.
- **Seller wants to protect employees:** If the seller wants a high price but also wants to protect the employees, a strategic buyer is still best, provided the seller sets limitations for the acquisition.
- **Cashing out but staying involved:** If the seller wants to cash out but remain involved in operations, a financial buyer is often the best fit. Strategic buyers may already have the expertise to run operations, whereas financial buyers may provide the capital but lack the specific operational knowledge, thereby needing to retain top-level management.

## Interview-ready answer

A strategic buyer is an operating company looking to acquire another business for operational synergies, such as horizontal or vertical integration, and usually pays a higher premium. A financial buyer, like a private equity firm, treats the acquisition as a standalone investment, aiming to generate returns through financial structuring (using leverage) and operational improvements before exiting, and usually relies on the target's existing management.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Forgetting that strategic buyers can justify paying more specifically because they can factor expected synergies into their valuation.
- Assuming financial buyers will fully take over day-to-day operations; they often lack the necessary knowledge and must retain top-level management.
