# Add-on acquisitions

> **TL;DR.** An add-on acquisition is the purchase of a smaller target company by an existing private equity portfolio company to integrate it into the larger "platform" company.

## When you'd use this

Private equity firms and their portfolio companies use add-on acquisitions (or the "buy-and-build" strategy) to accelerate inorganic growth. It is especially useful in fragmented markets where consolidating smaller competitors can yield significant synergies and increase market share without the risks of a single massive buyout.

## The 30-second version

In a buy-and-build strategy, a private equity firm first acquires a strong, stable "platform" company. This platform then acquires smaller "add-on" targets. Because these add-ons are smaller, they are typically bought at lower valuation multiples than the platform company commands. Once integrated, the add-on's earnings are valued at the platform's higher multiple (multiple arbitrage), instantly creating value. Additionally, integrating the add-on typically unlocks revenue synergies like cross-selling and cost synergies like reducing redundant back-office expenses.

## The full explanation

An add-on acquisition involves an existing portfolio company (the platform) acquiring smaller competitors or complementary businesses. The platform company serves as the anchor and typically possesses robust infrastructure, a strong management team, and established market leadership. Add-on targets, in contrast, may lack resources, optimal capital structure, or efficient management, presenting significant upside potential upon integration.

### Multiple Arbitrage
A primary financial driver of add-on acquisitions is multiple arbitrage. Smaller companies usually trade at lower valuation multiples than larger, more established companies. If a platform company valued at 10x EBITDA acquires an add-on for 6x EBITDA, the cash flows of the add-on are immediately valued at 10x EBITDA once integrated into the platform. This creates instant, accretive value for the private equity sponsor before any operational improvements are made.

### Value Creation and Synergies
Beyond multiple arbitrage, add-ons drive value through operational improvements and synergies. Revenue synergies can arise from geographic expansion, cross-selling products, and increased pricing power due to reduced competition. Cost synergies frequently include eliminating overlapping workforce functions, consolidating facilities, and gaining better negotiating leverage with suppliers due to increased scale. Together, these levers help grow the combined company's EBITDA, which is critical for driving LBO returns (IRR and MOIC) as reliance on high leverage has decreased.

## Excel and modeling notes

When modeling add-on acquisitions within an LBO, analysts typically build a roll-up model that phases in the target's financials over time. It is crucial to factor in the timing of the acquisition, the specific purchase multiple for the add-on, and the schedule for realizing synergies. Costs to achieve these synergies (integration costs) must also be modeled in the early years.

## Interview-ready answer

An add-on acquisition is when an existing PE portfolio company acquires a smaller business to integrate into its operations. This buy-and-build strategy creates value through multiple arbitrage—buying a smaller company at a lower multiple and valuing it at the platform's higher multiple—and by realizing cost and revenue synergies that grow overall EBITDA.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Underestimating integration costs and the complexity of merging different corporate cultures and IT systems.
- Overestimating the speed and magnitude of cost and revenue synergies.
- Failing to secure a strong platform company initially, which makes subsequent roll-ups fragile and harder to execute.
