# Management equity rollover

> **TL;DR.** Rollover equity is the portion of a seller's or management team's existing ownership stake that is reinvested into the acquiring entity rather than converted to cash at closing.

## When you'd use this

Rollover equity is fundamental to leveraged buyouts. It is used to align the incentives of the management team or founders with the private equity sponsor, reduce the new equity check required by the sponsor, and signal confidence in the company's future prospects.

## The 30-second version

In an LBO, instead of taking all cash for their shares, key management members or founders exchange a portion of their equity for shares in the new company formed by the PE acquirer. This keeps their "skin in the game," motivating them to drive EBITDA growth and successful exits. For the sponsor, it lowers the initial cash equity they need to invest, which can boost their return on invested capital.

## The full explanation

Rollover equity works by having existing shareholders exchange their target company shares for shares in the new acquisition vehicle (Newco/Holdco). This structure allows both the sponsor and rollover shareholders to participate proportionally in future value creation.

Typical rollover amounts vary, often ranging from 50-100% for management teams and 10-40% for founders or controlling shareholders. These shares usually come with limited governance rights compared to the sponsor's equity and may be subject to vesting or transfer restrictions as a retention tool.

In the sources and uses table of an LBO, rollover equity appears on both sides. It counts as a use of funds (part of the purchase price) and as a source of funds (equity contributed to the new capital structure), reducing the new cash the sponsor must provide dollar-for-dollar.

Depending on the deal structure, rollover may qualify for tax-deferred treatment, allowing the selling shareholder to avoid recognizing immediate gains on the rolled portion, preserving capital for compounding over the holding period.

## Excel and modeling notes

In an LBO model, rollover equity reduces the sponsor's required equity contribution. When calculating entry ownership, rollover percentage is calculated as rollover equity divided by total equity (sponsor plus rollover). At exit, both sponsor and rollover shareholders participate pro rata in gains based on these ownership percentages.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Confusing rollover equity with an option pool, as rollover has immediate value and comes from existing shares, whereas options require vesting and rely on future value increases.
- Assuming rollover equity reduces the purchase price; it is part of the total purchase price but reduces the new cash equity the sponsor must contribute.
- Not accounting for the tax implications, as some rollover structures may not be tax-deferred, creating a potential cash flow issue for the seller.
