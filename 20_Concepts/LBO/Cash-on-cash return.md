# Cash-on-cash return

> **TL;DR.** The cash-on-cash return compares a property's or investment's annual pre-tax cash flow to the initial equity contribution, showing the yield on invested cash for a specific year.

## When you'd use this

You'd use this to evaluate the short-term performance of real estate investments or leveraged buyouts. It helps determine the annual yield generated solely on the cash you personally invested, particularly when debt is used to finance the purchase.

## The 30-second version

The cash-on-cash return, or "cash yield," measures a real estate investor's annual pre-tax earnings relative to the initial equity outlay. Because it subtracts debt service (mortgage payments) from operating income, it reflects a "levered" return on the actual cash invested. This makes it a snapshot for a single year's performance, commonly used to quickly assess whether an investment provides adequate short-term cash flow. A common target range is 8% to 12%, though this varies by market.

## The full explanation

The cash-on-cash return isolates the performance of the equity portion of an investment. In deals involving debt, such as commercial real estate or LBOs, the cash flow available to investors is reduced by interest and principal payments.

Unlike the return on investment (ROI), which calculates cumulative yield across an entire holding period, the cash-on-cash return covers a current, one-year period. Since it only factors in debt service for that year, it can fluctuate annually based on rental income, expense changes, or refinancing.

It is also distinct from the capitalization rate (Cap Rate). The Cap Rate divides unlevered Net Operating Income (NOI) by the market value of the property, ignoring financing costs entirely. Cash-on-cash return accounts for financing costs, making it highly applicable to debt-funded acquisitions where the investor wants to know the exact cash yield on their down payment.

## Interview-ready answer

The cash-on-cash return measures the annual pre-tax cash flow generated by an investment relative to the initial equity invested. It is calculated on a post-financing basis, meaning debt service is deducted, providing a snapshot of the cash yield for a single year.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- **Ignores long-term value**: It only looks at a one-year snapshot and does not account for property appreciation, loan principal paydown, or the total return upon exit (unlike IRR or ROI).
- **Pre-tax limitation**: It does not factor in the investor's individual tax bracket or the tax benefits of depreciation.
