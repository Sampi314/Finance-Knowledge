# Tax considerations in RE

> **TL;DR.** In real estate, accurately distinguishing between real and personal property is a key tax consideration that can minimize property tax liability.

## When you'd use this

You would use this concept when evaluating a property's overall tax burden, particularly for properties with significant manufacturing facilities or equipment. Proper categorization can reveal instances where property was overtaxed, omitted from taxation, or taxed twice by the local jurisdiction.

## The 30-second version

The distinction between real property (buildings, improvements, land rights) and personal property (tangible movable items like equipment and furniture) has a major impact on taxes. Because many states have reduced or eliminated taxes on personal property, properly classifying assets as 'personal' rather than 'real' can lower the overall property tax burden. Additionally, personal property can often be depreciated more quickly.

## The full explanation

Almost every piece of property falls into the category of real or personal property. Real property generally consists of buildings, improvements, fixtures, and land rights. Personal property includes movable tangible items like tanks, overhead cranes, and furniture.

While these definitions seem straightforward, there is significant interpretation regarding what constitutes a fixture—items that were once personal property but have become attached to the real estate in a permanent manner. Most jurisdictions apply a three-part test (originating from *Teaff v. Hewitt*) to determine if an item is a fixture:
- **Annexation/Attachment:** How the property is connected to the real estate and how much damage its removal might cause.
- **Adaptation:** How the property is used in relation to the real estate.
- **Intent:** Whether the property is intended to be permanently affixed.

Because many states have phased out personal property taxes, and because personal property can generally be depreciated more quickly than long-lived real estate assets, accurately classifying and segregating these assets is a worthwhile exercise to minimize tax liability.

## Interview-ready answer

In real estate tax considerations, a major factor is properly categorizing assets as either real or personal property. Real property is typically taxed at different rates and has longer depreciation schedules, while personal property may be untaxed in some states and can be depreciated more quickly. Accurately classifying fixtures and equipment can materially reduce a property's tax liability.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Companies with significant manufacturing facilities often overlook distinguishing real and personal property, which may have a substantial impact on taxes.
- The lack of a distinct definition of a fixture and the grey area surrounding the delineation of real vs. personal property can lead to ambiguity.
