# Cash-on-cash return for RE

> **TL;DR.** The cash-on-cash return compares a real estate investment property's annual pre-tax cash flow to the initial equity contribution.

## When you'd use this

You would use the cash-on-cash return metric when evaluating the short-term, typically annual, yield of an income-generating real estate investment. It is especially useful for analyzing investments funded partially by debt, as it measures the return on the actual cash invested rather than the total purchase price.

## The 30-second version

The cash-on-cash return, sometimes called "cash yield", calculates the total cash earned on the total cash invested over a specific period, usually one year. It is determined by taking the annual pre-tax cash flow (after debt service) and dividing it by the initial equity invested. This metric offers a quick snapshot of performance, distinguishing itself from the cap rate by including financing costs, and from IRR or ROI by focusing only on a single year rather than the entire holding period.

## The full explanation

The cash-on-cash return provides insight into a real estate investor's annual yield on their actual equity outlay. It is a levered metric, meaning it factors in the effects of debt financing, such as mortgage interest and principal payments, which are subtracted from Net Operating Income (NOI) to find the annual pre-tax cash flow.

A key distinction is between cash-on-cash return and the capitalization rate (cap rate). While the cap rate divides the unlevered net operating income by the property's overall market value, the cash-on-cash return divides the levered cash flow by the invested equity.

Furthermore, cash-on-cash return differs from the Internal Rate of Return (IRR) and Return on Investment (ROI). IRR accounts for the time value of money over the life of an investment, and ROI measures the cumulative return across the entire holding period. In contrast, cash-on-cash return evaluates the yield for a specific year, ignoring the timing of future cash flows and the eventual sale of the property. For early-stage and venture capital investments, the equivalent metric is often called the money-on-money multiple, emphasizing the total return relative to capital invested regardless of time.

## Interview-ready answer

The cash-on-cash return measures the annual cash yield of a property relative to the initial equity invested. It's calculated by dividing the pre-tax cash flow, after deducting mortgage payments, by the actual cash down payment. Unlike the cap rate, it is a levered metric that accounts for debt financing.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- It ignores the time value of money, treating a dollar earned in year one the same as year ten.
- It is a single-period metric and does not capture the overall return across the entire investment lifespan.
- It does not account for the tax implications of the investment, as it relies on pre-tax cash flow.
