# Cash-on-cash return for RE

> **TL;DR.** The cash-on-cash return measures the annual pre-tax cash flow received per dollar of initial equity investment on a property.

## When you'd use this

Real estate investors use cash-on-cash return to evaluate the short-term (usually one-year) yield on a property investment relative to the actual cash they invested out of pocket. It's particularly useful when comparing properties that use debt financing, as it considers debt service.

## The 30-second version

Also known as "cash yield", the cash-on-cash return is the ratio of annual pre-tax cash flow to the initial equity invested. Unlike a cap rate, which measures return as if the property were bought in all cash (unlevered), the cash-on-cash return is a levered metric. It accounts for financing costs like mortgage payments by subtracting them from Net Operating Income (NOI) before dividing by the equity contribution. A generally targeted range for a good cash-on-cash return is between 8% and 12%.

## The full explanation

The cash-on-cash return provides an investor with an estimate of the annual yield received on a specific property relative to their actual out-of-pocket investment.

### Key Components

1. **Annual Pre-Tax Cash Flow**: This is the expected annual pre-tax income from the investment. It starts with the property's Net Operating Income (NOI) and subtracts debt service (e.g., mortgage interest and principal). Other expenses factored in include property taxes, maintenance fees, insurance premiums, and property upgrades.
2. **Invested Equity**: The initial outlay of cash on the date of purchase (i.e., the down payment or equity contribution).

### Cash-on-Cash Return vs. Cap Rate

While both are key real estate metrics, they serve different purposes:
- **Cap Rate**: An unlevered metric based on NOI and the property's total market value. It neglects the effects of financing.
- **Cash-on-Cash Return**: A levered metric based on pre-tax cash flow (after debt service) and only the equity invested.

### Cash-on-Cash Return vs. ROI

Return on Investment (ROI) calculates the yield across the entire holding period, factoring in the property's sale and total accumulated debt obligations. In contrast, the cash-on-cash return usually evaluates the current period (e.g., one year) and can fluctuate year over year based on rental income and expenses.

## Interview-ready answer

The cash-on-cash return is a levered return metric that calculates the annual pre-tax cash flow of a property relative to the initial equity invested. It helps real estate investors evaluate the short-term yield of a property after factoring in debt service.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- It only measures the return for a single year and does not account for the property's appreciation over time.
- The metric does not capture the overall return across the entire holding period, unlike the IRR or cumulative ROI.
- Changes in financing costs, taxes, or unexpected maintenance can significantly alter the cash-on-cash return year-over-year.
