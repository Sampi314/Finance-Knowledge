# Refinance proceeds

> **TL;DR.** Refinance proceeds are the extra cash generated and distributed to equity investors when a property's existing debt is replaced with a larger new loan, typically due to an increase in property value or a decrease in interest rates.

## When you'd use this

You would calculate refinance proceeds in real estate models or project finance when an asset has appreciated in value, stabilized its cash flows, or when interest rates have dropped. This allows sponsors to pull out some of their initial equity investment before actually selling the property, which can significantly boost their internal rate of return (IRR).

## The 30-second version

Refinancing means replacing an existing loan with a new one. If a property's Net Operating Income (NOI) has grown since it was acquired, its overall value has likely increased. If an investor takes out a new loan based on this higher value at the same or similar Loan-to-Value (LTV) ratio, the new loan amount will be larger than the outstanding balance of the old loan. The difference between the new loan amount and the old loan balance (minus any financing fees) is the "refinance proceeds," which are distributed as cash to the equity investors early.

## The full explanation

Equity investors in real estate and project finance often plan to refinance debt to boost returns. There are three primary drivers for refinancing that generate proceeds or improve cash flow:

**1. Increased Property Value:**
As a property's NOI increases (e.g., through higher rents or better management), its valuation grows. By refinancing at the same Loan-to-Value (LTV) ratio, the sponsor can secure a larger total loan. The excess funds, after paying off the original debt, go straight to the equity investors.

**2. Lower Interest Rates or Improved Credit Profile:**
If interest rates have fallen, or the property has stabilized and become less risky, investors can secure a new loan with a lower interest rate. While this might not always generate a large lump sum of refinance proceeds on day one, it reduces ongoing interest expenses, thereby increasing periodic cash flow to equity.

**3. Maturing Short-term Debt:**
Many developments use short-term construction or bridge loans that must be replaced once the property is built or stabilized. Lenders for these initial phases do not want to hold the long-term risk. Thus, the property must be refinanced into a permanent loan.

While refinancing can boost IRR by returning capital earlier, it also carries risks. It can increase default risk if the new debt burden lowers the Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR) too much, and it relies heavily on accurate estimations of the property's future stabilized value.

## Interview-ready answer

Refinance proceeds are the extra cash returned to equity investors when they replace an existing property loan with a new, larger loan. This usually happens when the property's value has increased due to NOI growth, allowing the sponsor to borrow more against the asset and distribute the difference, which boosts their IRR before the actual sale.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Refinancing can increase default risk if the new interest payments lower the Debt Service Coverage Ratio too much.
- Estimating the property's value for the new loan can be tricky if it hasn't fully stabilized.
- In some scenarios, interest rates might rise or property values might fall, making refinancing unviable or detrimental.
