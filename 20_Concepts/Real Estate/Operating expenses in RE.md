# Operating expenses in RE

> **TL;DR.** Operating expenses in real estate are the regular costs required to run a property's day-to-day operations, such as property management fees, utilities, and maintenance.

## When you'd use this

You use operating expenses when building a real estate pro-forma to calculate the Net Operating Income (NOI). By subtracting these expenses from the Effective Gross Income (EGI), you can evaluate the property's profitability and determine its value using a cap rate. They are essential for modeling both normal operations and downside scenarios.

## The 30-second version

Operating expenses are the short-term costs incurred regularly to maintain and run a business or property, unlike capital expenditures which are long-term investments. In real estate, these include property management fees, utilities, maintenance, insurance, property taxes, and reserves. They can be projected as a percentage of Effective Gross Income, based on a dollar amount per square foot, or as a percentage of the property's overall value. Accurately projecting these costs is crucial because they directly impact the Net Operating Income, a key metric for valuing the property.

## The full explanation

Operating expenses (OpEx) represent the day-to-day costs required to run a business's core operations. In the context of real estate, you list the operating expenses required to run the property's day-to-day operations after determining the Potential Revenue and making necessary deductions to find the Effective Gross Income.

Common operating expenses in real estate include:
- **Property Management Fees:** Owners rarely manage properties directly, often hiring third-party companies to handle tenants, collect rent, and set up repairs.
- **Maintenance and Repairs:** The costs of keeping the property in good condition.
- **Utilities and Insurance:** Basic costs associated with maintaining the physical asset.
- **Property Taxes:** Levied by local governments to fund public services.
- **Reserves:** Funds set aside to smooth out cash flows for large, irregular capital costs. There is debate on whether reserves should affect NOI, but including them is a conservative approach favored by lenders.

For larger properties, operating expenses might also cover staff payroll, sales and marketing, janitorial services, landscaping, and security.

Operating expenses are critical for determining the Net Operating Income (NOI). The formula used is Effective Gross Income minus Operating Expenses and Property Taxes. This is similar to EBITDA for normal companies, but they are not exactly the same since NOI might partially reflect capital costs if reserve allocations are deducted.

## Worked example

If a property has management fees set at 3% of Effective Gross Income (EGI), and the EGI is $1 million, then the property management fees are $30,000. For other expenses like maintenance or utilities, if they cost $10.00 per Rentable Square Foot per Year and the property has 50,000 rentable square feet, the annual operating expenses would be $500,000. For property taxes, if the most recently assessed value is $20 million and taxes are 3% of the value, the tax expense is $600,000.

## Excel and modeling notes

Some operating expenses are projected based on a percentage of Effective Gross Income, while others are calculated based on a dollar amount per square foot or square meter. In addition, some may be calculated as percentages of the property's value.

## Interview-ready answer

Operating expenses are the recurring, day-to-day costs required to maintain and operate a property, such as property management fees, utilities, property taxes, and insurance. They are deducted from Effective Gross Income to calculate Net Operating Income (NOI), making them a critical component for valuing real estate and analyzing investment performance.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Failing to properly distinguish between operating expenses and capital costs (like CapEx, TIs, and LCs) can lead to an inaccurate NOI and, consequently, an incorrect property valuation.
- There is disagreement on whether reserve allocations should be included in operating expenses; excluding them might result in a less conservative NOI that lenders do not favor.
