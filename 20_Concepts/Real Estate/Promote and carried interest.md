# Promote and carried interest

> **TL;DR.** Carried interest (or promote) is a right to participate in the future profits of a partnership, typically serving as the performance fee or profit share paid to general partners or sponsors once specific return hurdles are met.

## When you'd use this

You encounter promote and carried interest when analyzing the compensation structures of private equity funds, real estate partnerships, venture capital, and hedge funds. It is essential for understanding how the general partner (GP) or sponsor is incentivized to generate high returns for the limited partners (LPs).

## The 30-second version

Carried interest, often just called "carry" or "promote" in real estate, is the share of profits that the fund manager or sponsor earns. Unlike a management fee, which is a fixed percentage paid annually (usually around 2%), carried interest is variable and performance-based. Typically, the GP receives 20% of the profits, but only after the investors receive their initial capital back plus a minimum promised return, known as the hurdle rate (often 8% to 12%). This structure aligns the manager's interests with the investors by tying their true upside directly to the fund's long-term success.

## The full explanation

Carried interest gives general partners a percentage of profits as an incentive to maximize returns. In most alternative investment funds, the standard carry is 20%, though this can vary. The payment of carried interest is governed by a distribution waterfall, which dictates the order in which cash flows are distributed to investors and the GP.

The typical sequence in a waterfall is:
1. **Return of Capital:** All original capital contributed by investors is returned.
2. **Hurdle Rate (Preferred Return):** Investors receive a minimum annual return (e.g., 8-12%).
3. **Catch-up:** If the hurdle is met, the GP receives 100% of the next profits until their share equals the agreed-upon carry percentage (e.g., 20%) of the total profits distributed so far.
4. **Final Distribution:** Remaining profits are split according to the carry percentage (e.g., 80% to investors, 20% to the GP).

Important protective clauses often accompany carried interest:
- **Clawback Provision:** If early deals are profitable but later ones lose money, resulting in the overall fund missing its targets, the GP must return previously distributed carry to ensure LPs get their promised returns.
- **High Water Mark:** Ensures carry is not paid twice on the same gains.

In India, recent regulatory changes have clarified that carried interest is taxed as capital gains rather than business income and is not subject to GST, making the structure more favorable.

## Worked example

Suppose an investor commits ₹20 crores to a fund with a 12% hurdle rate and 20% carried interest. After five years, the fund exits with ₹40 crores total value.
- **Step 1:** The ₹20 crore initial capital is returned to investors. Remaining: ₹20 crores.
- **Step 2:** Investors receive their 12% compounded return, approximately ₹12.5 crores over 5 years. Remaining: ₹7.5 crores.
- **Step 3 (Carry):** The GP earns 20% of the remaining ₹7.5 crores, which equals ₹1.5 crores. Remaining: ₹6 crores.
- **Step 4:** The remaining ₹6 crores is split between investors based on ownership percentages.

## Interview-ready answer

Carried interest is the performance fee paid to a general partner, typically 20% of profits, earned only after limited partners receive their capital back plus a minimum hurdle rate. It aligns the GP's incentives with LPs, and its distribution is governed by the fund's waterfall model, often including clawback provisions to protect investors from aggregate underperformance.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Aggressive carry structures above 25% without a proven track record.
- Hurdle rates set too low (e.g., below 6%), rewarding the GP for average or poor performance.
- Lack of clawback provisions, allowing the GP to keep early profits even if the overall fund ultimately underperforms.
- Vague waterfall language or confusion between simple and compounded hurdle calculations.
