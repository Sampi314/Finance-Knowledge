# Gross potential rent

> **TL;DR.** Gross potential rent (GPR) is the total rental income that could be generated if each rental unit were hypothetically leased with a 100% occupancy rate and no issues related to credit losses.

## When you'd use this

You would use this concept when attempting to estimate the effective gross income (EGI) of an existing real estate property investment. It serves as the "ceiling" of total rental income that a property owner could obtain, which is necessary before deducting losses.

## The 30-second version

Gross potential rent (GPR), or potential gross income (PGI), represents the maximum potential rental income that a rental property could generate. It assumes a 100% occupancy rate (or 0% vacancy rate) and zero credit losses (no issues collecting rent from tenants). Calculating it is straightforward: multiply the total number of rental units by the gross rental rate. Since economic vacancy is measured on an annual basis, a monthly rental rate must be annualized by multiplying it by 12.

## The full explanation

The gross potential rent (GPR) acts as the baseline capacity for a property's revenue.

Because actual commercial real estate rarely achieves 100% occupancy with zero collection issues, calculating GPR is primarily a theoretical exercise to establish a property's revenue ceiling. From the GPR, property owners deduct the estimated costs attributable to vacancy and credit losses to arrive at a pro forma effective gross income (EGI), which is a more accurate representation of expected cash flows.

Economic vacancy itself is measured by finding the difference between this gross potential rent (GPR) and the actual rental income collected, expressed as a percentage of the GPR.

## Interview-ready answer

Gross potential rent (GPR) is the maximum potential rental income that a rental property could generate, assuming a 100% occupancy rate and no credit losses from tenants. To calculate it, you multiply the total number of rental units by the gross rental rate.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- GPR assumes a 100% occupancy rate and no credit losses, which is rarely achieved in practice.
- The gross rental rate is often expressed on a monthly basis, so it must be annualized to measure economic vacancy on an annual basis.
