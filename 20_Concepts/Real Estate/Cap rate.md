# Cap rate

> **TL;DR.** Cap rate is a measure of the expected rate of return on a real estate investment, such as a commercial rental property.

## When you'd use this

Commercial real estate investors use the cap rate to establish the relative attractiveness of a particular property to determine whether the asking price is overpriced, underpriced, or priced fairly. In practice, the cap rate serves as the primary shorthand whereby properties of comparable risk and return can be analyzed side-by-side.

## The 30-second version

The capitalization rate (cap rate) compares the net operating income (NOI) of a real estate property investment to its fair value to quantify the anticipated rate of return. It answers the fundamental question of: "For each dollar spent to acquire the property, what is the annual net operating income (NOI) expected to be generated?" A higher capitalization rate implies a higher potential return on investment (ROI) and more risk, whereas a lower cap rate coincides with a lower potential return but less risk.

## The full explanation

The cap rate is a fundamental measure of risk and return in the commercial real estate (CRE) market. By converting the stabilized NOI of a property into a percentage, the implied cap rate is a standardized metric that facilitates fair comparisons between different properties ("apples-to-apples").

The cap rate formula does not include mortgage payments or interest because of the logic that mortgage costs and interest expense charges are classified as financing costs (and, thus, not part of a property’s core operations). The cap rate neglects the effects of financing (i.e. capital structure neutral) since the numerator is net operating income (NOI), an unlevered profitability metric.

Cap rate expansion coincides with falling property values, while cap rate compression implies rising property values. The ideal cap rate is widely accepted as between 5% and 10% in the commercial real estate (CRE) market, but there are a broad number of factors, such as property type, classification, and asset class, that influence what a "good" cap rate is.

## Excel and modeling notes

When building a real estate pro forma forecast, it's critical to determine the effective gross income (EGI) by deducting vacancy and credit losses from potential gross income. Direct property expenses (such as property taxes, management fees, insurance, maintenance, and repairs) are then deducted from EGI to determine the net operating income (NOI). Performing a sensitivity analysis (or "what-if" analysis) using a matrix illustrates the relationship between net operating income (NOI) and purchase price, where a higher NOI and lower purchase price yield a higher cap rate.

## Interview-ready answer

The cap rate is the expected return on a rental property based on its income potential and implied risk. It is calculated by dividing a rental property's net operating income (NOI) by its market value as of the present date.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- The actual rental income can deviate from the projected income due to countless unforeseeable factors—both internal and external—such as unanticipated tenant vacancies and unfavorable market conditions (i.e. rising interest rates, economic recession).
- The scope of the implied cap rate is limited since the return reflects a pro-forma estimate of the property's income post-stabilization as of the date of analysis (Year 1).
