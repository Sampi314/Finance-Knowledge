# Real estate asset classes

> **TL;DR.** Real estate asset classes represent the different categories of properties—such as office, industrial, retail, and multifamily—each with distinct tenant types, lease structures, and risk-return profiles.

## When you'd use this

You would use this concept when analyzing potential real estate investments, building property pro-formas, or choosing a real estate private equity strategy. Understanding the nuances between asset classes is essential for determining the expected cash flows, lease terms, and operational complexities of a property.

## The 30-second version

The main categories of real estate asset classes are office, industrial, retail, and multifamily properties. Office, retail, and industrial properties typically lease to businesses with long-term, highly variable leases that include different rent structures and expense reimbursements. In contrast, multifamily properties lease to individuals on short-term leases (usually 1 year) with standardized terms. "Other" property types include hotels, storage, data centers, and healthcare facilities.

## The full explanation

Real estate is a tangible asset class rooted in real cash flows and combines financial analysis with on-the-ground knowledge. It offers elements of both Equities and Fixed Income, allowing for varied investment strategies. The core asset classes include:

- **Office, Industrial, and Retail:** These properties have businesses as tenants and typically offer long-term leases of 5-10 years. Their lease terms are highly variable and may include different rental rates, rent escalations, free months of rent, expense reimbursements, and tenant improvements. The pro-forma numbers for these properties tend to be "lumpier" because they have fewer tenants with more customized leases, leading to potentially long periods of downtime and significant concessions when new tenants move in.
- **Multifamily:** These properties have individuals as tenants and offer short-term leases (usually 1 year), with very similar terms across all tenants. This typically results in smoother, more predictable cash flows compared to commercial properties.
- **Other:** This broad category includes hotels, storage, data centers, healthcare facilities, and condominiums, which differ based on their specific tenant profiles and ownership structures.

Additionally, industrial properties can generally be built more quickly and cheaply than office and retail properties, which require more time and capital and tend to house more tenants.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Assuming cash flow patterns are similar across all asset classes, whereas office/retail cash flows are much "lumpier" than multifamily due to tenant turnover and concessions.
- Overlooking the complexity and length of commercial leases (office, retail, industrial) which often include tenant improvements and free rent periods.
