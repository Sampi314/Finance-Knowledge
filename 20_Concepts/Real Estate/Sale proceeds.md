# Sale proceeds

> **TL;DR.** Sale proceeds represent the capital generated when a business or real estate property is sold at the end of an investment holding period.

## When you'd use this

Sale proceeds are estimated during the exit modeling phase of financial analysis. This is essential when forecasting cash flows for leveraged buyouts (LBOs) or real estate distribution waterfalls to determine the final returns and distribution of capital to sponsors and investors.

## The 30-second version

Sale proceeds are the gross or net funds received from the sale of an asset, property, or business. In financial models, they often represent the lion's share of cash flow distributed to investors. The proceeds are typically estimated using an exit EBITDA multiple minus any transaction fees, and they are then distributed according to the structure of the investment, such as a partnership waterfall or a lender's equity kicker.

## The full explanation

When an investment like a leveraged buyout or a real estate property is realized, it is typically sold. The cash generated from this transaction constitutes the sale proceeds. In many financial modeling scenarios, estimating these proceeds is a critical step because the sale often provides the majority of the returns for investors.

In LBO modeling, estimating the sale proceeds involves applying an exit EBITDA multiple to the target's projected EBITDA in the final year of the holding period. Net sale proceeds are calculated by deducting any outstanding debt and transaction fees.

In real estate, sale proceeds can be split between general partners (GPs) and limited partners (LPs) via a distribution waterfall. Furthermore, lenders may occasionally secure a percentage of the sale proceeds through an equity kicker, which compensates them for offering debt financing at a reduced interest rate.

## Interview-ready answer

Sale proceeds are the funds obtained from exiting an investment. In modeling, we estimate net sale proceeds by applying an exit multiple to the final year's earnings (like EBITDA) and subtracting transaction fees and outstanding debt, which then flows to investors according to the capital structure or partnership waterfall.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Failing to account for transaction fees and outstanding debt when calculating net sale proceeds.
- Overestimating the exit multiple, which can artificially inflate the projected returns from the sale.
