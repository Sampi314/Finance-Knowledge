# Net operating income

> **TL;DR.** Net operating income (NOI) is a real estate profitability metric that calculates the total revenue generated by a property minus all reasonably necessary operating expenses, before deducting capital expenditures, debt service, or income taxes.

## When you'd use this

NOI is used extensively in commercial real estate underwriting and valuation. It is the primary metric used to determine the property's cap rate, estimate its fair market value, and assess its ability to generate cash flow for debt service and equity returns.

## The 30-second version

Net operating income is essentially the real estate equivalent of EBITDA. It measures the core operating profitability of a specific income-producing property, ignoring the owner's capital structure (how much debt they used) and tax rates. By stripping out interest and taxes, NOI provides an "apples-to-apples" comparison of operating performance across different properties or property classes, making it the foundational input for valuing commercial real estate via the capitalization rate.

## The full explanation

NOI is a standardized metric that serves as a proxy for cash flow. It starts with total revenue (largely rental income) and subtracts operating expenses.

When calculating NOI, expenses are generally categorized and treated as follows:
- **Non-controllable expenses:** Cash expenses like property taxes, utilities, and insurance are always deducted.
- **Controllable expenses:** Items like repairs, maintenance, and management fees are deducted, but often "normalized" to reflect market rates rather than the specific owner's potentially distorted figures.
- **Non-cash expenses:** Depreciation and amortization are not deducted. If starting from net income before taxes (NIBT), depreciation is added back.
- **Hypothetical expenses:** A "vacancy allowance" is usually deducted. Even if a property is fully occupied, underwriters apply a market-based vacancy rate to simulate potential downtime and calculate a normalized NOI.

NOI is crucial because it ignores capital structure. Two identical properties will have the same NOI even if one was bought with all cash and the other with a large mortgage, because interest expense is not factored into NOI.

## Excel and modeling notes

When building a real estate pro forma, NOI is a central line item. Analysts must be careful to exclude "below the line" items like tenant improvements, leasing commissions, capital expenditures, and debt service when arriving at the NOI figure.

## Interview-ready answer

Net operating income (NOI) is the primary profitability metric used in commercial real estate. It represents a property's total revenue minus all operating expenses, but before debt service, capital expenditures, and taxes. It's essentially the real estate equivalent of EBITDA and is used alongside the cap rate to determine a property's valuation.

## Pitfalls and gotchas

- Confusing NOI with cash flow: NOI does not account for capital expenditures, leasing commissions, or debt service, so it will differ from the actual cash flow available to equity investors.
- Using actual vs. normalized expenses: Owners might inflate or defer controllable expenses like maintenance or management fees. An accurate NOI calculation requires normalizing these to market rates.
- Ignoring vacancy: Using actual full occupancy without applying a market vacancy allowance will overstate the normalized, sustainable NOI of a property.
