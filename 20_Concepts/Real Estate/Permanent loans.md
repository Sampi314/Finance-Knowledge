# Permanent loans

> **TL;DR.** A permanent loan is a lower-risk, long-term financing instrument used to replace a riskier construction loan once a real estate property has stabilized.

## When you'd use this

You use a permanent loan during the stabilization phase of a real estate development or renovation deal. Once construction is complete and tenants begin to occupy the space, the property's value and cash flows are more predictable. At this point, developers refinance their initial, higher-interest construction loans with a permanent loan to lower their cost of capital and distribute excess cash to equity investors.

## The 30-second version

Construction loans are riskier and have higher interest rates, making them suitable only for the initial building phase. Once construction finishes and a property stabilizes (meaning it has tenants and steady Net Operating Income), it becomes eligible for a permanent loan. These loans attract different lenders whose risk tolerance matches stabilized properties. Refinancing into a permanent loan can also boost returns for equity investors if the property's value has increased, allowing them to extract cash based on the new, higher valuation.

## The full explanation

Permanent loans are a critical component in the lifecycle of real estate development and renovation deals. When a developer first acquires land and begins construction, they typically rely on a construction loan. Because the property is generating no income and faces significant completion risks, construction loans carry high interest rates, and interest is often capitalized during the construction period.

Once the property is built and leased out (stabilized), the risk profile drops dramatically. The property now generates regular cash flow (Net Operating Income, or NOI). This allows the owners to refinance the construction loan with a permanent loan.

To size the permanent loan, lenders look at the property's stabilized value—often determined by dividing its NOI by a Cap Rate—and apply a Loan-to-Value (LTV) ratio. The new permanent loan pays off the remaining construction loan. If the new loan amount is greater than the old debt, the excess proceeds are distributed to the equity investors, which can significantly boost their Internal Rate of Return (IRR) and Cash-on-Cash multiple.

## Pitfalls and gotchas
