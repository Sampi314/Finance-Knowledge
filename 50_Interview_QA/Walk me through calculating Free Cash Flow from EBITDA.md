# Walk me through calculating Free Cash Flow from EBITDA

## The question

Walk me through calculating Free Cash Flow from EBITDA.

## Short answer (30 seconds)

To calculate Free Cash Flow starting from EBITDA, you first subtract Cash Taxes. Then, you adjust for non-cash items like Deferred Taxes and the Change in Working Capital (adding it if it's a source of cash, subtracting if it's a use of cash). Finally, you subtract Capital Expenditures.

## Long answer (2 minutes)

EBITDA is often considered a proxy for cash flow, but it ignores critical items like taxes, working capital changes, and CapEx.

To get to Unlevered Free Cash Flow from EBITDA:
1. First, subtract Cash Taxes. You would estimate this based on Operating Income and the effective tax rate.
2. Next, adjust for the Change in Working Capital. If working capital increased, it's a use of cash, so you subtract it. If it decreased, it's a source of cash, so you add it.
3. Finally, subtract Capital Expenditures, which represent the cash needed to maintain and grow the business's asset base.

If you are asked to calculate *Levered* Free Cash Flow from EBITDA, you would perform the same steps but also subtract Net Interest Expense and any mandatory debt principal repayments.

## Common follow-ups

- **What if the company has significant deferred taxes?** Explain that you would add back the non-cash portion of the deferred income taxes, as the goal is to reflect actual cash taxes paid.
- **Why do we exclude stock-based compensation?** Stock-based compensation is a non-cash expense, but it is dilutive to equity holders, so analysts often treat it differently. Generally, it's best not to add it back if you want a true reflection of economic cash flow.
