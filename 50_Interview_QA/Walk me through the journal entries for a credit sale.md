# Walk me through the journal entries for a credit sale

## The question

Walk me through the journal entries when a company makes a $100 sale to a customer on credit (assuming no COGS/inventory for simplicity).

## Short answer (30 seconds)

When a company makes a sale on credit, it recognizes revenue but hasn't received the cash yet. You would debit Accounts Receivable for $100 to recognize the new asset, and credit Revenue for $100 to recognize the earned income.

## Long answer (2 minutes)

1. First, we identify the two accounts affected by the transaction. The company has earned revenue, so the Revenue account is affected. Because the customer has not paid in cash, the company has a claim to receive that cash in the future, which is recorded as Accounts Receivable (an asset account).
2. We apply the debit and credit rules. Accounts Receivable is an asset, and it is increasing. To increase an asset, we debit it. Therefore, we record a $100 debit to Accounts Receivable.
3. Revenue increases Retained Earnings (Equity). To increase an equity-like account, we credit it. Therefore, we record a $100 credit to Revenue.
4. Total debits ($100) equal total credits ($100), so the entry balances and the fundamental accounting equation is maintained.

## Common follow-ups

- **What happens when the customer finally pays the $100 in cash?** You debit Cash for $100 (increasing the cash asset) and credit Accounts Receivable for $100 (decreasing the A/R asset). Revenue is not touched again because it was already recognized.
