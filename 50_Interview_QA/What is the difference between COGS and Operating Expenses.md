# What is the difference between COGS and Operating Expenses

## The question

What is the difference between Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) and Operating Expenses (OpEx)?

## Short answer (30 seconds)

COGS represents the direct costs incurred to produce or acquire the goods a company sells, such as raw materials and direct labor. Operating Expenses are the indirect, day-to-day costs required to run the overall business, such as rent, salaries, and marketing. While COGS is subtracted from revenue to find gross profit, OpEx is subtracted from gross profit to find operating income.

## Long answer (2 minutes)

The primary difference between COGS and Operating Expenses lies in their direct relationship to revenue generation and the production of goods.

- **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS):** These are direct costs. They scale up or down depending on how many products the company produces or sells. If a company manufactures bicycles, the cost of the metal, rubber tires, and the wages of the assembly line workers fall under COGS. COGS determines the gross profit margin, which tells you how profitable the core product itself is.
- **Operating Expenses (OpEx):** These are indirect costs. They are the expenses necessary for the business to function but are not tied directly to creating the product. In the bicycle factory example, the salary of the CEO, the cost of advertising the bicycles, and the rent for the corporate office are all operating expenses. OpEx determines operating income (EBIT), which tells you how efficiently the overall company is being run after paying for its overhead.

In summary, COGS answers "How much does it cost to make our product?" while OpEx answers "How much does it cost to run our company?"

## Common follow-ups

- **Is depreciation included in COGS or OpEx?** It can be either. Depreciation on factory equipment used to produce goods is typically included in COGS. Depreciation on corporate office equipment or buildings is included in OpEx.
- **How are these forecasted differently in a model?** COGS is usually forecasted as a percentage of revenue because it is a variable cost tied directly to sales. OpEx is often forecasted differently; some items are fixed and grow with inflation, while others might be tied to headcount or revenue.
