# Walk me through a DCF

## The question

"Walk me through a DCF."

## Short answer (30 seconds)

A DCF values a company based on the present value of its future free cash flows. First, you project the company's unlevered free cash flows for 5 to 10 years. Then, you calculate the terminal value to capture the value of all cash flows beyond the forecast period. Finally, you discount both the projected cash flows and the terminal value back to their present value using the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) to arrive at the enterprise value.

## Long answer (2 minutes)

1. **Project Unlevered Free Cash Flows:** I would start by projecting the company's financials for a 5- to 10-year period to arrive at Unlevered Free Cash Flow. This involves taking EBIT, applying the tax rate to get NOPAT, adding back non-cash expenses like depreciation and amortization, subtracting capital expenditures, and subtracting changes in net working capital.
2. **Calculate the Terminal Value:** Since we can't forecast cash flows forever, I would then calculate the terminal value to capture the value of the business beyond the forecast period. I could do this using either the Gordon Growth Method, applying a long-term growth rate, or the Exit Multiple Method, applying an EV/EBITDA multiple to the final year's EBITDA.
3. **Determine the Discount Rate:** I need a discount rate to bring these future cash flows back to today's dollars. For an unlevered DCF, I would use the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC), which represents the blended cost of debt and equity based on the company's capital structure.
4. **Discount to Present Value:** Next, I discount all the projected free cash flows and the terminal value to their present value using WACC. Summing these present values gives me the company's Enterprise Value.
5. **Bridge to Equity Value (if asked):** If the question asks for equity value or a per-share price, I would take the Enterprise Value, add cash and cash equivalents, and subtract total debt, preferred stock, and minority interest to get Equity Value. Finally, I'd divide the Equity Value by the diluted shares outstanding to get the implied share price.

## Common follow-ups

- **How do you calculate Unlevered Free Cash Flow?** You start with EBIT, subtract taxes to get NOPAT (Net Operating Profit After Taxes), add back D&A, and subtract Capital Expenditures and changes in Net Working Capital.
- **Why do you use 5 to 10 years for the projection period?** That's typically how long it takes for a company to reach a "steady state" where its growth rate stabilizes, making it appropriate to apply a terminal value. Forecasting beyond 10 years becomes highly speculative.
- **What's the difference between Levered and Unlevered Free Cash Flow in a DCF?** Unlevered FCF excludes interest expense and mandatory debt repayments, meaning it's cash available to *all* investors (debt and equity). It's discounted at WACC to find Enterprise Value. Levered FCF includes interest expense and mandatory debt repayments, so it's cash available *only* to equity investors. It's discounted at the Cost of Equity to find Equity Value directly.
