# How do we measure the risk of equity investments?

## The question

How do we measure the risk of equity investments, and why is it more difficult than measuring the risk of debt?

## Short answer (30 seconds)

We measure the risk of equity investments by determining the risk added to a fully diversified portfolio, primarily capturing systematic (market) risk. This is often done using the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) and a stock's beta. It is more difficult than measuring debt risk because debt has an explicit, observable interest rate, while the cost of equity is implicit and based on the varying risk perceptions of equity investors.

## Long answer (2 minutes)

Measuring the risk of equity investments involves standardizing how risk is viewed, since individual investors can have wildly different perceptions of risk and differing required returns. To do this, finance theory focuses on the "marginal investor"—the investor with enough capital to influence the market price.

The fundamental assumption is that this marginal investor holds a broadly diversified portfolio. In a diversified portfolio, firm-specific risks cancel out, leaving only the risk that is tied to the broader market, which we call non-diversifiable or systematic risk.

To quantify this systematic risk, the most common framework is the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM). In CAPM, an asset's risk is encapsulated by its **Beta**, which measures how much the stock's price moves relative to the overall market.

This process is inherently more difficult than measuring the risk of debt for two primary reasons:
1.  **Implicit vs. Explicit Costs:** Debt comes with a stated interest rate and a clear schedule of payments, giving an explicit, observable cost. Equity has no guaranteed payouts or stated interest rate; its cost is purely implicit.
2.  **Risk in the Eyes of the Beholder:** Lenders can look at objective credit metrics (like interest coverage ratios) and credit ratings to assign a default spread. Equity risk relies on statistical estimates of past volatility and subjective estimates of the equity risk premium, leading to a wider variance in what investors believe the required return should be.

## Common follow-ups

- **What happens to equity risk if a company takes on more debt?** Taking on more debt increases financial leverage, which in turn increases the equity risk (and thus the cost of equity), even if the underlying business risk remains unchanged.
- **What if the company isn't publicly traded and doesn't have a beta?** For private companies, we typically use a "bottom-up" beta, calculated by taking the average unlevered beta of publicly traded comparable companies in the same industry, and then relevering it to reflect the private company's specific target debt-to-equity ratio.
