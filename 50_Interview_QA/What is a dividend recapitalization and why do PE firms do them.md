# What is a dividend recapitalization and why do PE firms do them?

## The question

What is a dividend recapitalization and why do PE firms do them?

## Short answer (30 seconds)

A dividend recapitalization is a transaction where a portfolio company borrows money specifically to pay a dividend to its equity holders, primarily the private equity sponsor. PE firms do this to accelerate returns by getting cash back earlier, which significantly improves IRR. It also de-risks the investment by allowing the sponsor to recover their initial equity while maintaining upside exposure.

## Long answer (2 minutes)

Private equity firms execute dividend recapitalizations for several strategic reasons:

1. **Accelerate Returns:** By receiving cash early in the holding period, the sponsor significantly improves their Internal Rate of Return (IRR) due to the time value of money, even if the ultimate exit value is somewhat reduced by the higher debt load.
2. **De-Risk the Investment:** It allows the sponsor to recover a substantial portion, or all, of their initial invested equity while still owning the company and maintaining exposure to future upside.
3. **Alternative to Exits:** It provides a mechanism to return capital to Limited Partners (LPs) when traditional exits through M&A or IPOs are challenging. This is especially useful during periods of extended holding periods and limited exit activity.

## Common follow-ups

- **How does a dividend recap affect LBO returns?** It primarily improves IRR by accelerating cash flows. While total Multiple of Invested Capital (MOIC) can sometimes increase, IRR is the main beneficiary. You must calculate IRR across all cash flows, including the mid-hold recap distribution and the final exit proceeds.
- **What are the risks?** The portfolio company bears the risk of increased debt, higher interest expense, reduced financial flexibility, and tighter covenant cushions. It also risks damaging lender relationships or facing rating agency downgrades.
- **How is capacity determined?** Capacity is determined by analyzing the company's debt capacity (cash flows and leverage metrics) and reviewing existing credit documentation for restricted payment covenants and incremental debt incurrence tests.
