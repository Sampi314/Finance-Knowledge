# What are the two methods for calculating terminal value?

## The question

What are the two methods for calculating terminal value?

## Short answer (30 seconds)

The two methods are the Gordon Growth Model (or perpetuity growth method) and the Exit Multiple Method. The Gordon Growth Model assumes the business continues operating forever, generating cash flows that grow at a constant perpetual rate. The Exit Multiple Method assumes the business is sold at the end of the projection period for a multiple of a financial metric, typically EBITDA.

## Long answer (2 minutes)

- **Gordon Growth Model:** This method is theoretically grounded in finance principles and calculates terminal value by treating the business as an ongoing enterprise with perpetually growing cash flows. The formula is $\text{FCF}_{n+1} / (\text{WACC} - g)$. It requires an explicit assumption about the long-term perpetual growth rate, which typically aligns with long-term GDP growth or inflation (e.g., 2% to 3%). It is best suited for stable, mature businesses and long-term holders.
- **Exit Multiple Method:** This method applies a market-based valuation multiple (such as EV/EBITDA) to a financial metric in the final projection year. It reflects actual transaction economics and is based on observable market data from comparable companies or precedent transactions. This approach is more practical for deal contexts like M&A advisory and LBO analysis.

Best practice is to calculate terminal value using both methods and compare the results to ensure your assumptions are internally consistent.

## Common follow-ups

- **Which terminal value method is better?** Neither is inherently better; they serve different purposes. Academics prefer Gordon Growth for theoretical rigor, while practitioners often prefer exit multiples for defensibility.
- **What is the implied growth rate?** When using exit multiples, you can calculate the implied perpetual growth rate embedded in the assumption to sanity-check whether the exit multiple implies realistic long-term economics.
