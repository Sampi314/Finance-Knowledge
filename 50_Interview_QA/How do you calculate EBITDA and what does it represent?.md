# How do you calculate EBITDA and what does it represent?

## The question

How do you calculate EBITDA and what does it represent?

## Short answer (30 seconds)

You calculate EBITDA by taking Operating Income (or EBIT) from the income statement and adding back the Depreciation and Amortization (D&A) expense found on the cash flow statement. EBITDA represents a company's core, recurring operating cash flow before accounting for its capital structure, taxes, and non-cash expenses like D&A. It is widely used to quickly compare the operational performance of companies and evaluate their ability to service debt.

## Long answer (2 minutes)

- **The calculation:** The most accurate way to calculate EBITDA is to start with Operating Income (EBIT) from the income statement and then add the total Depreciation and Amortization (D&A) from the cash flow statement.
- **Sourcing D&A:** It is critical to pull the D&A figure from the cash flow statement, not the income statement. On the income statement, D&A is often embedded within other line items like COGS or SG&A, so you might miss part of the expense if you rely on it there.
- **Adjusted EBITDA:** Often, you will further add back non-recurring charges (like legal settlements, restructuring costs, or one-time write-offs) to arrive at an "Adjusted EBITDA" that better reflects normalized operations.
- **What it represents:** EBITDA is a proxy for operating cash flow. It isolates the profit generated directly by a company's day-to-day operations by removing the impacts of how it is financed (interest), the tax jurisdiction it operates in (taxes), and its past capital investment decisions (depreciation and amortization).
- **Its limitations:** While widely used, EBITDA is not a true measure of cash flow because it ignores actual capital expenditures (CapEx) and changes in net working capital, both of which represent real cash outflows that a business needs to survive and grow.

## Common follow-ups

- **Why is stock-based compensation usually not added back?** Even though it is a non-cash expense, stock-based compensation is typically a recurring, necessary expense to retain employees and it dilutes the existing shareholder base. Standard practice generally dictates leaving it as an expense, though aggressive adjusted EBITDA calculations might try to add it back.
- **Why is EBITDA used as a proxy for cash flow if it ignores CapEx?** Because it is a quick, universal metric that allows for easy comparisons across companies with different capital structures and historical investments. For a true cash flow measure, investors use Free Cash Flow, which deducts CapEx.
