# Why is debt cheaper than equity

## The question

Why is debt considered a cheaper source of financing compared to equity?

## Short answer (30 seconds)

Debt is generally cheaper than equity for two main reasons. First, interest payments on debt are tax-deductible, creating an interest tax shield that lowers the effective cost of debt. Second, debt has a senior claim in the capital structure, meaning lenders take on less risk than equity investors and therefore require a lower rate of return.

## Long answer (2 minutes)

- **Tax Deductibility**: The cost of debt is almost always lower than the cost of equity because interest payments reduce a company's taxable income. This creates a tax shield. For example, if a company pays $100 million in interest and has a 25% tax rate, the effective after-tax cost is only $75 million. Dividends to equity holders provide no such tax benefit.
- **Seniority and Risk**: In the event of bankruptcy or liquidation, debt holders are paid before equity holders. Because equity investors are the residual claimants and face the highest risk of loss, they demand higher returns to compensate for this risk.
- **Hidden Costs of Debt**: It's important to mention that while the stated cost is lower, debt introduces mandatory fixed obligations (interest and principal payments), covenants, and bankruptcy risk, which indirectly constrain the company and increase overall risk as leverage grows.

## Common follow-ups

- **If debt is cheaper, why don't companies fund themselves with 100% debt?** You would explain the trade-off theory and how the costs of financial distress start to outweigh the tax benefits at higher leverage levels.
