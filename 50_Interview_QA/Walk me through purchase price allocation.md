# Walk me through purchase price allocation

## The question

Walk me through purchase price allocation.

## Short answer (30 seconds)

Purchase price allocation is the process of assigning the acquisition consideration to the assets acquired and liabilities assumed at fair value. You first identify and mark up tangible assets, then separately identify and value intangible assets, and record assumed liabilities at fair value. Any remaining consideration paid above the fair value of these net identifiable assets is recorded as goodwill.

## Long answer (2 minutes)

Purchase price allocation is the process of assigning acquisition consideration to the assets acquired and liabilities assumed at fair value.

1. First, I identify all **tangible assets** and adjust them from book value to fair value, with the most common adjustments involving PP&E and inventory.
2. Second, I identify and value **intangible assets** including customer relationships, technology, trademarks, and other separable or contractual assets.
3. Third, I record **assumed liabilities** at fair value, including adjustments for debt and deferred revenue.
4. Finally, any residual consideration after allocating to net identifiable assets becomes **goodwill**.

The allocation matters because it determines future depreciation and amortization expense, which directly impacts post-acquisition earnings.

## Common follow-ups

- **How does PPA affect the income statement?** Fair value step-ups on PP&E increase depreciation expense, while identified finite-lived intangibles create amortization expense over their useful lives. These non-cash charges reduce reported net income.
- **What's the difference between goodwill and other intangibles?** Goodwill is the residual value that cannot be separately identified (like assembled workforce or expected synergies) and is not amortized. Other intangibles are separately identifiable and most are amortized over their useful lives.
- **Why might goodwill be high relative to purchase price?** It typically indicates an asset-light target (like software or services) where value resides in people and growth potential, or that the acquirer paid a significant strategic premium.
