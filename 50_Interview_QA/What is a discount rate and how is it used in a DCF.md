# What is a discount rate and how is it used in a DCF

## The question

What is a discount rate and how is it used in a Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) analysis?

## Short answer (30 seconds)

A discount rate is the interest rate used to translate future cash flows into their present value, reflecting the time value of money and the risk of the investment. In an unlevered DCF, the discount rate is the company's Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC), which represents the blended minimum return required by all capital providers. We use it to discount the projected free cash flows and the terminal value back to today to determine the company's Enterprise Value.

## Long answer (2 minutes)

- **Definition:** The discount rate is the rate used to calculate the present value of future cash flows. It accounts for two things: the time value of money (a dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow because it can be invested) and risk (riskier cash flows require a higher potential return to compensate investors).
- **Application in a DCF:** In a standard unlevered DCF, you project out the company's unlevered free cash flows for 5 to 10 years and then calculate a terminal value representing all cash flows beyond that period.
- **Why we use WACC:** Because unlevered free cash flows represent the cash available to *all* investors (both debt and equity holders), the discount rate applied must reflect the blended cost of financing from all those capital sources. This blended rate is the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC).
- **The calculation:** You discount each projected cash flow and the terminal value back to the present day using the WACC. The sum of these present values gives you the Enterprise Value of the company.

## Common follow-ups

- **What happens to the DCF valuation if the discount rate increases?**
  - As the discount rate increases, the present value of the future cash flows decreases, which leads to a lower Enterprise Value. This makes sense conceptually: if investors require a higher return (due to higher interest rates or perceived risk), they are willing to pay less today for the same future cash flows.
- **Why do we use WACC instead of the cost of equity for an unlevered DCF?**
  - Unlevered free cash flow is pre-debt (it represents cash available before paying interest or principal). Because it belongs to both debt and equity investors, it must be discounted by a rate that blends the cost of both debt and equity. Cost of equity is only used as the discount rate in a *levered* DCF, where the cash flows represent money available solely to equity holders.
