# What happens if Depreciation increases by $10?

## The question

"What happens if Depreciation increases by **$10**?"

## Short answer (30 seconds)

On the Income Statement, pre-tax income falls by $10, reducing Net Income by $6 (assuming a 40% tax rate). On the Cash Flow Statement, Net Income is down $6, but the $10 non-cash depreciation is added back, so Cash Flow from Operations increases by $4. On the Balance Sheet, Cash is up $4, PP&E is down $10, meaning Assets decrease by $6, which balances with the $6 decrease in Retained Earnings from the lower Net Income.

## Long answer (2 minutes)

"If Depreciation increases by **$10**:

**Income Statement:**
*   Operating expenses increase by **$10**
*   Pre-tax income decreases by **$10**
*   Assuming a 40% tax rate, Net Income decreases by **$6** (**$10** x 60%)

**Cash Flow Statement:**
*   Net Income is **$6** lower (starting point)
*   But Depreciation is a non-cash charge, so we add back the full **$10**
*   Net impact on Cash Flow from Operations: positive **$4** (**$10** added back minus **$6** lower Net Income)

**Balance Sheet:**
*   Cash increases by **$4**
*   PP&E decreases by **$10** (due to accumulated depreciation)
*   Total Assets decrease by **$6**
*   On the other side, Retained Earnings decreases by **$6** (due to lower Net Income)
*   Assets (**-$6**) = Liabilities (**$0**) + Equity (**-$6**). It balances."

## Common follow-ups

- "What if the tax rate is 0%?" - The cash flow impact is zero. Net Income goes down by $10, you add back $10, so cash is unchanged. Assets are down $10 (PP&E) and Equity is down $10 (Retained Earnings).
- "Does depreciation affect cash flow?" - Not directly, but it shields income from taxes, which saves cash, as seen in the positive cash flow impact when taxes are considered.
