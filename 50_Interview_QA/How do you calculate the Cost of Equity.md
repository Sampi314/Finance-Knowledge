# How do you calculate the Cost of Equity

## The question

How do you calculate the Cost of Equity?

## Short answer (30 seconds)

The most common way to calculate the cost of equity is by using the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM). The formula is the Risk-Free Rate plus Beta multiplied by the Equity Risk Premium. Alternatively, for mature companies that pay dividends, you can use the Dividend Discount Model.

## Long answer (2 minutes)

The Cost of Equity represents the rate of return an equity investor expects for holding a company's stock, compensating them for the risk. Since equity risk is unobservable directly, we use models to estimate it.

The primary approach is the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM), which involves three steps:
1. **Find the Risk-Free Rate:** You start with the yield of a default-free government bond, like the 10-year US Treasury note.
2. **Determine Beta:** This measures the volatility of the stock relative to the overall market. A Beta of 1 means it moves with the market, >1 means it is riskier, and <1 means it is less risky.
3. **Calculate the Equity Risk Premium (ERP):** This is the expected return of the stock market as a whole minus the risk-free rate. You multiply this ERP by the company's Beta and add it to the Risk-Free Rate.

Another method is the Dividend Capitalization Model (or Dividend Discount Model). This approach adds the dividend yield (next year's expected dividend divided by the current stock price) to the expected long-term growth rate of those dividends. However, it's less commonly used because it only applies to dividend-paying companies and doesn't explicitly factor in market risk.

## Common follow-ups

- **How do you find Beta for a private company?** You find comparable publicly traded companies, "unlever" their Betas to remove the effect of their specific debt levels, find the median unlevered Beta, and then "relever" it based on the private company's target capital structure.
- **Why is the Cost of Equity higher than the Cost of Debt?** Because equity investors take on more risk. Debt holders are guaranteed interest payments and are higher up in the capital structure, meaning they get paid first in the event of bankruptcy. Equity holders get whatever is left over, so they demand a higher potential return.
