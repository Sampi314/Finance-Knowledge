Cash Flow from Operations
=========================

The amount of cash a company generates from its operating activities

What is Cash Flow from Operations?
----------------------------------

Cash flow from operations is the section of a company’s [cash flow statement](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/statement-of-cash-flows/)
 that represents the amount of cash a company generates (or consumes) from carrying out its operating activities over a period of time. Operating activities include generating [revenue](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/revenue/)
, paying expenses, and funding working capital. It is calculated by taking a company’s (1) [net income](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/what-is-net-income/)
, (2) adjusting for non-cash items, and (3) accounting for changes in working capital.

![Cash Flow from Operations - Net Income, Non-Cash Items, Changes in Working Capital](https://cdn.corporatefinanceinstitute.com/assets/cash-flow-from-operations-diagram.png)

### Cash Flow from Operations Formula

While the exact formula will be different for every company (depending on the items they have on their income statement and balance sheet), there is a generic cash flow from operations formula that can be used:

#### **Cash Flow from Operations = Net Income + Non-Cash Items + Changes in Working Capital**

Learn more with detailed examples in CFI’s [Financial Analysis Course](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/course/financial-analysis-fundamentals/)
.

### Cash Flow from Operations Example

Below is an example of [Amazon’s operating cash flow from 2015 to 2017](https://ir.aboutamazon.com/annual-reports-proxies-and-shareholder-letters/default.aspx)
. As you can see in the screenshot below, the statement starts with net income, then adds back any non-cash items, and accounts for changes in [working capital](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/valuation/what-is-net-working-capital/)
.

Follow these three steps:

1.  Take net income from the income statement
2.  Add back non-cash expenses
3.  Adjust for changes in working capital

![Example of Amazon’s Operating Cash Flow - How to Calculate Cash Flow from Operations](https://cdn.corporatefinanceinstitute.com/assets/cash-flow-from-operations-sections.png)

### Cash Flow from Operations vs Net Income

[Operating cash flow](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/operating-cash-flow/)
 is calculated by starting with net income, which comes from the bottom of the income statement. Since the income statement uses [accrual-based accounting](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/accrual-principle/)
, it includes expenses that may not have actually been paid for yet. Thus, net income has to be adjusted by adding back all non-cash expenses like depreciation, stock-based compensation, and others.

Once net income is adjusted for all non-cash expenses it must also be adjusted for changes in working capital balances. Since accountants [recognize revenue](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/revenue-recognition/)
 based on when a product or service is delivered (and not when it’s actually paid), some of the revenue may be unpaid and thus will create an [accounts receivable](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/what-is-accounts-receivable/)
 balance. The same is true for expenses that have been accrued on the income statement, but not actually paid.

### Sample Calculation

Let’s look at a simple example together from CFI’s [Financial Modeling Course](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/collections/financial-modeling/)
.

![Cash Flow From Operations Model in Excel](https://cdn.corporatefinanceinstitute.com/assets/cash-flow-from-operations-model.png)

**Step 1**: Start calculating operating cash flow by taking net income from the [income statement](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/income-statement/)
.

**Step 2**: Add back all non-cash items. In this case, [depreciation](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/what-is-depreciation-expense/)
 and amortization is the only item.

**Step 3**: Adjust for changes in working capital. In this case, there is only one line because the model has a separate section below that calculates the changes in accounts receivable, [inventory](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/inventory/)
, and accounts payable.

### Cash Flow from Operations vs EBITDA

Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization ([EBITDA](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/valuation/what-is-ebitda/)
) is one of the most heavily quoted metrics in finance. [Financial Analysts](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/certifications/financial-modeling-valuation-analyst-fmva-program/)
 regularly use it when comparing companies using the ubiquitous [EV/EBITDA](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/valuation/ev-ebitda/)
 ratio. Since EBITDA doesn’t include depreciation expense, it’s sometimes considered a proxy for cash flow.

Since EBITDA excludes interest and taxes, it can be very different from operating cash flow. Additionally, the impact of changes in working capital and other [non-cash expenses](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/non-cash-expenses-and-adjustments/)
 can make it even more different.

Learn more in CFI’s [Business Modeling Courses](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/collections/)
.

### Capital Expenditures

While operating cash flow tells us how much cash a business generates from its operations, it does not take into account any capital investments that are required to sustain or grow the business.

To get a complete picture of a company’s financial position, it is important to take into account [capital expenditures](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/capital-expenditures/)
 (CapEx), which can be found under [Cash Flow from Investing Activities](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/cash-flow-from-investing-activities/)
.

Deducting capital expenditures from cash flow from operations gives us [Free Cash Flow](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/valuation/what-is-free-cash-flow-fcf/)
, which is often used to value a business in a [discounted cash flow (DCF) model](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/financial-modeling/dcf-model-training-free-guide/)
.
