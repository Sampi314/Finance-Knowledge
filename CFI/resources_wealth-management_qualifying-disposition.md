Qualifying Disposition
======================

The sale or other disposition of stock shares that receive a favorable tax treatment for the individual disposing of the stock

Written by [CFI Team](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/about-cfi/)

Published December 27, 2020

Read Time 3 minutes

What is a Qualifying Disposition?
---------------------------------

Qualifying disposition is a tax term used in the U.S. that refers to a sale or other disposition of shares that receive favorable tax treatment for the individual disposal of the stock.

Qualifying dispositions are important for stockholders because there may be a wide disparity between the stockholder’s regular income tax rate and the significantly lower long-term [capital gains tax rate](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/wealth-management/capital-gains-tax/)
. Therefore, qualifying dispositions can save stockholders a substantial amount of money in relation to taxes owed.

![Qualifying Disposition](https://cdn.corporatefinanceinstitute.com/assets/qualifying-disposition.png)

The qualifying disposition tax rules most commonly apply to stock that individuals acquire by virtue of being employed by the company issuing the stock. They may acquire stock shares through an employee stock purchase plan (known as an ESPP) or incentive stock option plans (referred to as ISOs).

ISOs are typically stock purchase options offered to employees who occupy an upper-level executive management position, such as the Chief Executive Officer (CEO), Chief Financial Officer (CFO), or Sales Manager.

### Summary

*   **Qualifying disposition is a tax term used in the U.S. that refers to a sale or other disposition of shares that receives favorable tax treatment.**
*   **The qualifying disposition rules are important because of the wide disparity between an individual’s marginal tax rate and the significantly lower capital gains tax rate.**
*   **The determining factor for qualifying dispositions is the length of time an individual holds onto stock shares before selling them.**

### How It Affects Your Taxes – Example

Assume that you acquire 100 shares of your company’s stock through an ESPP that enables you to buy shares at a 10% discount to their current [market price](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/valuation/market-price/)
 of $20 per share, so you only need to pay $18 per share for the stock. Your total purchase price for 100 shares is $1,800.

Further, assume that you can sell your stock shares for a price of $35 a share a few years later. Your total proceeds from the sale of your shares are $3,500. $3,500 minus your $1,800 purchase price gives you a tidy gross profit of $1,700.

Here is how the tax treatment of your stock profits would look if the sale of your shares is a qualifying disposition:

*   You would be taxed at your regular income tax rate on the $2 per share gain you realized by being able to purchase the shares at a discounted price of $18 per share (versus a market price of $20); assuming that you are in the 35% tax bracket, that [tax liability](https://www.businessinsider.com/personal-finance/what-is-tax-liability)
     would come out to $70 ($2 per share, times 100 shares, multiplied by 35%).
*   The additional gain you realized upon the sale of your shares is $15 per share – the difference between your sale price of $35 per share and the non-discounted market price of $20 per share that existed when you bought the stock; that total gain of $1,500 would only be taxed at the much lower capital gains tax rate of 15%, making your tax liability $225 ($15 per share, times 100 shares, multiplied by 15%).
*   Your total tax liability would equal $295 ($70 + $225 = $295).

In contrast, if the sale of your stock shares was not a qualifying disposition, then you would be taxed at the 35% income tax rate on the whole [profit](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/profit-vs-cash/)
 realized from your stock – $1700. It would make your tax liability on your stock profits $595 ($1,700 multiplied by 35%) – nearly twice as much as your tax liability with a qualifying disposition.

### What Qualifies as a Qualifying Disposition?

Since it can obviously make a big difference for you in terms of your tax liability, it is important to know the rules that govern whether the sale of your stock shares meets the requirements for being a qualifying disposition. Two basic rules that determine qualifying dispositions:

1.  The date of the stock sale must be at least one year or more from the date the stock was purchased.
2.  The date of the stock sale must be at least two years or more from the date when the option to purchase the stock was granted (when companies offer stock purchase options to employees, the offer period typically extends for quite some time).

Thus, your company may have originally granted you the option to purchase shares at the 10% discount in May 2004, but you did not actually purchase your shares under the terms of the offer until June 2005).

The above conditions must be met for the sale of your stock shares to be deemed a qualifying disposition.
