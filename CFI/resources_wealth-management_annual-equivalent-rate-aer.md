Annual Equivalent Rate (AER)
============================

The rate of interest after taking into account the effects of compounding to normalize the interest rate

What is the Annual Equivalent Rate (AER)?
-----------------------------------------

The Annual Equivalent Rate (AER) is the rate of interest after taking into account the effects of compounding to normalize the [interest rate](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/commercial-lending/interest-rate/)
. The AER is the actual interest rate an investment, loan, or savings account will yield after accounting for compounding.

![Annual Equivalent Rate](https://cdn.corporatefinanceinstitute.com/assets/annual-equivalent-rate-aer.jpeg)

### **Summary**

*   **The Annual Equivalent Rate (AER) is the real rate of interest because it accounts for the effects of compounding.**
*   **It is an important tool for evaluating bonds, loans, or accounts to understand the real return on investment (ROI) or interest rate.**
*   **The AER will always be higher than the nominal, or the stated rate, when compounding is present.**

### How AER is Used

The annual equivalent rate is used to compare the interest rates between loans or investments with different compounding periods, such as weekly, monthly, half-yearly, or yearly. Therefore, it can be used by both an individual looking for the best [savings account](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/wealth-management/savings-account/)
 or an investor comparing bond yields.

### Significance of the AER

The AER is crucial in finding the true [return on investment (ROI)](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/what-is-return-on-investment-roi/)
 from interest-bearing assets. The nominal rate, or the stated rate, can be materially different than the AER due to the effects of compounding. It means that the AER is always higher than the nominal rate when considering compounding.

### Annual Equivalent Rate vs. Nominal Interest – Example

For example, let’s say Bond A offers a semi-annual [coupon rate](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/fixed-income/coupon-rate/)
 of 3%. The nominal rate of the bond is 6% since it is two 3% coupons. However, the AER of the bond will be higher given that interest is paid out two times a year. Therefore, the AER of the bond will be calculated as:

AER = (1+ (0.06 / 2 )^2)) – 1 = **6.09%**

Bond B, on the other hand, offers a [quarterly](https://www.bankrate.com/banking/savings/compound-savings-calculator/)
 coupon rate of 1.5%. The nominal rate of the bond is still 6%. However, the AER will be even higher, as the coupons are paid out four times a year. Therefore, the AER of the bond will be:

AER = (1+ (0.06/4)^4)) – 1 = **6.14%**

After analyzing the AER of the two bond options, a rational investor will select Bond B, assuming all else equal, even though both bonds are the same from face value.
