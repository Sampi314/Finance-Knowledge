What is Liquidity?
------------------

In [financial markets](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/career-map/sell-side/capital-markets/financial-markets/)
, liquidity refers to how quickly an investment can be sold without negatively impacting its price. The more liquid an investment is, the more quickly it can be sold (and vice versa), and the easier it is to sell it for fair value or current market value. All else being equal, more liquid assets trade at a [premium](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/equities/liquidity-premium/)
 and illiquid assets trade at a [discount](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/valuation/discount-rate/)
.

In accounting and financial analysis, a company’s liquidity is a measure of how easily it can meet its [short-term financial obligations](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/current-liabilities/)
.

![Liquidity illustration - Market Liquidity and Financial Liquidity](https://cdn.corporatefinanceinstitute.com/assets/Liquidiy-Diagram.png)

### Ranking of Market Liquidity (Example)

Below is an example of how many common investments are typically ranked in terms how quickly and easily they can be turned into cash (of course, the order may be different depending on the circumstances).

Liquidity rankings:

1.  Cash
2.  Foreign Currency ([FX](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/foreign-exchange/foreign-exchange/)
    )
3.  Guaranteed Investment Certificates (GICs)
4.  Government Bonds
5.  Corporate Bonds
6.  [Stocks](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/equities/common-stock/)
     (publicly traded)
7.  Commodities (physical)
8.  [Real Estate](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/commercial-real-estate/real-estate/)
    
9.  Art
10.  [Private Businesses](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/valuation/private-company-valuation/)
    

As you can see in the list above, cash is, by default, the most liquid asset since it doesn’t need to be sold or converted (it’s already cash!). Stocks and [bonds](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/fixed-income/bonds/)
 can typically be converted to cash in about 1-2 days, depending on the size of the investment. Finally, slower-to-sell investments such as real estate, art, and private businesses may take much longer to convert to cash (often months or even years).

### Financial Liquidity

Items on a company’s balance sheet are typically listed from the most to the least liquid. Therefore, cash is always listed at the top of the asset section, while other types of assets, such as [Property, Plant & Equipment (PP&E),](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/ppe-property-plant-equipment/)
 are listed last.

In finance and accounting, the concept of a company’s liquidity is its ability to meet its financial obligations. The most common measures of liquidity are:

*   [Current Ratio](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/current-ratio-formula/)
     – Current assets minus current liabilities
*   [Quick Ratio](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/quick-ratio-definition/)
     – The ratio of only the most liquid assets (cash, accounts receivable, etc.) compared to current liabilities
*   [Cash Ratio](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/cash-ratio-formula/)
     – Cash on hand relative to current liabilities

### Liquidity Example (Balance Sheet)

Below is a screenshot of Amazon’s 2017 [balance sheet](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/balance-sheet/)
, which displays its assets and liabilities in order of their liquidity, as well as its [stockholders’ equity](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/stockholders-equity-guide/)
.

![Liquidity Example - Amazon's (Balance Sheet)](https://cdn.corporatefinanceinstitute.com/assets/Liquidity-Amazon-Example.png)

As you can see in the image, Amazon’s assets are separated into two categories, current assets and non-current assets (everything else).

**Current assets are as follows:**

*   Cash
*   [Marketable securities](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/marketable-securities/)
     (These would include publicly traded stocks, bonds, and other investments)
*   [Inventories](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/inventory/)
     (Products, finished goods, raw materials, etc. that can be sold)
*   [Accounts receivable](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/what-is-accounts-receivable/)
     (Cash owed from sales to customers on credit)

For most companies, these are four of the most common current assets. Their liquidity, however, can vary. For many companies, accounts receivable is more liquid than inventories (meaning the company expects to receive payment from customers faster than it takes to sell products in inventory).

**Current liabilities are listed as follows:**

*   [Accounts payable](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/what-is-accounts-payable/)
     (money owed to vender and suppliers)
*   [Accrued expenses](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/accrued-expense/)
     and other (money the company expects to owe to vendors and suppliers in the near future)
*   Unearned revenue (also called [deferred revenue](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/deferred-revenue/)
    )
