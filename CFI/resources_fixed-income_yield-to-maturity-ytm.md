Yield to Maturity (YTM)
=======================

The speculative rate of return or interest rate of a fixed-rate security

Written by [CFI Team](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/about-cfi/)

Published January 23, 2020

Read Time 3 minutes

What is the Yield to Maturity (YTM)?
------------------------------------

Yield to Maturity (YTM) – otherwise referred to as redemption or book [yield](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/fixed-income/yield-definition-formula/)
 – is the speculative [rate of return](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/valuation/rate-of-return-guide/)
 or interest rate of a fixed-rate security, such as a [bond](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/fixed-income/bonds/)
. The YTM is based on the belief or understanding that an investor purchases the security at the current market price and holds it until the security has matured (reached its full value), and that all interest and coupon payments are made in a timely fashion.

![Yield to Maturity (YTM) Theme - Financial Graph](https://cdn.corporatefinanceinstitute.com/assets/Yield-to-Maturity-1024x683.jpeg)

How to Calculate YTM
--------------------

YTM is typically expressed as an [annual percentage rate (APR)](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/wealth-management/annual-percentage-rate-apr/)
. It is determined through the use of the following formula:

![Yield to Maturity Formula](https://cdn.corporatefinanceinstitute.com/assets/Yield-to-Maturity-Formula-1024x476.png)

Where:

*   C – Interest/coupon payment
*   FV – [Face value](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/par-value-overview/)
     of the security
*   PV – Present value/price of the security
*   t – How many years it takes the security to reach maturity

The formula’s purpose is to determine the yield of a bond (or other fixed-asset security) according to its most recent market price. The YTM calculation is structured to show – based on compounding – the effective yield a security should have once it reaches maturity. It is different from simple yield, which determines the yield a security should have upon maturity, but is based on dividends and not [compounded interest](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/wealth-management/compound-interest/)
.

### Approximated YTM

It’s important to understand that the formula above is only useful for an approximated YTM. In order to calculate the true YTM, an analyst or investor must use the trial-and-error method. This is done by using a variety of rates that are substituted into the current value slot of the formula. The true YTM is determined once the price matches that of the security’s actual current market price.

Alternatively, this process can be sped up by using the [SOLVER function](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/excel/excel-solver/)
 in Excel, which determines a value based on user-defined conditions. This means that an analyst can set the present value (price) of the security and solve for the YTM, which acts as the interest rate for the PV calculation.

Example of a YTM Calculation
----------------------------

To get a better understanding of the YTM formula and how it works, let’s look at an example.

Assume that there is a bond on the market priced at $850 and that the bond comes with a face value of $1,000 (a fairly common face value for bonds). On this bond, yearly coupons are $150. The [coupon rate](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/fixed-income/coupon-rate/)
 for the bond is 15% and the bond will reach maturity in 7 years.

Importance of Yield to Maturity
-------------------------------

The primary importance of yield to maturity is the fact that it enables investors to draw comparisons between different securities and the returns they can expect from each. It is critical for determining which securities to add to their portfolios.

Yield to maturity is also useful as it also allows the investors to gain some understanding of how changes in [market conditions](http://www.marketwatch.com/market-data)
 might affect their portfolio because when securities drop in price, yields rise, and vice versa.
