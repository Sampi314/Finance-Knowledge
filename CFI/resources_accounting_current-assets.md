What are Current Assets?
------------------------

Current assets are all assets that a company expects to convert to cash within one year. They are commonly used to measure the liquidity of a company. A company’s assets on its [balance sheet](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/balance-sheet/)
 are split into two categories – current and non-current (long-term or capital assets).

![Current Assets - Keyboard button showing Current Assets](https://cdn.corporatefinanceinstitute.com/assets/current-assets4.jpeg)

### **Current (Short-term) vs. Non-Current (Long-term Assets)**

Examples include:

*   Cash and cash equivalents
*   [Accounts receivable](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/what-is-accounts-receivable/)
    
*   Inventory
*   Short-term investments
*   Notes receivable
*   Prepaid expenses (e.g., insurance premiums that have not yet expired)
*   Marketable securities

On the other hand, long-term assets (also known as capital assets) take longer to, and are more difficult to, convert into cash. Examples include:

*   Property, plant, and equipment
*   Long-term investments
*   [Intangible assets](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/intangible-assets/)
     (trademarks, patents, goodwill)
*   Deferred charges

### Listing Assets on a Balance Sheet

For example, consider the balance sheet of Walmart for the period ending January 31, 2017:

![Current Assets - Example of Listing Current Assets on a Balance Sheet](https://cdn.corporatefinanceinstitute.com/assets/current-assets.png)

Note that the assets are clearly listed in order of liquidity. Cash and cash equivalents are the most liquid, followed by short-term investments, etc. The total current assets for [Walmart](https://www.walmart.com/)
 for the period ending January 31, 2017, is simply the addition of all the relevant assets ($57,689,000).

![Total Current Assets](https://cdn.corporatefinanceinstitute.com/assets/current-assets5.png)

### Important Ratios That Use Current Assets

Below is a list of useful liquidity ratios:

The **Cash Ratio** is a liquidity ratio used to measure a company’s ability to meet short-term liabilities. The cash ratio is a conservative debt ratio since it only uses cash and cash equivalents. This ratio shows the company’s ability to repay current liabilities without having to sell or liquidate other assets.

![Cash Ratio Formula](https://cdn.corporatefinanceinstitute.com/assets/current-assets1.png)

The **Quick Ratio**, also known as the acid-test ratio, is a liquidity ratio used to measure a company’s ability to meet short-term financial liabilities. The quick ratio uses assets that can be reasonably converted to cash within 90 days.

![Quick Ratio Formula](https://cdn.corporatefinanceinstitute.com/assets/current-assets2.png)

The **Current Ratio** is a liquidity ratio used to measure a company’s ability to meet short-term and long-term financial liabilities. The current ratio uses all of the company’s immediate assets in the calculation.

![Current Ratio Formula](https://cdn.corporatefinanceinstitute.com/assets/current-assets3.png)

It is important to note that the current ratio can overstate liquidity. This is because the current ratio uses inventory, which may or may not be easily converted to cash within a year (this is the case for many retailers and other inventory-intensive businesses).
