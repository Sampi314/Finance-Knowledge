Mining and Critical Minerals Course Overview
============================================

By Haydn Palliser | December 20, 2024

### Course Overview

This video is an overview of our Mining and Critical Minerals online financial modeling course.

Understand how financiers and investors assess mining deals, including mining specific debt metrics such as the Reserve Tail Ratio (RTR) and the Reserve Life Coverage Ratio (RLCR).

### Video

**Video Transcript** 

**Haydn:** If you want to learn how to build and analyze financial models for the mining and critical minerals industry, you’ve come to the right place.

This Pivotal180 course is what we call an industry course. We consider it an add-on to our other project finance modeling courses. It’s geared towards people who can already build a project finance model and want to focus on the specifics related to the mining industry in a simple and practical manner.

You will learn how to model mining operations with low and high grade ore mining, strip ratios, and calculations of the average mined copper grade. Processing calculations based on a limited plant capacity with a ramp up, stockpiles, and processing recoveries. Revenue calculations for sales of the product at market or spot prices with hedging via forward contracts. You’ll model operating costs for mining, processing, transportation, and rehabilitation fixed and variable costs. You’ll also review the cashflow waterfall, debt sizing with cash sweeps or cash sharing, and debt ratios, such as the debt service coverage ratio, or DSCR, the loan life coverage ratio, or LLCR, the project life coverage ratio, or PLCR, and finally, the reserve life coverage ratio, or RLCR. You’ll also look at what we call pre-operational cash flows and their impact on funding during the construction period among many other topics.

Note that for this industry course, we only do the coding calculations related to the mine’s operations. We will complete relevant operational calculations and also run sensitivities so you can clearly see and review the impact of changes in assumptions on outputs.
