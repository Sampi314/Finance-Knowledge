Quick Ratio: Calculations, Examples, and Meaning

  
The **Quick Ratio**, commonly known as the “acid-test” ratio, is normally defined as (Cash & Cash-Equivalents + Accounts Receivable) / Current Liabilities, and it captures a company’s ability to service its short-term obligations using its _most-liquid_ assets.

The Quick Ratio is an example [Liquidity Ratio](https://breakingintowallstreet.com/kb/financial-statement-analysis/liquidity-ratios/)
; like the others, it measures the company’s operational and credit risk.

It is **more stringent** than the [Current Ratio](https://breakingintowallstreet.com/kb/financial-statement-analysis/current-ratio/)
, so it is more useful for “stress testing” the company, as it excludes short-term assets that are more difficult to convert into Cash, such as Inventory.

What is the Quick Ratio?
------------------------

**Quick Ratio Definition:** The Quick Ratio equals a company’s (Cash & Cash-Equivalents + Accounts Receivable) divided by its Current Liabilities. It indicates whether a company can adequately meet its short-term funding obligations using its _most-liquid_ assets. Higher ratios are generally better, but if the ratio is _too high_, it could indicate that the company is mismanaging its Cash.

![Quick Ratio Formula & Meaning](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%202433%20517'%3E%3C/svg%3E)

As with the other Liquidity Ratios, the Quick Ratio is important mostly **in context**.

For example, has the company’s Quick Ratio declined significantly in the most recent few quarters? How does it compare to its historical levels and [comparable companies](https://breakingintowallstreet.com/kb/valuation/comparable-company-analysis-cca/)
 in the industry?

If the Quick Ratio was elevated for a few years, is there a specific reason why? Is the company actually _better off_ with a slightly lower Quick Ratio because it is using some of its Cash to re-invest in its business?

You can see an example of the calculation for Illinois Tool Works \[ITW\] below (see the Excel file and filing PDF above):

![Liquidity Ratios - Quick Ratio](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%20586%20537'%3E%3C/svg%3E "Liquidity Ratios - Quick Ratio")

The Quick Ratio here is 0.87x, which means that ITW has $0.87 of Cash + AR for each $1.00 in current liabilities.

This means that ITW could _not_ afford to repay all its obligations using its Cash + Owed Payments from Customers.

Some people would call that a negative sign, but, again, **context** is king here.

It’s only negative if the company ever enters such a stressed situation that an immediate repayment becomes necessary; if not, it means nothing.

The bigger issue is that it seems like **the company’s liquidity has deteriorated over the past few years**:

![Liquidity Ratios for Illinois Tool Works](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%20716%20751'%3E%3C/svg%3E "Liquidity Ratios for Illinois Tool Works")

This is concerning because it suggests that ITW’s Cash balance has been falling, while its Short-Term Debt has been rising.

On the other hand, even with these additional numbers, we still don’t have **the full context**.

For example, many companies raised additional Debt and Equity during the COVID-19 pandemic to boost their Cash reserves.

If Illinois Tool Works did this, its FY 20 numbers may be artificially inflated – and its FY 22 numbers may simply be a “return to normal.”

To determine this, we’d need to go back 10-15 years and also look at the **quarterly numbers** rather than the simple annual ones.

Quick Ratio vs. Current Ratio
-----------------------------

Both the Quick Ratio and Current Ratio are valuable in financial analysis and are used to evaluate a company’s liquidity.

However, there are distinct differences in their calculations and meaning:

**1) Scope of Assets Considered:** The Quick Ratio focuses narrowly on the most-liquid assets — specifically, Cash, Cash-Equivalents, and Accounts Receivable. It is based on the idea that certain assets, such as Inventory, may not be as easily convertible into Cash, especially in a crisis or downturn.

On the other hand, the Current Ratio includes all current assets, including everything above and Inventory and Prepaid Expenses.

**2) Conservatism:** Given its exclusion of Inventory and other less-liquid assets, the Quick Ratio is often viewed as the **more conservative** liquidity measure. This is especially pronounced in industries with slow inventory turnover, such as spirits (alcohol) companies.

**3) Lender Perspective:** From a creditor’s standpoint, understanding a firm’s immediate liquidity is important. While the Current Ratio provides a broader perspective on short-term solvency, the Quick Ratio tests the company’s ability to meet its obligations without liquidating Inventory or selling other assets.

As a result, lenders may weigh the Quick Ratio more heavily when assessing creditworthiness in certain contexts.
