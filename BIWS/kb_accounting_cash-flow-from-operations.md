Cash Flow from Operations: Definition, Exceptions, and Valuation Usage
======================================================================

On the Cash Flow Statement, the Cash Flow from Operations (CFO) section starts with Net Income from the Income Statement, adjusts for non-cash income and expenses, and reflects the Change in Working Capital (either positive or negative); CFO represents a company’s recurring cash flow \*before CapEx\* and other investing/financing activities.

> **Cash Flow from Operations Definition:** On the Cash Flow Statement, the Cash Flow from Operations (CFO) section starts with Net Income from the Income Statement, adjusts for non-cash income and expenses, and reflects the Change in Working Capital (either positive or negative); CFO represents a company’s recurring cash flow \*before CapEx\* and other investing/financing activities.

Some companies also use the Direct Method for their [Cash Flow Statements](https://breakingintowallstreet.com/kb/accounting/cash-flow-statement/)
, in which case Cash Flow from Operations will be based on cash inflows (receipts from customers) and cash outflows (payments to suppliers, employees, etc.). You will need to find a reconciliation to a CFS that starts with a metric such as [Net Income](https://breakingintowallstreet.com/kb/accounting/net-income/)
 if the company does this.

Two basic definitions for Cash Flow from Operations are as follows:

![Cash Flow from Operations Definitions](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%201392%20351'%3E%3C/svg%3E "Cash Flow from Operations Definitions")

However, it is rarely this simple for real-life companies.

They often have many additional non-cash adjustments beyond [Depreciation & Amortization](https://breakingintowallstreet.com/kb/accounting/capex-depreciation/)
, and parts of the CFO section may need to be recategorized, especially for non-U.S. companies that follow IFRS.

Some people claim that Cash Flow from Operations is like [EBITDA](https://breakingintowallstreet.com/kb/accounting/ebitda/)
, or that EBITDA is a “proxy” for Cash Flow from Operations.

**This is not true because there are substantial differences between them, such as the Cash Interest Expense and Cash Taxes.**

CFO corresponds _mostly_ to the Current Assets and Liabilities on a company’s [Balance Sheet](https://breakingintowallstreet.com/kb/accounting/balance-sheet/)
, and any calculation of CFO must reflect the company’s cash taxes, non-cash adjustments, and [Change in Working Capital](https://breakingintowallstreet.com/kb/financial-statement-analysis/change-in-working-capital/)
.

It should _not_ reflect other items, such as the Dividends the company issues, Capital Expenditures (CapEx), or Changes in Debt; if these items are in CFO, you should move them to other sections of the Cash Flow Statement for modeling purposes.

You need to **calculate** Cash Flow from Operations in almost all financial models, but you tend not to use it as a **direct** **valuation metric or multiple** because it’s an “intermediate” metric.

It has many non-standard and idiosyncratic line items, depending on the company, but it also excludes major items that affect a company’s true cash flow, such as CapEx.

Therefore, it’s not that useful as a “comparison metric” (e.g., EBITDA or Revenue), but it’s also not great for approximating a company’s true cash flows.

**Cash Flow from Operations: Calculation Variations**
-----------------------------------------------------

There are three main ways to set up the Cash Flow from Operations section in a company’s financial statements and in [financial models](https://mergersandinquisitions.com/financial-modeling/)
:

*   **Indirect Method:** Start with Net Income and adjust for all the non-cash items that have affected revenue and expenses on the [Income Statement,](https://breakingintowallstreet.com/kb/accounting/income-statement/)
     and then reflect the Change in Working Capital.
*   **Indirect Method with Non-Standard Starting Point:** Do the same thing, but start with a metric like [Operating Income](https://breakingintowallstreet.com/kb/accounting/ebit-operating-income/)
    , Pre-Tax Income, or EBITDA instead of Net Income. This makes the **non-cash adjustments trickier** because you only adjust if the item in question has affected the starting metric.
*   **Direct Method:** Show the cash inflows the company has received from product/service sales, and the cash outflows it has spent on employees, suppliers, rent, etc.

You must be prepared for all these possibilities in modeling tests and on the job.

[LBO modeling tests](https://mergersandinquisitions.com/lbo-modeling-test/)
, especially, _love_ to present you with Cash Flow Statements that start with EBITDA rather than Net Income.

So, if you’re completing a modeling test or case study, you must make sure your Cash Flow from Operations section reflects the correct items, even if it looks different from the standard setup.

We recommend using this checklist to verify your model (assuming it starts with a profitability-based metric, such as EBITDA or Net Income):

*   **Cash Taxes:** Does your CFO section fully deduct the Cash Taxes the company pays? This could be a direct line item, or it could be based on the Book Taxes (deducted in Net Income) +/- a Deferred Tax adjustment.
*   **Change in Working Capital:** The CFO section must always reflect this (it is negative when Working Capital increases and positive when it decreases).
*   **Cash Net Interest Expense:** Does your CFO section include the full Cash Interest Income and deduct the full Cash Interest Expense? These should both be part of Net Income, but there may be non-cash adjustments; if CFO starts with EBITDA, you’ll have to add these lines manually.
*   **No CapEx, Investments, Debt, or Equity:** The CFO section should never have lines related to these.
*   **Non-Cash Adjustments:** The CFO section should **reverse** any non-cash items that have affected the starting metric, such as Net Income. The most common one is Depreciation & Amortization, but Gains and Losses and Impairments are also in this category. If CFO starts with EBITDA, there will be many fewer reversals.

**Cash Flow from Operations: Common Adjustments in Financial Models**
---------------------------------------------------------------------

To illustrate the most common adjustments and transformations, we’ll walk through the thought process for three companies: Target in the U.S., Watches of Switzerland in the U.K., and Telstra in Australia.

For U.S.-based companies that use the Indirect Method for the Cash Flow Statement, such as Target, the list of adjustments tends to be short:

*   **Zero out** Stock-Based Compensation in the projected period since it dilutes existing shareholders and distorts the share count. Treat it as a normal cash expense.
*   **Zero out** non-recurring lines, such as Gains and Losses, in the projections.
*   **Consolidate** smaller line items so that the CFO section does not extend too long.

Below are Target’s “baseline” Cash Flow from Operations section and our modified version.

Note that in real life, we would not “zero out” any items in the historical period; these are set to 0 here only to demonstrate what would happen **in the projected period:**

![Target - Cash Flow from Operations](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%20911%201007'%3E%3C/svg%3E "Target - Cash Flow from Operations")

For a company like Watches of Switzerland that follows IFRS, it’s common to see “summary” Cash Flow Statements that start with a metric such as EBITDA or Adjusted EBITDA instead of Net Income.

The biggest problems here relate to the company’s classification of Net Interest Expense and CapEx, which we would change around in any forecast that requires Cash Flow from Operations and [Free Cash Flow](https://breakingintowallstreet.com/kb/financial-statement-analysis/how-to-calculate-free-cash-flow/)
:

![Watches of Switzerland - Baseline Cash Flow from Operations](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%201079%20691'%3E%3C/svg%3E "Watches of Switzerland - Baseline Cash Flow from Operations")

Our modified version looks like this:

![Watches of Switzerland - Modified Cash Flow from Operations](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%20879%20542'%3E%3C/svg%3E "Watches of Switzerland - Modified Cash Flow from Operations")

Moving to the last example, Telstra’s reported Cash Flow from Operations section (based on a reconciliation to the “Direct” version of its CFS) has several glaring issues:

![Telstra - Baseline Cash Flow from Operations](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%201111%20906'%3E%3C/svg%3E "Telstra - Baseline Cash Flow from Operations")

We would do the following to fix this:

*   **Delete the reclassifications** for Finance Income and Finance Costs (i.e., Net Interest Expense) – this line always needs to be deducted in CFO. Net Income already deducts it, so remove these reclassifications.
*   **Zero out** the Gains/Losses in the forecasts.
*   **Keep** several of the “reclassification” lines since Government Grants should not be in CFO, as they are investing/financing-related.
*   **Keep** most of the Non-Cash Adjustments, but **zero out** the SBC and Impairments in the forecasts.
*   **Consolidate** a few line items or put them in an “Other” category to make this section shorter.

Here’s the revised version:

![Telstra - Baseline Cash Flow from Operations](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%20739%20533'%3E%3C/svg%3E "Telstra - Baseline Cash Flow from Operations")

The **time** you spend on these adjustments depends heavily on the context.

For example, if it’s a 60-minute modeling test, you can’t afford to change much because that’s barely enough time to finish a real model.

But if it’s a 1-week test or an on-the-job task, you might spend hours making these changes.

**Cash Flow from Operations: Why It’s Not an Ideal Valuation Metric**
---------------------------------------------------------------------

Most metrics used in [valuation multiples](https://breakingintowallstreet.com/kb/valuation/valuation-multiples/)
 aim to do one of two things:

*   **Allow for an Easier Comparison of Companies** – For example, is one company growing more quickly than other, similar firms?
*   **Approximate the Company’s Cash Flows** – This could be [cash flow to all the investors](https://breakingintowallstreet.com/kb/discounted-cash-flow-analysis-dcf/unlevered-free-cash-flow/)
     or [just the equity investors](https://breakingintowallstreet.com/kb/valuation/levered-free-cash-flow/)
    .

**The problem is that Cash Flow from Operations does neither one well.**

Effectively, it’s “the worst of both worlds” because it’s _not_ that great for comparing companies due to the wide range of non-standard adjustments and line items in the historical periods…

…but it’s also not great for approximating the company’s cash flows to _any_ investor group because it excludes major items like CapEx.

So, in valuation methodologies such as [comparable public companies](https://breakingintowallstreet.com/kb/valuation/comparable-company-analysis-cca/)
 and [precedent transactions](https://breakingintowallstreet.com/kb/valuation/precedent-transaction-analysis/)
, you tend to use metrics that are better suited for comparison purposes, such as [EBIT and EBITDA](https://breakingintowallstreet.com/kb/valuation/ebit-vs-ebitda/)
.

And in cash flow-based methodologies, such as the [DCF](https://mergersandinquisitions.com/dcf-model/)
, you use metrics like Unlevered Free Cash Flow that better approximate the recurring cash flow.

**Cash Flow from Operations Variations in Other Industries (Oil & Gas)**
------------------------------------------------------------------------

All that said, you will sometimes see valuations based on multiples such as Equity Value / Cash Flow from Operations, also known as P / CFO when these are calculated on a per-share basis.

Here’s an example from the oil & gas industry in a [Fairness Opinion for ConocoPhillips’ acquisition of Marathon Oil](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1163165/000110465924074456/tm2416360-3_s4.htm#tOOMS1)
:

![Oil & Gas - P / Cash Flow Multiples](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%201154%20230'%3E%3C/svg%3E "Oil & Gas - P / Cash Flow Multiples")

Have the bankers lost their minds and decided to use a non-ideal valuation metric?

It’s entirely possible that they have lost their minds after pulling 10 consecutive all-nighters, but the **likely motivation** is as follows:

For an [Upstream oil & gas company](https://mergersandinquisitions.com/oil-gas-modeling-101/)
, i.e., one that searches for oil and gas and drills wells to extract it, **capital structure and taxes** are very important.

These companies tend to use a lot of Debt, resulting in a high Interest Expense, and many use accelerated Depreciation and other deductions to reduce their Cash Taxes.

If the bankers used only EBITDA or EBITDAX, these metrics would not reflect the impact of these policies:

![Oil & Gas - Cash Flow Definition](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%201094%2061'%3E%3C/svg%3E "Oil & Gas - Cash Flow Definition")

This raises the question of why they didn’t use Free Cash Flow to reflect CapEx fully, and we can’t answer that based on this document.

In Canada, many oil & gas companies use metrics such as “Adjusted Funds Flow from Operations” or “Funds Flow,” which are variations of Cash Flow from Operations.

Typically, they reverse the Change in Working Capital, Transaction Expenses, and the Decommissioning Expenditure required to retire oil/gas wells:

![Oil & Gas - Adjusted Funds Flow from Operations Variation](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%201384%20217'%3E%3C/svg%3E "Oil & Gas - Adjusted Funds Flow from Operations Variation")

This metric, like CFO, also pairs with Equity Value in valuation multiples.

The logic is similar: Unlike EBITDA, this AFFO metric reflects the Net Interest Expense and Cash Taxes but _not_ Capital Expenditures.

Therefore, it might be useful if you care about companies’ capital structures and tax policies but want to _normalize or ignore_ their Capital Expenditures.

(Again, we question why they would not use Free Cash Flow to account for all of this and better approximate the true cash flow.)
