Understanding the Disposition Effect
------------------------------------

### What Causes This Bias?

The disposition effect stems from **loss aversion**, a key finding from behavioral finance. Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory (1979) showed that losses feel about twice as painful as equivalent gains feel good.

This asymmetry creates two problematic behaviors:

*   **Selling winners too early** - to "lock in" gains and avoid the regret of watching profits disappear
*   **Holding losers too long** - avoiding the pain of realizing a loss, hoping to "get back to even"

### The Tax Consequence

When you sell a winning position before holding it for more than one year, you trigger **short-term capital gains**, taxed at your ordinary income rate (up to 37%).

If you wait past the one-year mark, you qualify for **long-term capital gains** rates (0%, 15%, or 20%), which are significantly lower for most investors.

The disposition effect's urge to sell winners early often costs investors real money in unnecessary taxes.

#### Biased Behavior: Sell Early

*   Triggers short-term capital gains tax
*   Taxed at ordinary income rates
*   Driven by fear of losing gains
*   Feels "safe" but costs more

#### Rational Alternative: Wait for LT

*   Qualifies for long-term capital gains
*   Taxed at preferential rates
*   Requires overcoming emotional urge
*   May save significant tax dollars

**Important:** Waiting for long-term rates isn't always the right choice. Consider opportunity cost, market risk, and portfolio rebalancing needs. This calculator shows the tax cost, but that's just one factor in your decision.

Frequently Asked Questions
--------------------------

### What is the disposition effect?

The disposition effect is a behavioral bias where investors tend to sell winning positions too early (to "lock in" gains) while holding losing positions too long (avoiding the pain of realizing a loss).

It was identified by Shefrin and Statman in 1985 and is commonly explained by loss aversion from Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory. The term comes from investors' "disposition" to sell winners and hold losers - the exact opposite of the tax-optimal strategy.

### How does the disposition effect affect taxes?

When you sell a winning position before holding it for more than one year, you pay short-term capital gains tax (taxed at ordinary income rates, up to 37%). If you wait until after the one-year mark, you qualify for long-term capital gains rates (0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income).

The disposition effect's urge to sell winners early often triggers the higher short-term rate unnecessarily, costing investors real money in avoidable taxes.

### What if I've already held for more than a year?

Great news - you already qualify for long-term capital gains rates! There's no additional tax cost from the disposition effect in this scenario.

The calculator will show Tax Savings = $0 because you're already at the lower rate. Any decision to sell now is not penalized by the short-term/long-term tax difference.

### What if my position is at a loss?

If your position is underwater (current value less than cost basis), the disposition effect works in reverse: it causes you to HOLD the loser, hoping to "get back to even." This is called "get-even-itis."

Ironically, this is the opposite of tax-optimal behavior. You should consider **tax-loss harvesting** - selling the loss to offset gains elsewhere and reduce your tax bill. This calculator focuses on the winning-position scenario; tax-loss harvesting value is not modeled here.

### How is the disposition effect related to loss aversion?

The disposition effect is commonly explained by loss aversion, a key concept from behavioral finance discovered by Kahneman and Tversky in 1979.

Loss aversion means that losses feel about twice as painful as equivalent gains feel good. This asymmetry causes investors to:

*   **Sell winners early** - to secure the good feeling and avoid watching gains disappear
*   **Hold losers too long** - to avoid the pain of realizing a loss

Other behavioral factors like mental accounting and regret aversion also contribute to the disposition effect.

### Should I always wait for long-term capital gains rates?

Not necessarily. While this calculator shows the tax cost of selling early, there are valid reasons to sell before reaching long-term status:

*   **Opportunity cost** - you may have better uses for the capital
*   **Market outlook** - if you expect the position to decline, the tax savings may not offset the loss
*   **Portfolio rebalancing** - maintaining proper asset allocation may require selling
*   **Loss harvesting** - you may have gains to offset with other losses

Tax optimization is important, but it's just one factor in investment decisions. Don't let the tax tail wag the investment dog.
