Understanding Accretion/Dilution Analysis
-----------------------------------------

### What is Accretion/Dilution Analysis?

**Accretion/dilution analysis** measures the pro forma effects of an M&A transaction on the acquirer's earnings per share (EPS). By comparing post-deal EPS to standalone EPS, analysts determine whether a transaction creates or destroys shareholder value on an EPS basis.

Core Logic

**Accretive:** Pro Forma EPS > Standalone EPS  
**Dilutive:** Pro Forma EPS < Standalone EPS  
Public companies generally avoid dilutive transactions

### Cash vs Stock Financing Trade-off

#### Cash (Debt) Financing

**Creates interest expense**  
Reduces pro forma net income by financing cost, but does not dilute share count. Favorable when cost of debt < acquirer's earnings yield.

#### Stock Financing

**Dilutes share count**  
No interest expense, but increases shares outstanding. Favorable when acquirer's stock is highly valued (high P/E multiple).

### Key Drivers of Accretion

*   **Higher target earnings:** More net income contribution improves pro forma EPS
*   **Lower purchase price:** Fewer new shares issued, less debt interest
*   **Greater synergies:** Cost savings and revenue enhancements boost earnings
*   **Cheaper financing:** Lower interest rate on debt portion
*   **Higher acquirer P/E:** Stock consideration dilutes less per dollar

**Crossover Premium:** The maximum price an acquirer can pay before a deal turns dilutive. Sell-side advisors use this to push buyers to increase bids.

**Limitations:** This simplified model excludes purchase accounting (intangible amortization, write-ups), transaction costs, and multi-year EPS trajectories. Real M&A analysis involves significantly more complexity.

Frequently Asked Questions
--------------------------

### What is accretion/dilution analysis?

Accretion/dilution analysis measures how an M&A transaction affects the acquirer's earnings per share (EPS). If pro forma EPS exceeds standalone EPS, the deal is accretive. If pro forma EPS falls below standalone EPS, the deal is dilutive. Public companies are generally reluctant to pursue dilutive transactions due to potential negative effects on share price.

### What makes a deal accretive vs dilutive?

A deal is accretive when pro forma EPS exceeds the acquirer's standalone EPS. This occurs when the target's earnings contribution (net income plus synergies minus financing costs) adds more to the numerator than the impact on the denominator from new shares issued or reduced earnings from interest expense. Higher target earnings, lower purchase price, cheaper financing, and greater synergies all increase accretion.

### Why does cash vs stock financing matter?

Cash financing creates interest expense that reduces pro forma net income, but doesn't dilute share count. Stock financing dilutes shares outstanding but adds no interest expense. The optimal mix depends on the acquirer's cost of debt (after-tax interest rate) versus its earnings yield (inverse of P/E ratio). When interest rate < earnings yield, cash financing is more accretive.

### What is the "crossover" premium in M&A?

The crossover premium is the maximum price (or premium to target's unaffected share price) an acquirer can pay before a deal turns from accretive to dilutive. It's a key negotiation benchmark - sellers push buyers to this ceiling while buyers resist paying more than their accretion analysis supports. Note: this calculator helps analyze specific scenarios but does not directly solve for crossover premium.

### How do synergies affect accretion/dilution?

Synergies (cost savings, revenue enhancements) flow directly to pro forma net income and improve accretion. Higher synergies allow acquirers to pay higher premiums while maintaining accretion. Synergy assumptions are often scrutinized in deal negotiations - sellers want buyers to credit more synergies (justifying higher prices), while buyers are cautious about achievability.

### What are the limitations of this simplified model?

This calculator excludes purchase accounting (intangible amortization, asset write-ups), transaction costs (advisory fees, financing costs), and multi-year EPS trajectories. Real M&A models also consider cash flow impact, balance sheet effects, control premiums, and deal structure nuances. Use this for directional analysis and learning, not final deal decisions.

##### Disclaimer

This calculator is for educational purposes only and uses a simplified M&A model. Actual deal analysis involves purchase accounting, transaction costs, multi-year projections, and many other factors not captured here. This tool should not be used for investment or transaction decisions. Consult professional advisors for actual M&A transactions.
