What is a CFO?
--------------

The CFO, or Chief Financial Officer, is the mastermind behind a [company’s financial strategy](https://escalon.services/blog/strategies-to-improve-financial-operations/)
. They are strategic visionaries who use their financial acumen to steer the organization towards long-term value creation. In our nautical analogy, if your company is a ship braving turbulent economic waters, the CFO is the captain charting the course to success.

CFOs are senior-level executives with a significant say in everything from the company’s investments and expenses to forecasting and funding sources. Anything that impacts the company’s finances, such as a project budget, must first be signed off by the CFO.

### What is a Financial Controller?

Now, let’s shift our focus to the financial controller, often likened to the backbone of a company’s financial operations. While the CFO sets the strategic direction, the financial controller is the engine that keeps the financial gears turning smoothly. They are the architects, ensuring the CFO’s vision becomes a reality and aligns with the necessary regulations and standards. Financial controllers are responsible for maintaining a company’s financial infrastructure, ensuring compliance, accuracy, and efficiency.

### CFO vs. Controller: Roles & Responsibilities

The roles of CFOs and financial controllers may sometimes overlap, depending on the company’s size. Still, their core responsibilities diverge in significant ways. CFOs primarily focus on big-picture financial strategies, such as forecasting, budgeting, and [financial planning](https://escalon.services/blog/financial-planning-and-analysis/)
. Their role is more outward-facing, interacting with investors, analyzing market trends, and making high-stakes decisions to drive the company forward. On the other hand, financial controllers are more hands-on, overseeing accounting functions, managing financial reporting, and ensuring regulatory compliance. They work internally within the organization, safeguarding its financial integrity and meticulously scrutinizing every transaction to maintain accuracy and transparency.

### 4 Key Differences Between a CFO and Controller

To help you distinguish which one to opt for – financial controller or CFO services for SaaS companies – you first need to understand the critical distinctions between the two. Some of these distinctions include:

1.  **Scope of Responsibility:** CFOs typically have broader strategic responsibilities, including [risk management](https://escalon.services/blog/5-financial-risk-management-best-practices-every-entrepreneur-should-know/)
    , capital structure, and investor relations, while financial controllers focus more on day-to-day financial operations. CFOs are often more external-facing and represent the face of the company, while financial controllers maintain a more internal-facing role, acting as the face of the accounting team.
2.  **Decision-Making Authority:** CFOs often have the final say in major financial decisions, whereas financial controllers provide recommendations and support to aid decision-making. When a company has a CFO and a financial controller, CFOs always sit higher in the hierarchy. In contrast, financial controllers report to the CFO.
3.  **Strategic vs. Tactical:** CFOs are forward-looking, setting long-term financial goals and strategies. In contrast, financial controllers focus more on executing those strategies and ensuring operational efficiency. A CFO’s roles and responsibilities take a broader approach. On the other hand, financial controllers need to be more precise and technical with their tasks.
4.  **Depth of Knowledge:** The scope of each role also impacts the kind of knowledge each must have. Since CFOs are the strategizers, they require an overarching understanding of financial markets and trends, but not necessarily the nitty-gritty details that go into them. Financial controllers must be well-versed in accounting details and are also expected to maintain consistent [GAAP](https://escalon.services/blog/glossary-the-accounting-and-finance-terms-every-business-owner-must-know/)
     compliance.
