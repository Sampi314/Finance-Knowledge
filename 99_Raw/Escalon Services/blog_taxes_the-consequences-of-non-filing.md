Unpaid business taxes can have dire repercussions for a company, big or small. It can lead to a chain of consequences that can be detrimental to the business’s financial health as well as legal standing.

Let’s explore what happens when a small business does not file taxes — or makes [mistakes related to filing taxes](https://escalon.services/blog/is-your-startup-making-these-6-tax-mistakes/)
 — and the potential impact it can have on both the business and its owners.

### Consequence 1: Penalties and interest charges

The most immediate consequence of not filing taxes on time is the incurrence of penalties and interest charges. The Internal Revenue Service (IRS) imposes penalties for [Failure to File](https://www.irs.gov/payments/failure-to-file-penalty)
 and [Failure to Pay taxes](https://www.irs.gov/payments/failure-to-pay-penalty)
, which can quickly accumulate, exacerbating the financial burden on the small business.

These penalties are typically based on a percentage of the unpaid taxes owed, and the longer the taxes remain unpaid, the more the business owes in penalties and interest. According to the IRS, the Failure to File Penalty is based on how late you file your tax return and the amount of unpaid tax as of the original payment due date. It is 5 percent of the unpaid taxes for each month or part of a month that a tax return is late. Whereas the Failure to Pay Penalty based on how long your overdue taxes remain unpaid. It is 0.5 percent of the unpaid taxes for each month or part of a month the tax remains unpaid.  

### Consequence 2: Legal action

If a small business repeatedly fails to file taxes or neglects to pay its tax obligations, the IRS may escalate its response by taking legal action against the business. This can involve the issuance of a tax lien or tax levy.

A [tax lien](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/understanding-a-federal-tax-lien)
 gives the government a legal claim to the business’s assets, making it challenging for the business to access credit or sell its assets without satisfying the tax debt. On the other hand, a [tax levy](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/what-is-a-levy)
 allows the IRS to seize the business’s assets, including bank accounts, equipment and inventory, to settle the outstanding tax liability.

In addition, the [Federal Payment Levy Program (FPLP)](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/federal-payment-levy-program)
 allows the IRS to collect a business’s overdue taxes through a continuous levy on certain federal payments disbursed by the Bureau of the Fiscal Service (BFS).  

### Consequence 3: Loss of business credibility

Beyond he financial consequences, not filing (or [misfiling](https://escalon.services/blog/small-business-taxes/)
) taxes can significantly damage a small business’s reputation and credibility. Customers, suppliers, vendors as well as potential partners and investors may view the business as untrustworthy or unreliable, making it difficult to maintain existing relationships or establish new ones. This loss of credibility can hinder growth opportunities and create a negative image in the market. 

### Consequence 4: Criminal charges

In cases where a small business willfully engages in tax evasion or fraud, the consequences can escalate to criminal charges, too. The IRS takes tax evasion seriously, and if found guilty, the business owners could face significant fines, probation or even imprisonment.

### Consequence 5: Accumulation of debt

As penalties and interest continue to accrue, the tax debt can snowball into an overwhelming financial burden. Small businesses that struggle to keep up with their tax obligations may find it increasingly challenging to break free from the cycle of debt, leading to potential insolvency and closure.
