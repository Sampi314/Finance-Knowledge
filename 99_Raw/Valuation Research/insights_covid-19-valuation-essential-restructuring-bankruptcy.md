Many businesses are being hit hard by the current shutdown amid the coronavirus pandemic. Hopes were up briefly with the launch of the SBA Economic Injury Disaster Loan program and the Paycheck Protection Program (PPP) loans. The sudden and unfortunate reality is that the funds from the first round of funding from the programs dried up in 13 days, and the second round may be depleted even more quickly.

Even with the additional injection of $310 billion into the PPP, which is part of the $484 billion interim emergency bill that passed the U.S. Senate on April 22nd, many businesses may need to seek other sources of financing, which may also need to include restructuring through an out-of-court workout, a Chapter 11 reorganization, or a Chapter 7 liquidation.

In all cases, obtaining an objective valuation is a crucial component of the restructuring process.

### The Out-of-Court Workout

An out-of-court workout is a restructuring of the liabilities and obligations of a debtor through negotiations directly with the debtor’s creditors outside of the bankruptcy courts. Creditors in an out of court workout look to the future earnings of the debtor to satisfy their claims.

Out-of-court workouts are generally driven by management’s desire to reach a suitable agreement with creditors to avoid having to file a bankruptcy petition. In addition, management generally seeks to restructure uneconomical contracts or contracts that have been breached. Management is also looking to obtain financial relief and raise additional capital to execute its strategy successfully.

Companies face many challenges in a workout, including complying with the terms of existing agreements that may place limitations on transactions, in particular, affiliated transactions, and determining the value of negotiated deal items that will be used to settle creditors’ claims.

A fairness opinion is often needed in workout situations to support new capital raises, including affiliated transactions. Additionally, any new securities that are issued in connection with the workout will need to be valued.

### Chapter 11 Reorganizations

A Chapter 11 reorganization is a rehabilitation of a creditor under the Bankruptcy Code. Either the debtor-in-possession (DIP) or trustee, a representative of the creditors, remains in control of the business and assets. Like an out-of-court workout, creditors in a Chapter 11 reorganization look to the future earnings of the debtor to satisfy their claims.

The principal goals of a Chapter 11 reorganization are the following:

*   Continue operating the business.
*   Formulate a plan of rehabilitation of the debtor and have the creditors and bankruptcy courts accept the plan.
*   Discharge certain liabilities as a result of the plan’s confirmation.
*   Make payments according to the plan.

In a Chapter 11 scenario, the challenges for the debtor are to:

*   Establish a reorganization plan that will be approved by the impaired creditors.
*   Obtain DIP financing.
*   Determine the value of securities or payments that will be made to the creditors.

Valuation services are needed at various points during the reorganization process. Liquidation and going concern valuations are required under Section 1126 of the Bankruptcy Code. In addition, any divisions or assets that are sold will need to be valued. For fresh start accounting purposes, determination of the reorganization value and a valuation of the company’s assets and liabilities is necessary.

### Chapter 7 Liquidation

In a Chapter 7 liquidation, the trustee collects all nonexempt property of the debtor, converts the property to cash, and distributes the cash to creditors. All distributions to creditors are cash, not in kind. Creditors look to the current liquidation value of assets to satisfy their claims.

The most significant issues facing the debtor in a Chapter 7 liquidation are:

*   Determining the values of the assets or business divisions.
*   Whether the assets should be liquidated individually or sold as part of a going concern.

In conjunction with a Chapter 7 liquidation VRC often provides a liquidation value of assets as well as an enterprise valuation to support the process.
