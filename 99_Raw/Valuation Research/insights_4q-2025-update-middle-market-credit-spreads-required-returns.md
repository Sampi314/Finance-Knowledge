_The article in brief:_

*   _Middle market credit spreads were unchanged in 4Q 2025, while Original Issue Discounts (OIDs) continued to tighten._
*   _New deal flow improved into year-end, although lender demand continues to outstrip new issuance._
*   _Looking into 2026, the deal backdrop remains constructive for higher-quality assets, although tariff- and cycle-exposed credits may require higher risk premiums._  

In the fourth quarter of 2025, new deal flow in private credit improved, partially offsetting lenders’ capital deployment needs and strong competition from the broadly syndicated market. VRC maintained middle market credit spread ranges in December versus September. However, OIDs continue to tighten with issuance prices at approximately 98.75 to 99.25 versus roughly 98.50 to 99.00 in September.[![Traditional Middle Market Credit Spreads and Required Returns 4Q 2025](https://www.valuationresearch.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/264767-VRC-Tables-R1.svg)](https://www.valuationresearch.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/264767-VRC-Tables-R1.svg)
While VRC’s middle market credit spread bands remain unchanged quarter-over-quarter, several trends remain: 

*   A growing share of deals—across varying quality—are pricing in the low-to-mid band range amid intense lender competition. 
*   The band’s upper end is typically reserved for lower-quality credits (e.g., storied, cyclical, highly leveraged, tariff-exposed, etc.), which may have a difficult time coming to market. 
*   Pricing grids, tied to lower leverage, with step-downs (e.g., S+475 → S+450 or S+450 → S+425) are increasingly common. 
*   Yields compressed following the Federal Reserve’s 25 basis point rate cuts in September, October, and December. 

Overall, credit markets remain active and competitive, with spreads near historic lows and yields still supported by elevated reference rates. 

### Outlook

Several factors continue to drive a resurgence in M&A and leveraged buyout pipelines moving into 2026: portfolio reviews revealed limited tariff impact, recent trade developments provided greater clarity, and Federal Reserve rate cuts have improved market sentiment. Additionally, private credit and equity firms continue to hold significant dry powder, while private equity funds face mounting pressure to exit legacy investments with extended hold periods of seven to eight years. 

While uncertainty persists regarding the broader effects of tariffs on GDP, inflation, and interest rates, the current deal environment remains favorable for high-quality assets. Elevated purchase multiples, tight credit spreads, and aggressive underwriting are expected to continue, barring any major macro shocks. However, companies exposed to tariffs or cyclical trends will likely face higher risk premiums. This dynamic may persist until deal volume accelerates or reference rates decline meaningfully—both of which are beginning to show early signs of momentum. 

### Portfolio Performance and Credit Risk

Market participants generally expect existing portfolio investments to remain resilient, supported in part by anticipated relief from elevated cash interest expenses as reference rates continue to decline. While idiosyncratic challenges will persist, default rate expectations remain modest.  

However, portfolios still contain troubled companies with elevated leverage, constrained interest coverage, and near-term maturities. These situations may require restructuring or other capital structure solutions, which could weigh on valuations.  

As always, valuation analyses must be approached on a case-by-case basis, emphasizing core performance metrics, debt sustainability, and liquidity. Some credits will continue to outperform others, reflecting the uneven nature of the current environment.
