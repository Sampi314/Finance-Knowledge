_The article in brief:_

*   _Lower interest rates, temporarily easing tariff concerns, and record equity markets helped drive a year-end surge in deal value._
*   _Exit backlogs continued to shape sponsor behavior, accelerating liquidity strategies such as dividend recapitalizations and continuation vehicles._
*   _Private credit markets remained supportive, with competitive terms and improving market stability._
*   _Looking ahead, tariff and tax-driven planning may accelerate reorganizations and cross-border activity in 2026 – driving increased demand for valuation support._

[![U.S. PE Deal Activity](https://www.valuationresearch.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/264767-Deals-charts-R1_US-PE-Deal-activity.svg)](https://www.valuationresearch.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/264767-Deals-charts-R1_US-PE-Deal-activity.svg)
After a choppy first half, shaped in part by April’s “Liberation Day” volatility, deal activity and investor confidence strengthened meaningfully in the second half of 2025. Federal Reserve rate cuts in September, October, and December, narrowing valuation gaps, and record-high equity market performance helped support the rebound. While full-year fourth-quarter data is not yet available, year-end activity points to a strong fourth quarter and a shift in market tone from “cautiously optimistic” to “optimistic” entering 2026.

After a brief slowdown in the second quarter, 2025 deal activity rebounded, led by mega transactions in technology, AI, and digital infrastructure. Financial services, industrials, healthcare, and power utilities also saw strong deal flow. In the middle market, activity remained robust across industrials, healthcare, residential services, and professional services. Add-on activity remained elevated, accounting for roughly 70% of deal volume, alongside a notable uptick in carve-out transactions. We also saw a more noticeable pickup in platform activity, particularly in traditional middle market transaction sizes.

[![U.S. PE Exit Activity](https://www.valuationresearch.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/264767-Deals-charts-R1_PE-exit-activity.svg)](https://www.valuationresearch.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/264767-Deals-charts-R1_PE-exit-activity.svg)
Exits—and liquidity more broadly—remained a central focus for private equity firms. IPO conditions improved relative to recent periods, and sponsor-to-sponsor deals continued to play an important role in providing exit pathways.

### Exits Remain the Pressure Point

Private equity holding periods have moved back toward historical norms of five-plus years, with average holds now around six years. Higher interest rates, economic uncertainty, and valuation mismatches have continued to slow exits, even as activity improved in 2025. With a large backlog of sponsor-owned companies the pace of exits remains a central theme entering 2026. Given the slow pace of exit options, liquidity solutions remain at the forefront. Dividend recapitalizations remained active in 2025, [reaching $43.6 billion](https://pitchbook.com/news/articles/us-leveraged-loan-dividend-payouts-hit-post-gfc-high)
, a new record.

Continuation vehicles also remained a liquidity option in select situations, though adoption and market sentiment can vary by sponsor, fund maturity, and LP dynamics. Even with that variability, [continuation vehicles represent roughly 16% of sponsor exit volume](https://www.ropesgray.com/en/insights/alerts/2025/11/secondaries-q3-2025-update)
, underscoring their growing role in today’s exit environment.

Deal structures have also continued to do more work as buyers and sellers continue to bridge residual valuation gaps. In the middle market, earnouts and rollover equity remain common tools, helping transactions move forward while underwriting forward performance remains sensitive to timing and execution. We expect these structures to remain an important tool in 2026 as the market builds momentum.

### Private Credit Remains a Key Deal Enabler

After April volatility faded, credit conditions stabilized quickly and remained broadly supportive of deal activity. While pockets of stress created short-term noise, lending spreads stayed tight, and financing remained available for high-quality borrowers.

In the middle market, observed unitranche coupons generally held in the 475 to 550 basis-point range, and original issue discounts compressed, with many new deals clearing closer to par. PIK features also appeared more frequently in certain structures during 2025, reflecting an ongoing focus on flexibility and liquidity management. While broader indicators remain constructive, liability management activity and pockets of performance pressure in certain post-COVID vintages remain areas to watch as the cycle continues to normalize. These are market observations for context, not pricing or execution guidance.

At the same time, private market product structures continue to evolve, including evergreen and perpetual-capital vehicles designed for institutional channels and high-net-worth investors. As these structures expand – and valuation cycles become more frequent – governance and transparency remain critical pillars for market confidence.

In parallel, a slower fundraising environment has given LPs negotiating leverage. To stay competitive, transaction-fee rebates, volume discounts, and tighter expense caps are more common. Management fee economics have drawn increased attention, reinforcing a broader industry focus on the “2” in the traditional “2 and 20” model. Middle market and emerging market managers remain more constrained by scale and bargaining power.

### Tariff and Tax Policy May Drive 2026 Valuation Demand

Looking ahead, evolving U.S. tariff actions and the [“One Big Beautiful Bill Act”-related tax changes](https://www.valuationresearch.com/insights/tax-valuation-impacts-one-big-beautiful-bill-act/)
 may create a meaningful uptick in valuation needs in 2026. We expect increased focus on reorganizations, restructurings, and cross-border transactions – driving demand for valuation analyses supported by robust, controversy-ready documentation.

Key areas we’re monitoring include international tax planning and related entity and intangible asset realignments, tariff-driven supply chain restructurings, and financing redesign due to shifting interest expense limitations. We also expect valuation needs tied to bonus depreciation strategies, R&D expense related planning activity, and equity structuring considerations for closely held and growth-stage companies.

### Evolving and Converging Valuation Standards

Regulators continue to emphasize the importance of valuation methodology, assumptions, and governance for illiquid assets, particularly as evergreen structures and more frequent valuation cycles become more common.  The SEC has also reinforced its focus on cooperation and convergence with international standard‐setters.

The AICPA recently released a working draft of an updated [_Valuation of Privately-Held-Company Equity Securities Issued as Compensation Accounting and Valuation Guide_](https://www.aicpa-cima.com/advocacy/download/working-draft-of-the-updated-valuation-of-privately-held-company-equity-unpublished)
_._ The most notable revisions focus on value allocation within complex capital structures and the treatment of secondary transactions. The working draft reflects best practice considerations for current and future transactions and valuations.

### 2026 Outlook: Steady, Not Exponential

There is strong momentum entering 2026, supported by easing monetary policy and improved market sentiment. While tariff uncertainty, geopolitical tensions, and inflation risks remain meaningful headwinds, sentiment has strengthened entering 2026.

We expect deal activity to build over time rather than snap back in a single surge, with continued strength in middle market add-ons and a gradual return of platform activity as confidence improves. Early January announced activity is tracking above typical levels, and we anticipate ongoing emphasis on creative liquidity solutions and deal structures that help bridge residual pricing gaps as sponsors work through exit backlogs.

We also expect tax and tariff-driven planning activity to become a more visible catalyst for reorganizations and cross-border transactions as 2026 unfolds.

This year, we’ll be watching whether platform activity continues to rebuild, how quickly exit backlogs clear, the nature and prevalence of liquidity solutions, and whether credit conditions remain supportive as refinancing needs to come into focus.
