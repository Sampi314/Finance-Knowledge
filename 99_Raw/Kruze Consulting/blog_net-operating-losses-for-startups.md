Net Operating Losses for Startups: How Founders Can Turn NOLs Into Future Tax Savings
=====================================================================================

by

Dave Lowe, CPA

For many startup founders, [net operating losses (NOLs)](https://kruzeconsulting.com/blog/net-operating-loss/)
 feel like a future problem. In the early years, when the business is focused on product development, hiring, and fundraising, tax losses rarely seem like a priority. But as a startup scales toward profitability, those accumulated losses can become one of the most important tax assets on the company’s books – and a key part of effective startup accounting.

If managed correctly, NOLs can help reduce future cash taxes, improve [financial statement](https://kruzeconsulting.com/blog/3-financial-statements/)
 presentation, and support value in an [acquisition](https://kruzeconsulting.com/blog/startup-acquisition-process/)
. For venture-backed technology companies, understanding how these tax attributes work is an important part of smart financial planning.

Why NOLs Become More Valuable Over Time
---------------------------------------

Most technology startups generate losses in their early years. That is normal. Engineering-heavy companies often invest aggressively in software development, sales expansion, and infrastructure before revenue catches up. Those tax losses generally become NOL carryforwards that may be used in future years.

As the company moves closer to profitability, those NOLs begin to matter much more. They can offset future taxable income and reduce the amount of federal [income tax](https://kruzeconsulting.com/blog/startup-tax-guide/)
 the company would otherwise owe. For founders, this can create meaningful cash savings during a critical growth stage, when preserving capital still matters even if the business is performing well.

In other words, yesterday’s losses may help fund tomorrow’s growth, an important part of startup accounting and smart financial planning.

How the 80% Limitation Works
----------------------------

Under current federal tax law, many NOLs generated in recent years can be carried forward indefinitely. However, they generally can only offset up to 80% of taxable income in any one year.

That rule matters because it means startups with large NOL balances may still owe tax once they become profitable. If a company generates $5 million of taxable income, its NOLs may only reduce that amount by up to $4 million. The remaining $1 million is still subject to federal income tax.

This often catches founders by surprise. A company may have spent years accumulating losses, yet still face a current tax bill when profitability arrives. State rules can complicate things even further, since some states do not conform to the federal rules and may impose their own limits, suspensions, or expiration periods. Staying aware of these differences is part of good startup accounting practice.

NOLs and Deferred Tax Assets
----------------------------

NOLs are not just a tax return concept. They also affect your financial statements. On the [balance sheet](https://kruzeconsulting.com/blog/balance-sheet/)
, NOL carryforwards may create deferred tax assets, which reflect the future tax benefit of those losses.

For startups with audited financials, outside investors, or acquisition plans, this matters. A properly tracked deferred tax asset can help present the company’s tax position more accurately. But there is a catch: if future profitability is uncertain, the company may need to record a valuation allowance against that asset.

As the business matures and forecasting improves, some of that valuation allowance may be released, which can have a meaningful impact on reported tax expense. This is one reason startups should keep detailed tax attribute schedules and revisit them regularly, especially after fundraising rounds, major hiring expansion, or shifts in the revenue model. Organized, up-to-date tax tracking is an essential part of sound startup accounting.

Pairing NOLs With R&D Credits
-----------------------------

NOLs are powerful, but they are not the whole story. Because of the 80% limitation, they often leave a portion of taxable income exposed. That is where the [federal R&D tax credit](https://kruzeconsulting.com/blog/research-and-development-tax-credit-eligibility/)
 can become especially valuable.

Unlike NOLs, which reduce taxable income, R&D credits reduce tax liability dollar-for-dollar. For startup technology companies investing heavily in engineers, developers, and technical product work, these credits can meaningfully reduce cash taxes once the company starts paying income tax.

There is an important limitation, however. Under the general business credit rules, companies often run into the so-called “25/25 limitation.” Once tax liability exceeds $25,000, credits generally may offset the first $25,000 of tax plus only 25% of the tax liability above that threshold. That means even profitable companies may not be able to use all available R&D credits immediately.

Still, unused credits generally carry forward, and when combined with NOL planning, they can materially reduce long-term tax exposure. For startups approaching profitability, modeling NOL usage and R&D credit utilization together usually produces a much more accurate tax forecast than looking at either item alone.

382: The Trap Founders Miss
---------------------------

One of the biggest risks to NOL value is [Section 382](https://kruzeconsulting.com/blog/section-382-triggers/)
. If a company experiences an ownership change, generally defined as a cumulative shift of more than 50 percentage points among certain shareholders over a rolling three-year period, the use of pre-change NOLs may be severely limited.

This issue commonly arises after multiple financing rounds, secondaries, recapitalizations, or an acquisition. Startups can have substantial NOLs on paper, but discover later that their annual usage is restricted under Section 382. That can significantly reduce the real value of the tax asset.

This is why ownership-change studies should happen before major transactions, not during [diligence](https://kruzeconsulting.com/blog/preparing-for-due-diligence/)
 cleanup. Monitoring these events should be a standing task that’s part of your startup accounting and tax compliance routines.

NOL Value in M&A
----------------

In an acquisition, buyers rarely assign full value to the headline NOL number. Instead, they evaluate whether the losses are actually usable. They will look at future taxable income projections, state-by-state limitations, whether [returns were filed correctly](https://kruzeconsulting.com/startup-tax-returns/)
, and whether Section 382 applies.

Well-documented NOL schedules and proactive tax analysis can increase credibility and help preserve deal value. Poor records, by contrast, often lead buyers to discount the asset or ignore it altogether.

For founders thinking about an exit, NOLs should be viewed as a strategic asset, not just a historical byproduct of startup losses.

Conclusion: What to do now?
---------------------------

If your startup is approaching profitability, planning a financing, or preparing for a transaction, now is the time to evaluate your NOLs, R&D credits, and Section 382 exposure.
