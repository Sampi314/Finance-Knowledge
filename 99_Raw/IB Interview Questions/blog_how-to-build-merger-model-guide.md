What is a Merger Model?
-----------------------

A **merger model** is a financial model that analyzes the impact of an M&A transaction on the acquiring company's **earnings per share (EPS)**. Also called an accretion/dilution model or M&A model, this analysis combines the financial statements of two companies into **pro forma combined financials** to determine whether the deal creates or destroys value for the acquirer's shareholders.

The core question a merger model answers is straightforward: will the acquirer's EPS increase or decrease after completing the transaction? If EPS increases, the deal is **accretive**. If EPS decreases, the deal is **dilutive**. This simple metric drives significant deal decisions because shareholders and boards care deeply about earnings impact.

Merger Model (Accretion/Dilution Model)

A financial model that combines two companies' income statements into pro forma combined financials to determine whether an acquisition will increase (accrete) or decrease (dilute) the acquirer's earnings per share. It incorporates purchase price, financing structure, synergies, and purchase accounting adjustments.

Beyond the headline accretion/dilution result, merger models also analyze **purchase price allocation**, goodwill creation, synergy realization, and the optimal mix of cash, stock, and debt to finance the transaction. Investment banking analysts build merger models to advise clients on deal structure, negotiate transaction terms, and present recommendations to boards and shareholders.

For investment banking interviews, merger modeling represents a **core technical competency** that every candidate must understand. Interviewers expect you to explain the mechanics of accretion/dilution, walk through the key drivers, and discuss how different deal structures affect the analysis. This guide covers everything you need to know to build a merger model from scratch.

The Core Concept: Accretion vs Dilution
---------------------------------------

### Understanding EPS Impact

The fundamental purpose of a merger model is determining whether a transaction is **accretive or dilutive** to the acquirer's earnings per share. This analysis compares the acquirer's standalone EPS to pro forma EPS after combining with the target.

**Accretive deal**: Pro forma EPS exceeds acquirer's standalone EPS. The acquisition increases earnings available per share, which is generally viewed positively by shareholders.

**Dilutive deal**: Pro forma EPS falls below acquirer's standalone EPS. The acquisition decreases earnings available per share, which may concern shareholders unless justified by strategic benefits.

The math is intuitive. When you acquire a company, you are buying its **earnings stream**. If you pay less for those earnings than your own earnings are valued, the deal is accretive. If you pay more, the deal is dilutive. The payment method matters because **stock consideration** increases shares outstanding while **cash consideration** does not.

### The Key Drivers

Several factors determine whether a deal is accretive or dilutive:

**Relative P/E multiples**: If the acquirer trades at a higher P/E than the target, stock deals tend to be accretive. The acquirer is trading expensive currency (high P/E stock) for cheaper earnings (low P/E target). Conversely, if the acquirer has a lower P/E, stock deals tend to be dilutive.

**Purchase premium**: Higher premiums make deals more dilutive because you are paying more for the same earnings stream. The premium above the target's market value directly affects accretion/dilution math.

**Financing mix**: Cash deals avoid share dilution but sacrifice interest income on cash used and potentially add interest expense on debt raised. Stock deals preserve cash but increase shares outstanding. The optimal mix depends on **relative costs and capital availability**.

**Synergies**: Cost savings and [revenue synergies](https://ibinterviewquestions.com/blog/synergies-in-ma-revenue-vs-cost)
 improve pro forma earnings, making deals more accretive. Higher synergies can turn a dilutive deal into an accretive one.

Understanding these drivers helps you anticipate accretion/dilution results before building the model and explain the sensitivity of results to key assumptions.

Step 1: Gather Transaction Assumptions
--------------------------------------

### Acquirer Information

Begin by collecting the **acquirer's key financial data**:

*   Current net income (or projected net income for the relevant period)
*   Shares outstanding (basic and diluted)
*   Current stock price
*   Implied P/E multiple (price divided by EPS)
*   Cash balance available for acquisition
*   Current debt levels and capacity for additional borrowing
*   Cost of debt (interest rate on new borrowings)
*   Tax rate

This information establishes the acquirer's **baseline earnings and valuation**, which you will compare against pro forma results.

### Target Information

Collect **parallel information for the target company**:

*   Current or projected net income
*   Book value of equity
*   Book value of assets and liabilities
*   Any significant off-balance sheet items
*   Existing debt that may be refinanced or remain outstanding

For public targets, you can gather this from SEC filings. For private targets, information comes from management presentations, data rooms, or [confidential information memoranda](https://ibinterviewquestions.com/blog/how-to-prepare-cim-guide)
.

### Deal Assumptions

Define the **transaction structure**:

*   Purchase price (enterprise value or equity value)
*   Premium to current stock price (for public targets)
*   Consideration mix: percentage cash vs. stock vs. debt
*   Transaction fees (investment banking, legal, accounting)
*   Financing fees (if raising new debt)
*   Expected synergies (cost and revenue)
*   Timeline for synergy realization
*   Any contingent consideration ([earnouts](https://ibinterviewquestions.com/blog/what-is-earnout-structure-examples-negotiation)
    )

These assumptions drive the entire model. Different financing mixes and synergy levels produce dramatically different accretion/dilution results, which is why **sensitivity analysis** is essential.

Step 2: Calculate Purchase Price and Financing
----------------------------------------------

### Determining Total Consideration

The total purchase price includes several components beyond the headline equity value:

**Equity purchase price** = Target shares outstanding x offer price per share (for stock deals) or total cash payment (for cash deals)

**Transaction fees** typically run 1-3% of deal value for advisory fees plus legal and accounting costs. These are usually expensed rather than capitalized.

**Financing fees** on new debt issuance are capitalized and amortized over the debt term.

**Total consideration** = Equity purchase price + transaction fees + financing fees

### Structuring the Financing

Determine how the acquirer will **fund the purchase price**:

**Cash on hand**: Using existing cash eliminates the need for new financing but sacrifices interest income. Calculate foregone interest as cash used multiplied by the acquirer's reinvestment rate.

**New debt**: Raising debt adds interest expense but avoids share dilution. Calculate interest expense as new debt multiplied by the cost of debt. Remember to **tax-effect the interest expense** since interest is tax-deductible.

**Stock issuance**: Issuing shares avoids cash outflow but dilutes existing shareholders. Calculate new shares issued as equity consideration divided by acquirer's stock price.

Most deals use a **combination of financing sources**. The optimal mix balances several factors: maintaining target credit ratings, minimizing dilution, preserving financial flexibility, and meeting any regulatory requirements.

### Example Calculation

Consider an acquisition with **$1 billion equity purchase price**:

*   Financing mix: 50% cash, 30% new debt, 20% stock
*   Cash used: **$500 million**
*   New debt raised: **$300 million** at 5% interest rate
*   Stock issued: **$200 million** / **$50** stock price = 4 million new shares

Annual interest expense on new debt: **$300 million** x 5% = **$15 million** pre-tax After-tax interest expense (at 25% tax rate): **$15 million** x (1 - 0.25) = **$11.25 million**

Foregone interest on cash (assuming 3% reinvestment rate): **$500 million** x 3% x (1 - 0.25) = **$11.25 million** after-tax

These financing costs reduce **pro forma earnings** and must be incorporated into accretion/dilution analysis.

Step 3: Purchase Price Allocation
---------------------------------

### Understanding Goodwill

When an acquirer pays more than the **fair value of net identifiable assets**, the excess is recorded as **goodwill** on the combined balance sheet. This accounting treatment is required under acquisition accounting (ASC 805).

Goodwill\=Purchase Price−Fair Value of Net Assets Acquired\\text{Goodwill} = \\text{Purchase Price} - \\text{Fair Value of Net Assets Acquired}Goodwill\=Purchase Price−Fair Value of Net Assets Acquired

Goodwill represents intangible value including brand recognition, customer relationships, assembled workforce, and expected synergies that cannot be separately identified and valued. For a deeper look at this accounting treatment, see our guide on [goodwill and intangible assets in M&A](https://ibinterviewquestions.com/blog/goodwill-intangibles-ma-accounting)
.

Goodwill

The excess of the purchase price paid for a company over the fair value of its net identifiable assets. Goodwill is recorded as an intangible asset on the acquirer's balance sheet and is tested annually for impairment rather than being amortized.

### Fair Value Adjustments

Purchase price allocation requires **stepping up target assets to fair value**. Common adjustments include:

**Property, plant, and equipment**: Appraised value may differ from book value, particularly for real estate or specialized equipment.

**Intangible assets**: Identify and value customer relationships, technology, trade names, and other intangibles not on the target's balance sheet.

**Inventory**: Step up to fair value, which may create **temporary margin compression** as higher-cost inventory flows through cost of goods sold.

**Deferred revenue**: Write down to fair value representing remaining fulfillment costs, which reduces revenue recognition post-close.

These adjustments affect pro forma earnings through changed depreciation, amortization, and temporary purchase accounting effects.

### Example Purchase Price Allocation

Target company has:

*   Book value of net assets: **$400 million**
*   Fair value adjustments: +**$100 million** (PP&E step-up, identified intangibles)
*   Fair value of net assets: **$500 million**
*   Purchase price: **$800 million**

Goodwill created: **$800 million** - **$500 million** = **$300 million**

The **$100 million** in fair value adjustments will generate additional depreciation and amortization expense that reduces pro forma earnings.

Step 4: Model Synergies
-----------------------

### Types of Synergies

[Synergies](https://ibinterviewquestions.com/blog/synergies-in-ma-revenue-vs-cost)
 are the value created by combining two companies that would not exist if they remained separate. Properly modeling synergies is critical because they often determine whether a deal is **accretive or dilutive**.

**Cost synergies** include:

*   Headcount reductions from eliminating duplicate functions
*   Facility consolidation and real estate savings
*   Procurement savings from increased purchasing power
*   Technology and systems integration savings
*   Reduced public company costs (for take-private transactions)

**Revenue synergies** include:

*   Cross-selling opportunities across combined customer bases
*   Geographic expansion using combined distribution
*   Product bundling and enhanced offerings
*   Pricing power from reduced competition

Cost synergies are generally **more credible and achievable** than revenue synergies. Models typically give full credit to cost synergies while applying haircuts to revenue synergy assumptions.

### Timing and Costs

Synergies do not appear immediately. Most models assume synergies **phase in over two to three years** as integration progresses:

*   Year 1: 25-50% of total synergies realized
*   Year 2: 75-90% of total synergies realized
*   Year 3: 100% of total synergies realized

Achieving synergies requires **integration costs** including severance payments, facility closure costs, systems integration expenses, and consulting fees. These one-time costs should be modeled separately from ongoing synergy benefits.

### Synergy Impact on Accretion/Dilution

Synergies improve pro forma earnings, making deals more accretive (or less dilutive). For accretion/dilution analysis, add **after-tax synergies** to combined net income:

Synergy Benefit\=Pre-tax Synergies×(1−Tax Rate)\\text{Synergy Benefit} = \\text{Pre-tax Synergies} \\times (1 - \\text{Tax Rate})Synergy Benefit\=Pre-tax Synergies×(1−Tax Rate)

A deal that is 5% dilutive before synergies might become 3% accretive with **$50 million** in annual cost synergies. This explains why synergy assumptions are among the **most scrutinized elements** of merger analysis.

Step 5: Build Pro Forma Income Statement
----------------------------------------

### Combining the Companies

The [pro forma income statement](https://ibinterviewquestions.com/blog/pro-forma-financial-statements-ma-guide)
 **adds together the two companies' financials** with adjustments for transaction effects:

**Pro Forma Revenue** = Acquirer revenue + Target revenue + Revenue synergies

**Pro Forma Operating Expenses** = Acquirer expenses + Target expenses - Cost synergies + D&A from fair value step-ups

**Pro Forma Operating Income** = Pro forma revenue - Pro forma operating expenses

**Pro Forma Interest Expense** = Acquirer interest + Target interest + New debt interest - Interest income foregone on cash used

**Pro Forma Pre-Tax Income** = Operating income - Interest expense

**Pro Forma Net Income** = Pre-tax income x (1 - Tax rate)

Pro Forma Financial Statements

Financial statements that combine the acquirer's and target's historical or projected financials, adjusted for transaction effects such as new financing costs, foregone interest, purchase accounting adjustments, and expected synergies. Pro forma statements show what the combined entity's earnings would look like as if the deal had already closed.

### Calculating Pro Forma EPS

With pro forma net income calculated, determine **pro forma EPS**:

Pro Forma EPS\=Pro Forma Net IncomePro Forma Shares Outstanding\\text{Pro Forma EPS} = \\frac{\\text{Pro Forma Net Income}}{\\text{Pro Forma Shares Outstanding}}Pro Forma EPS\=Pro Forma Shares OutstandingPro Forma Net Income​

Where pro forma shares = Acquirer shares outstanding + New shares issued to target shareholders

Compare pro forma EPS to acquirer standalone EPS to determine accretion or dilution:

Accretion/(Dilution)\=Pro Forma EPS−Standalone EPSStandalone EPS\\text{Accretion/(Dilution)} = \\frac{\\text{Pro Forma EPS} - \\text{Standalone EPS}}{\\text{Standalone EPS}}Accretion/(Dilution)\=Standalone EPSPro Forma EPS−Standalone EPS​

A positive percentage indicates accretion; negative indicates dilution.

Step 6: Sensitivity Analysis
----------------------------

### Key Variables to Test

Merger models require **sensitivity analysis** around key assumptions because small changes can flip deals from accretive to dilutive. Build sensitivity tables testing:

**Purchase price sensitivity**: How does accretion/dilution change at different premium levels? This helps negotiate appropriate deal terms.

**Financing mix sensitivity**: Compare all-cash, all-stock, and various mixed scenarios. Identify the optimal structure given current conditions.

**Synergy sensitivity**: What synergy level is required for the deal to be accretive? How sensitive are results to synergy timing assumptions?

**Interest rate sensitivity**: If financing with debt, how do different rate scenarios affect results?

### Building Sensitivity Tables

Create **two-way sensitivity tables** showing accretion/dilution across combinations of key variables. For example:

**Purchase price vs. synergies**: Rows show different premium levels, columns show different synergy amounts, cells show resulting accretion/dilution percentage.

**Cash % vs. stock %**: Holding total price constant, show how different financing mixes affect EPS impact.

These tables help clients and deal teams understand the **range of potential outcomes** and make informed decisions about transaction structure. Understanding how to present and interpret sensitivity analysis is essential for [accretion/dilution interview questions](https://ibinterviewquestions.com/blog/accretion-dilution-analysis-guide)
.

Common Interview Questions
--------------------------

### Walk Through Building a Merger Model

"Start with transaction assumptions including purchase price, financing mix, and synergies. Calculate the purchase consideration and how it will be funded across cash, debt, and stock. Perform purchase price allocation to determine goodwill. Build pro forma financials by combining the two companies and adjusting for financing costs, foregone interest, and synergies. Calculate pro forma EPS and compare to acquirer standalone EPS to determine accretion or dilution."

### What Makes a Deal Accretive vs Dilutive?

"The key driver is the relationship between what you pay for earnings and what your own earnings are worth. If the acquirer's P/E is higher than the target's effective P/E (including premium), stock deals tend to be accretive because you're exchanging expensive currency for cheaper earnings. Cash deals are accretive if the target's earnings yield exceeds the after-tax cost of financing. Synergies improve accretion by adding earnings without adding shares."

### How Do Synergies Affect Accretion/Dilution?

"Synergies increase pro forma net income without increasing shares outstanding, so they make deals more accretive. A deal that's dilutive before synergies can become accretive once synergy benefits are included. This is why synergy assumptions are heavily scrutinized, because aggressive synergy projections can make almost any deal appear accretive on paper."

### Why Might a Company Pursue a Dilutive Deal?

"Short-term dilution may be acceptable if the deal creates long-term strategic value. Reasons include: acquiring critical technology or capabilities, eliminating a competitor, gaining market share in attractive markets, defensive moves to prevent a competitor from acquiring the target, or expected future accretion once synergies fully materialize. Management must articulate a **compelling strategic rationale** to justify near-term dilution."

Common Mistakes to Avoid
------------------------

### Modeling Errors

Several technical mistakes frequently appear in merger models:

**Forgetting to tax-effect adjustments**: Interest expense, synergies, and other adjustments must be calculated on an **after-tax basis** since they affect pre-tax income.

**Double-counting synergies**: If synergies are already reflected in projections, do not add them again as a separate line item.

**Ignoring purchase accounting effects**: Fair value step-ups create additional D&A that reduces pro forma earnings.

**Miscounting shares**: Ensure you correctly calculate new shares issued based on the stock consideration and acquirer's share price.

**Forgetting foregone interest**: Cash used for the acquisition was previously earning interest income that is now lost.

### Conceptual Errors

Beyond mechanics, avoid these conceptual mistakes:

**Assuming accretion equals value creation**: A deal can be accretive but still destroy value if the acquirer overpays. Accretion/dilution is **one metric**, not the complete picture.

**Ignoring integration risk**: Models assume clean execution, but integration challenges can prevent synergy realization and create unexpected costs.

**Using unrealistic synergies**: Aggressive synergy assumptions make any deal look attractive. Credible models use **conservative, well-supported synergy estimates**.

Key Takeaways
-------------

*   A merger model analyzes **EPS impact** of an acquisition by combining two companies' financials into pro forma statements
*   Deals are **accretive** if pro forma EPS exceeds standalone EPS; **dilutive** if pro forma EPS is lower
*   Key drivers include **relative P/E multiples, purchase premium, financing mix, and synergies**
*   **Purchase price allocation** determines goodwill and fair value adjustments affecting pro forma earnings
*   **Synergies** improve accretion by adding earnings without adding shares; cost synergies are more credible than revenue synergies
*   **Financing mix** involves trade-offs: cash preserves shares but sacrifices interest income; stock preserves cash but dilutes shareholders
*   **Sensitivity analysis** tests how accretion/dilution changes across different assumptions
*   For interviews, understand the **mechanics, drivers, and how to explain accretion/dilution** conceptually

Conclusion
----------

Building a merger model requires combining **technical financial modeling skills** with strategic understanding of deal dynamics. The mechanics follow a logical flow: gather assumptions, structure financing, allocate purchase price, model synergies, build pro forma financials, and analyze accretion/dilution. Each step involves judgment calls about assumptions that significantly impact results.

For investment banking interviews, you must understand both the how and the why of merger modeling. Being able to build a model is necessary but not sufficient. Interviewers expect you to explain **why deals are accretive or dilutive**, how different structures affect results, and what strategic considerations might justify accepting short-term dilution.

The best way to develop merger modeling proficiency is practice. Build models using public transaction announcements, varying assumptions to see how results change. This hands-on experience develops the **intuition** that allows you to anticipate results and explain dynamics clearly in interviews.
