Accretion Expense Accounting Explained with Full Example and Journal Entries
============================================================================

by [Ben Belk, Lease Accounting Consultant](https://finquery.com/author/ben-belk/ "Posts by Ben Belk, Lease Accounting Consultant")
 | Nov 11, 2021

What is accretion in accounting?
--------------------------------

Accretion is the periodic recognition of an expense associated with an increase [in the present value](https://finquery.com/blog/how-to-calculate-present-value-of-future-lease-payments-in-excel/)
 of a liability over time. This expense is recognized as an [operating expense](https://finquery.com/blog/capex-vs-opex/)
 in the statement of income. In practice, accretion expense is commonly recognized in relation to an [asset retirement obligation](https://finquery.com/blog/asset-retirement-obligation-aro-accounting-asc-410/)
 (AROs).

An ARO is a liability established for the removal of [fixed assets](https://finquery.com/blog/what-are-fixed-assets/)
 such as property, equipment, or [leasehold improvements](https://finquery.com/blog/leasehold-improvements-accounting-amortization-us-gaap/)
 at the [termination of the lease](https://finquery.com/blog/lease-termination-accounting/)
. For example, if a company enters into a lease for a building, constructs leasehold improvements, and determines based on the provisions of the lease it is legally obligated to remove the leasehold improvements at the end of the lease, then the [company has an ARO liability](https://finquery.com/blog/asset-retirement-obligation-aro-accounting-asc-410/)
. An ARO is recorded as a credit in the amount of the present value of the estimated cost to return the building to its original condition when the leasehold improvements are retired, offset with a debit to the leasehold improvements asset, which is then depreciated over the shorter of the asset’s useful life or the remaining [lease term](https://finquery.com/blog/determining-correct-dates-lease-term-asc-842/)
. Accretion expense is recognized in subsequent periods as the present value of the ARO is increased, or accreted, to the actual cash value to be paid out at the asset’s future retirement date.

Per [ASC 410](https://finquery.com/blog/asset-retirement-obligation-aro-accounting-asc-410/)
 _Asset Retirement and Environmental Obligations_, section 20 (ASC 410-20-35-1), if multiple events occur to impact the value of the ARO, the value of the ARO is adjusted. Reductions to the ARO liability estimate require adjusting the current ARO liability and accretion schedule. Increases to the ARO liability estimate are considered additional layers. Each layer of the liability is recognized at its fair value, which is the same as present value, in the period in which the event causing the change occurred. For example, an US oil company might incur an ARO liability for returning leased land to its original condition. Midway through the lease, the US and Canada reach a trade halt which forces the oil company to find an alternative provider of peat moss, a common crop used to help clean up leaking oil, increasing the estimated costs of returning the land to its original condition. Measurement and recognition of additional ARO liability layers will be required if the company has to pay more than initially expected.

### Accretion vs. amortization

Accretion is not the same thing as amortization. Amortization is an accounting technique best conceptualized as [depreciation](https://finquery.com/blog/straight-line-method-depreciation-explained-example/)
 for intangible assets. It is used to periodically reduce the value of these types of assets over an estimated useful life. Amortization generally applies to leased [right-of-use assets](https://finquery.com/blog/right-of-use-asset-lease-liability-asc-842-ifrs-16-gasb-87/)
, patents, trademarks, loans, and other non-physical assets.

On the other hand, accretion is the accounting treatment used when a liability has been discounted to present value at initial recognition and subsequently increased over a period of time. This applies to AROs, such as [environmental remediation liabilities which result from normal business operations](https://finquery.com/blog/environmental-liabilities-accounting-vs-asset-retirement-obligations-asc-410-30/)
, and represent an expected future payment by a [lessee](https://finquery.com/blog/lessee-vs-lessor-differences-accounting-and-more-explained/)
 to restore their leased asset back to its original or non-specialized condition.

The journal entry to recognize amortization expense of $50,000 would be a debit to amortization expense, offset by a credit to the asset being amortized:

![Amortization expense journal entry](https://finquery.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/50000-amortization-expense-intangible-asset.png)

A helpful way to think about amortization expense and how it differs from accretion expense is to look at its effect on your financial statements. An illustration of the entry shows a debit/decrease to equity, caused by the debit to an expense account, and a credit/decrease to the asset account:

![Recognition of amortization expense](https://finquery.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/recognition-amortization-expense.png)

The journal entry to recognize accretion expense of $50,000 is a debit to accretion expense, offset by a credit to the associated liability:

![Accretion expense journal entry](https://finquery.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/50000-accretion-expense-asset-retirement-liability.png)

Below is an illustration of the financial statement effect of an entry recognizing accretion expense showing a debit/decrease to equity, caused by the debit to an expense account, and a credit/increase to the liability account:

![Recognition of accretion expense](https://finquery.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/recognition-accretion-expense.png)

As seen in the financial statement impact illustrations, both techniques debit (increase) an expense, which reduces equity. The difference is the offsetting credit for amortization expense decreases the value of an asset, and the offsetting credit for an accretion expense increases the value of a liability.

Example: Accretion accounting for an ARO liability with journal entries
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Below is an example of how to account for accretion expense with journal entries, including an event during the lease which leads to an incremental liability that must be recognized as an additional layer.

Benjam, Inc. leases a building for 5 years to host their annual awards shows and other company events. The lease runs from January 1, 2017 through December 31, 2021, with an annual payment of $1,000,000.

**Key Lease Information**

**Payment Terms:** $1,000,000 / year

**Start Date:** January 1, 2017

**End Date:** December 31, 2021

**Rate:** 4% [discount rate](https://finquery.com/blog/discount-rate-implicit-in-the-lease-asc-842/)

At lease inception, the company informs the owners of the building they would like to install different smart accessories to accommodate their proprietary technology. It will cost an estimated $50,000 at the end of the lease to remove the accessories and restore the building to its original condition. The present value of this $50,000 obligation in 5 years time at a 4% annual rate must first be calculated using [LeaseQuery’s free downloadable present value calculator tool](https://finquery.com/project/present-value-calculation-tool/)
:

![Present value calculator](https://finquery.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/present-value-tool-50000-5-years.png)

[![Present Value Calculator](https://finquery.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/11/PresentValueCalculator.jpg)](https://finquery.com/project/present-value-calculation-tool/)

The $41,096 represents the present value of the estimated $50,000 asset retirement obligation to be paid at a future date, and is recorded on January 1, 2017 as follows:

![Present value of asset retirement obligation journal entry](https://finquery.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/present-value-aro-liability-lease-commencement.png)

At the end of each year, Benjam, Inc. records accretion expense as the present value of the expected future liability accretes to the full $50,000 payment. The accretion schedule is shown in the table below:

![ARO accretion schedule](https://finquery.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/aro-accretion-schedule-5-years.png)

The journal entry to record accretion at December 31, 2017 is as follows:

![Journal entry to record accretion](https://finquery.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/accretion-december-2017-journal-entry.png)

On January 1, 2019, the start of the third lease year, Benjam, Inc. made a major technological breakthrough and quickly updated their leased building accordingly. The updates increased the expected future cost of the asset retirement from $50,000 to $300,000. Given this new information, the corporation must update their recognition of the present value of the expected future asset retirement obligation. First, Benjam, Inc. calculates the present value of the additional $250,000 expected future expenditure as follows:

![Present value calculator](https://finquery.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/present-value-tool-additional-future-expenditure.png)

As discussed above, **ASC 410-20-35-1** states incremental liabilities incurred are considered an additional layer of the liability, and recognized at fair value in each subsequent period.

As such, Benjam, Inc. must recognize the present value of the second layer of ARO liability while continuing to accrete the first layer as normal. The following entry would be made on January 1, 2019 to record the present value of the additional ARO liability layer for the present value of the $250,000:

![ARO journal entry](https://finquery.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/present-value-additional-250k-aro-journal-entry.png)

Below is the accretion schedule for the complete ARO. Going forward, Benjamin, Inc. will have to keep track of accretion expense for both layers:

![ARO accretion schedule](https://finquery.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/combined-aro-accretion-schedule.png)

Benjam, Inc. will record an accretion entry for the combined total of both layers at December 31, 2019:

![Journal entry to record periodic accretion expense](https://finquery.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/combined-accretion-2019-journal-entry.png)

At the end of their lease on December 31, 2021, Benjam, Inc. has fully accreted the expected future liability of $300,000. The actual cost to remove their [leasehold improvements](https://finquery.com/blog/leasehold-improvements-accounting-amortization-us-gaap/)
 from the building is $300,000 and Benjam, Inc. makes the following entry to pay for the retirement costs and relieve the liability:

![ARO payment journal entry](https://finquery.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/payment-aro-2021-journal-entry.png)

In this instance the asset retirement costs equaled the estimated liability. However if the actual costs to remove the leasehold improvements were less or more than the estimated costs, the difference would have been recorded as a gain or loss, respectively, on the income statement.

Summary
-------

As you can see, properly accounting for accretion liabilities and AROs, even in this simple example, is technical and challenging. In the real world, added complexities of making improvements to your leased assets, accurately estimating the cost to retire those assets at termination, and multiple updates to your original estimates exist.
