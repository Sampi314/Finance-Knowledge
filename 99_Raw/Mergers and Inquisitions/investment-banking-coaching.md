The Problem with Finding a Job In Investment Banking Today…
-----------------------------------------------------------

Ten years ago, before I successfully broke into the finance industry, I was stuck in limbo.

Career-wise, I was spinning my wheels… here’s where I was at:

*   I had stellar grades… so far, so good…
*   I had solid, relevant experience – the type they’d be looking for…
*   And I applied to over **200** jobs…
*   Got around **100** interviews over multiple rounds…
*   And made it into **7** final rounds, winning **2** full-time offers.

Not a bad result, right?

**Wrong.**

See, plenty of friends who had _worse_ grades and _less_ experience than me had a **MUCH** higher success rate.

Some people simply applied to the exact jobs they wanted and got them – often with success rates of close to 100%.

But I was sitting there “fighting over the scraps” and working **way** too hard for the results I was getting.

Oh, and here’s the punch line: **this was back when the market was good**.

This was when things were _far_ less competitive, when there were more spots open, and when banks were still “expanding aggressively.”

**If I were to follow this same strategy today, I wouldn’t have made it to any final rounds.**

In the end, things worked out OK: I worked at banks like **JP Morgan** and **Credit Suisse** in roles ranging from investment banking to sales to private banking.

But I made things _far_ more difficult on myself than I had to.

Let’s be honest: generic career coaches are **useless** in this industry because “the standard advice” just doesn’t apply.

Here are some of the hilariously awful things they’ll tell you if you ask them for “advice”:

*   **Always include a “Summary and Objectives” section on your resume / CV!** (Horrible idea, unless you’re very senior with 10-15+ years of experience)
*   **Make sure you touch on everything in your resume / CV when introducing yourself** (Completely false… it’s called “telling your story” for a reason – it’s a story, not a recitation of your resume)
*   **Always do something for others before asking them for anything in return, like referrals or career advice** (This doesn’t work well when you’re first entering the industry – how can you really help a pro? Nice idea, but it doesn’t hold up in practice)
*   **Always “ping” your contacts with links and articles and anything else you can think of** just to stay in touch every few weeks (People in finance are busy and don’t have time for this – yes, stay in touch, but ask something specific and be direct)
*   **Your technical skills and knowledge of financial modeling don’t matter that much** – they’re looking for “soft skills” (Maybe this was true in 2005… not so much anymore. Yes, soft skills matter but they WILL grill you on the technical side as well)

So you can’t bother with the generic coaching services – unless you also want “generic” results, AKA no job offers.
