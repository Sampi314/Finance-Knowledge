_Short definition:_ [Free cash flow (FCF)](https://www.financial-modeling.com/glossar/free-cash-flow/)
 is the cash flow generated by a company’s operations that is available for distribution to investors (both debt and [equity](https://www.financial-modeling.com/glossar/equity/)
 holders) after accounting for capital expenditures.  

_Explanation:_ FCF represents the cash a company has left over after paying for its [operating expenses](https://www.financial-modeling.com/glossar/operating-expenses/)
 and investments in its business. It is a key metric for investors as it indicates a company’s ability to generate cash to pay [dividends](https://www.financial-modeling.com/glossar/dividends/)
, reduce debt, or reinvest in growth opportunities.

_Example:_ FCF can be calculated in different ways, but a common formula is:

FCF = Operating Cash Flow – Capital Expenditures

_Additional information (optional):_ There are two main types of FCF: [levered free cash flow (LFCF)](https://www.financial-modeling.com/glossar/levered-free-cash-flow/)
 and [unlevered free cash flow (UFCF)](https://www.financial-modeling.com/glossar/unlevered-free-cash-flow/)
. LFCF considers the cash flow available to equity holders after debt obligations have been met, while UFCF represents the cash flow available to all investors before any debt payments.
