Break the Conglomerate Discount: Building a Sum-of-the-Parts (SOTP) Valuation That Stands Up in Investment Committees
=====================================================================================================================

![](https://www.financial-modeling.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/sum-of-the-parts-valuation.jpg)

A Sum-of-the-Parts (SOTP) [valuation](https://www.financial-modeling.com/glossar/valuation/)
 decomposes a diversified company into distinct business units, values each segment using the most appropriate methodology, and recombines them into an [equity value](https://www.financial-modeling.com/glossar/equity-value/)
. When built rigorously, SOTP reveals hidden value, supports strategic decisions, and withstands scrutiny from lenders, auditors, and investment committees.

Why SOTP matters in real transactions
-------------------------------------

In complex groups, a single blended multiple often obscures economic reality. High-growth divisions, capital-intensive units, and non-core assets behave differently — and markets price them differently.  
  
SOTP isolates value drivers, clarifies capital allocation decisions, and provides a transparent bridge from operating forecasts to equity value.

Step 1 — Define the perimeter and segment logic
-----------------------------------------------

Start with a clear segmentation framework aligned with how the business is managed and reported.  
  
Typical segmentation approaches include:

*   Business line (products or services)
*   Geography
*   [Asset](https://www.financial-modeling.com/glossar/asset/)
     class
*   Regulatory perimeter
*   Operating vs. non-operating assets

Consistency with segment reporting improves credibility and auditability.

Step 2 — Normalize financials for each business unit
----------------------------------------------------

Before applying valuation techniques, normalize segment financials to reflect sustainable performance.  
  
Key adjustments typically include:

*   Removing non-recurring items
*   Allocating shared costs using defensible drivers
*   Separating corporate overhead
*   Adjusting for IFRS 16 lease impacts
*   Aligning accounting policies across units

The goal is comparability — not perfection.

Step 3 — Select the appropriate valuation methodology per segment
-----------------------------------------------------------------

A robust SOTP rarely relies on a single method. Instead, methodologies are chosen based on business characteristics.  
  
Common approaches include:

*   **DCF (Discounted Cash Flow)** for stable or forecastable units
*   **Trading multiples** (EV/EBITDA, EV/EBIT) for comparable businesses
*   **Transaction multiples** where relevant precedent deals exist
*   **NAV (Net Asset Value)** for asset-heavy divisions
*   **Sum of invested capital** for early-stage segments

Matching method to economic reality is critical for credibility.

In practice: Where most models fail
-----------------------------------

Many SOTP models collapse because corporate costs are treated inconsistently.  
  
In real engagements, the most robust approach is to clearly distinguish between:

*   Truly eliminable costs
*   Strategic holding costs
*   Temporary transition costs

This distinction directly affects equity value and negotiation dynamics.  
  
**If your model is used in a transaction context, this is where scrutiny will focus first.**

Step 4 — Apply valuation and build segment bridges
--------------------------------------------------

Each segment should have a transparent valuation bridge from operating metrics to enterprise value.  
  
This typically includes:

*   Forecast drivers
*   Margin evolution
*   Capital expenditure assumptions
*   [Working capital](https://www.financial-modeling.com/glossar/working-capital/)
     dynamics
*   Terminal value logic

Clear bridges improve model auditability and stakeholder confidence.

Step 5 — Consolidate to enterprise value
----------------------------------------

After valuing each segment, aggregate values and adjust for:

*   Corporate center value
*   Net debt
*   Pension liabilities
*   Minority interests
*   Associates and joint ventures
*   Non-operating assets

The consolidation bridge should reconcile cleanly to equity value.

**Building SOTP models for decision-making or transactions requires absolute transparency in assumptions and structure.**  
A robust model is not just accurate — it is explainable.

Advanced modeling considerations professionals include
------------------------------------------------------

To build a model that stands up under diligence, advanced practitioners incorporate:

*   Conglomerate discount [sensitivity analysis](https://www.financial-modeling.com/glossar/sensitivity-analysis/)
    
*   Scenario matrices (base / upside / downside)
*   WACC calibration by segment
*   Tax attribute modeling
*   Cross-holdings adjustments
*   Control premium considerations

These layers significantly improve decision usefulness.

Common pitfalls that undermine credibility
------------------------------------------

Even technically correct models can lose trust if they lack clarity.  
  
Frequent issues include:

*   Overly complex allocation keys
*   Inconsistent forecast horizons
*   Double counting synergies
*   Mixing nominal and real assumptions
*   Poor documentation

Clarity is as important as precision.

Frequently asked questions about SOTP valuation
-----------------------------------------------

### When is SOTP most appropriate?

SOTP is most useful for diversified companies with distinct business units that have different growth, risk, or capital intensity profiles.

### How do you handle corporate costs?

They should be clearly separated into recurring holding costs and eliminable costs, with transparent allocation logic.

### Does SOTP always create higher valuation?

Not necessarily. It reveals value drivers — which can lead to higher or lower valuations depending on segment performance.

### How do you justify a conglomerate discount?

Through sensitivity analysis, market evidence, and structural factors such as complexity, [liquidity](https://www.financial-modeling.com/glossar/liquidity/)
, and governance.  
  
**If you are building valuation models for strategic decisions, investors, or transactions, rigor and transparency determine credibility.**  
Well-structured models enable better decisions — and better outcomes.
