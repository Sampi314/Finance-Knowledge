The Latest Changes To Transfer Pricing Guidelines In Singapore
==============================================================

by [Editor](https://www.maxlewis.com.sg/author/impeditor/ "Posts by Editor")
 | Jul 25, 2024 | [Transfer Pricing](https://www.maxlewis.com.sg/category/transfer-pricing/)

![The Latest Changes To Transfer Pricing Guidelines In Singapore](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20width='640'%20height='427'%20viewBox='0%200%20640%20427'%3E%3C/svg%3E)

In a significant move aimed at enhancing compliance and aligning with international standards, the Inland Revenue Authority of Singapore (IRAS) has released the 7th Edition of the Singapore Transfer Pricing Guidelines (7th Ed TPG) on June 14, 2024. This update comes after a hiatus of nearly three years and introduces several critical changes to transfer pricing documentation (TPD) requirements, enforcement mechanisms, and procedural guidelines.

Here’s a detailed look at the latest changes to [transfer pricing in Singapore](https://www.maxlewis.com.sg/our-services/transfer-pricing/ "Transfer Pricing Singapore")
 and what they mean for businesses operating here.

Increased Exemption Thresholds for Transfer Pricing Documentation
-----------------------------------------------------------------

One of the most notable [changes in the 7th Ed TPG](https://www.maxlewis.com.sg/changes-to-singapores-transfer-pricing-rules-from-ya-2026/ "Changes To Singapore’s Transfer Pricing Rules From YA 2026")
 is the increase in the exemption threshold for TPD compliance. Effective from the Year of Assessment (YA) 2026, the threshold for certain related party transactions will rise from SGD 1 million to SGD 2 million. This change applies to service fees, royalties, license fees, guarantee fees, and other transactions, excluding purchases, sales, and loans, which retain the SGD 15 million threshold.

The increased exemption threshold aims to alleviate the compliance burden on businesses with smaller amounts of related party transactions. For instance, a company with related party service income of SGD 1.5 million, previously required to prepare TPD, will now be exempt under the new threshold. However, businesses must still ensure that their related party transactions are conducted at arm’s length, substantiating this with adequate transfer pricing analysis to avoid scrutiny from IRAS.

Elimination of the Simplified Approach for Interest Restriction
---------------------------------------------------------------

The 7th Ed TPG removes the simplified approach of using interest restriction as a proxy for arm’s length pricing for domestic related party loans from January 1, 2025. Instead, taxpayers must rely on the indicative margin or conduct their analysis to determine the appropriate arm’s length interest rates for such loans. This change aligns the treatment of domestic loans with that of cross-border loans, where the indicative margin cannot be relied upon for loans exceeding SGD 15 million.

This shift underscores IRAS’s commitment to enforcing the arm’s length principle (ALP) more rigorously. While the removal of the simplified approach may increase compliance costs for taxpayers, it ensures that interest income and expenses are accurately reported, preventing potential understatement or overstatement.

Tightened Transfer Pricing Enforcement
--------------------------------------

The 7th Ed TPG introduces significant changes to the transfer pricing audit (TPA) process. Under the new guidelines, IRAS will now impose transfer pricing adjustments and surcharges upfront, requiring taxpayers to file formal objections if they disagree. Previously, IRAS would discuss proposed adjustments with taxpayers before making assessments.

Additionally, the new guidelines stipulate that taxpayers must date their simplified TPD each year to qualify for using the first year’s TPD as Qualifying Past TPD (QTPD) for the subsequent two years. This ensures that taxpayers review and update their related party transactions annually.

The revised TPA process highlights the importance of maintaining robust and contemporaneous TPD. Businesses must be proactive in preparing their TPD to support their transfer pricing positions, as failure to do so can result in immediate adjustments and penalties, affecting cash flow and financial stability.

Streamlined Mutual Agreement Procedure (MAP) Process
----------------------------------------------------

The 7th Ed TPG also streamlines the MAP process, a facility for resolving tax and transfer pricing disputes between IRAS and foreign tax authorities. The new process eliminates the need for a pre-filing meeting, reducing the steps from five to four: Submission of MAP application, IRAS’s evaluation, review and negotiation, and implementation.

This change aims to expedite the resolution of transfer pricing disputes, allowing more cases to be managed efficiently. According to the OECD’s FY 2022 MAP statistics, Singapore’s average time for resolving MAP cases was 21.2 months, below the OECD average of 28.9 months. The streamlined process aligns with IRAS’s goal of improving dispute resolution capabilities and adhering to international standards.

Additional Guidance in Key Areas
--------------------------------

The 7th Ed TPG incorporates new guidance on several critical areas:

**1\.** **Working Capital Adjustments:** Introduces a new FAQ on making working capital adjustments during comparability analysis, emphasising their acceptability when they improve the reliability of the analysis.

**2\.** **Financial Transactions:** Provides guidance on transitioning from Interbank Offered Rates (IBOR) to Risk-Free Rates (RFR) and the need for annual reviews of long-term loans.

**3\.** **Strict Pass-Through Costs:** Clarifies conditions under which group service providers can pass on costs without a mark-up, including the need for written agreements, which can now be in the form of email correspondences.

**4\.** **Government Assistance:** Reaffirms the treatment of government assistance benefits for transfer pricing purposes, expecting taxpayers to retain these benefits unless independent parties would share them.

**5\.** **Exemption Rules for Related Party Domestic Loans:** Effective from January 1, 2025, new exemption rules apply to related party domestic loans. Taxpayers must adhere to updated regulations for arm’s length pricing and documentation. 

Transfer Pricing Adjustments and Capital Transactions
-----------------------------------------------------

The guidelines now include enhanced examples and detailed guidance on transfer pricing adjustments relating to capital transactions. This addition provides clarity on how such adjustments should be handled to maintain compliance with the arm’s length principle.

Partial or Full Remission of Surcharge
--------------------------------------

Section 34E of the ITA outlines circumstances under which partial or full remission of the surcharge will be granted. This update ensures that businesses are aware of potential relief measures available during compliance assessments.

Base Reference Rates with IBOR Reform
-------------------------------------

With the reform of Interbank Offered Rates (IBOR), guidance is provided on adopting base reference rates for related party loans. This ensures that businesses transition smoothly to Risk-Free Rates (RFR) and comply with new regulatory standards.

Conclusion
----------

The 7th Edition Singapore Transfer Pricing Guidelines represent a significant step towards stricter compliance and enforcement of transfer pricing rules in Singapore. By raising exemption thresholds, removing simplified approaches, tightening enforcement, and providing additional guidance, IRAS aims to align Singapore’s transfer pricing regime with global standards while managing the compliance burden for businesses. It is crucial for taxpayers to stay informed and ensure their transfer pricing practices are robust and well-documented to avoid penalties and support their positions in case of audits.
