Transfer pricing documentation report is a report that showing whether the transactions entered into between related companies within a group of companies are at arm’s length. Arm’s length means that the prices or margins of these intercompany transactions are comparable to what would have been entered into between outside or unrelated parties.  
Companies that are required to prepare Transfer Pricing documentation reports have annual revenue is at least S$10 million for each financial year and the related party transactions met the following threshold table:

| Category of transactions  <br>(A) | Total value #  <br>(S$)  <br>YA 2025 and before  <br>(B) | Total value #  <br>(S$)  <br>YA 2026 and onwards  <br>(C) | Meaning of value of transaction  <br>(D) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Purchase of goods by the taxpayer from a related party | 15 million | 15 million | Amount paid or payable by the taxpayer for the goods |
| Sale of goods by the taxpayer to a related party | 15 million | 15 million | Gross revenue derived by the taxpayer from the sale |
| Loan by the taxpayer to a related party | 15 million | 15 million | Principal amount of the loan |
| Loan to the taxpayer by a related party | 15 million | 15 million | Principal amount of the loan |
| Provision of service to the taxpayer by a related party | 1 million | 2 million | Amount paid or payable by the taxpayer for the provision, i.e. service fee expenses |
| Provision of service by the taxpayer to a related party | 1 million | 2 million | Gross revenue derived by the taxpayer from the provision, i.e. service fee income |
| Grant of a right to use movable property to the taxpayer by a related party | 1 million | 2 million | Amount paid or payable by the taxpayer for the grant, i.e. royalty expenses |
| Grant of a right to use movable property by the taxpayer to a related party | 1 million | 2 million | Gross revenue derived by the taxpayer from the grant, i.e. royalty income |
| Lease of any property to the taxpayer by a related party | 1 million | 2 million | Amount paid or payable by the taxpayer for the lease, i.e. rental expenses |
| Lease of any property by the taxpayer to a related party | 1 million | 2 million | Gross revenue derived by the taxpayer from the lease, i.e. rental income |
| Grant of a guarantee to the taxpayer by a related party | 1 million | 2 million | Amount paid or payable by the taxpayer for the grant, i.e. guarantee expenses |
| Grant of a guarantee by the taxpayer to a related party | 1 million | 2 million | Gross revenue derived by the taxpayer from the grant, i.e. guarantee income |
| Any other transaction ^ | 1 million | 2 million | Amount paid or payable by the taxpayer to the related party under the transaction, or gross revenue derived by the taxpayer from the related party under the transaction, as the case may be |

\# The value as disclosed in the financial accounts for the basis period.  
^ For the purpose of determining if the threshold is met, the aggregation here will be based on each category of other transactions.

Benchmarking studies play a crucial role in any transfer pricing documentation and are a necessary defence for compliance purposes. They are carried out to understand how a company prices its related party transactions, and seek to validate that their pricing was done at arm’s length by reference to independent and comparable transactions.

The purpose is to find out the general conditions of the transactions conducted between international related parties. These studies will generate a range of values: referred to as the mark-up range or arm’s length range . Statistically, the arm’s length range is defined by the upper quartile and lower quartile, comprising the range of values of price or profit attached to the related party transactions between unrelated parties that are in the same or similar circumstances.

If a transfer price or profit level lies outside of the applicable arm’s length range, the tax authority will take the median value.

**1\. Understand the Regulatory Framework**  
• Identify applicable rules: Review local transfer pricing legislation, OECD Transfer Pricing Guidelines, and relevant double taxation treaties.  
• Check thresholds: Some jurisdictions (including Singapore) require TP documentation only if certain revenue or related-party transaction thresholds are exceeded.  
• Stay updated: Regulations evolve (e.g., BEPS Action 13, CbCR, Master File, Local File), so continuous monitoring is essential.

**2\. Map Group Transactions**  
• Catalogue related parties: Identify all related entities in the corporate structure.  
• List intercompany transactions: Cover sales/purchases of goods, provision of services, royalties, loans, cash pooling, management fees, cost sharing, etc.  
• Quantify transaction values: Rank transactions by size and materiality to prioritise documentation focus.

**3\. Conduct a Functional Analysis (FAR)**  
• Functions: Determine which functions (e.g., R&D, manufacturing, marketing, distribution, treasury) each entity performs.  
• Assets: Identify tangible and intangible assets used, including intellectual property rights.  
• Risks: Map contractual vs. actual risks (market, credit, FX, warranty, inventory, etc.) borne by each entity.  
• Entity characterisation: Classify entities (e.g., limited-risk distributor, contract manufacturer, principal entrepreneur).  
\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_  
**4\. Select the Most Appropriate TP Method**  
• OECD-recognised methods:  
o Comparable Uncontrolled Price (CUP)  
o Resale Price Method (RPM)  
o Cost-Plus Method  
o Transactional Net Margin Method (TNMM)  
o Profit Split Method  
• **Method selection criteria**:  
o Nature of the transaction  
o Availability of reliable comparables  
o Functional/risk profile of the parties  
\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_  
**5\. Perform Benchmarking Analysis**  
• Identify comparables: Use commercial databases or public financials.  
• Apply screening filters: Industry classification, geography, turnover, functional similarity.  
• Calculate arm’s length range: Apply statistical measures (e.g., interquartile range) to determine acceptable profit margins or mark-ups.  
• Adjust for differences: Normalise comparables for working capital, risk, and accounting differences.

**6\. Prepare Transfer Pricing Documentation**  
• Master File: Provides group-level overview (structure, intangibles, financial activities, global TP policy).  
• Local File: Details local entity’s intercompany transactions, FAR analysis, method selection, benchmarking.  
• CbCR (if applicable): Country-by-country reporting for large MNE groups.  
• Defence file: Prepare supporting evidence such as intercompany agreements, invoices, and board approvals.  
\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_  
**7\. Implement Transfer Pricing in Practice**  
• Draft intercompany agreements: Ensure legal contracts reflect the TP policy.  
• Embed in accounting systems: Align ERP/accounting processes to record TP adjustments in real time.  
• Training: Educate finance, tax, and operations teams on applying TP charges consistently.  
• Monitor actuals vs. policy: Compare real margins against arm’s length benchmarks.  
\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_  
**8\. Review, Monitor, and Defend**  
• Annual review: Update benchmarking and documentation regularly, especially if transactions or functions change.  
• Audit readiness: Maintain clear evidence of benefits, cost allocations, and services provided.  
• Dispute management: Be prepared for tax authority reviews; consider APAs (Advance Pricing Agreements) for certainty.  
• Adjustments: Implement year-end true-ups to align results within the arm’s length range.

**9\. Practical Tips for Successful Implementation**  
• Keep TP policies simple, standardised, and aligned with actual business practices.  
• Avoid “copy-paste” benchmarking; always ensure local relevance.  
• Document substance: “If it’s not documented, it didn’t happen.”  
• Use TP as a strategic tool — not just compliance — to manage effective tax rates, capital allocation, and performance measurement.

**An Advance Pricing Agreement (APA)** is a formal arrangement between a taxpayer and one or more tax authorities that sets out the arm’s length pricing methodology for intercompany transactions over a fixed period of time.  
You typically need an APA when:

**1\. High-Risk or High-Value Transactions**  
• Large transaction values (e.g., royalties, service fees, or intercompany loans exceeding millions annually).  
• High-risk transaction types such as:  
o Royalty and IP licensing  
o Cost contribution arrangements (CCAs/CSAs)  
o Intra-group financing and cash pooling  
o Principal structures or supply chain hubs  
• Where disputes could lead to significant tax exposures or double taxation.

**2\. Complex Transfer Pricing Issues**  
• Transactions involving unique intangibles where reliable comparables are limited.  
• Highly integrated global supply chains with multiple jurisdictions involved.  
• New business models (e.g., digital platforms, biotech R&D sharing, fintech structures) where TP rules are still evolving.

**3\. Risk of Double Taxation**  
• When tax authorities in two or more jurisdictions may apply different TP adjustments to the same transaction.  
• Bilateral or multilateral APAs are especially useful to ensure both (or all) jurisdictions agree on a consistent approach.

• History of tax audits or disputes over similar transactions.  
• Desire to avoid protracted litigation, penalties, and reputational risk.  
• Cases where prior TP documentation has been repeatedly challenged.

• When the business requires certainty for long-term projects or investments (e.g., a 10-year IP licensing structure or cross-border financing arrangement).  
• For listed companies or investors, APAs can provide assurance on effective tax rate stability and compliance.

**Country-by-Country Reporting (CbCR)** is part of the OECD BEPS Action 13 framework and has been adopted by many jurisdictions, including Singapore. It requires large multinational enterprise (MNE) groups to provide a breakdown of their global operations by tax jurisdiction.

**You need to prepare and file CbCR when the following conditions apply:**

**1\. Group Revenue Threshold**

The MNE group’s consolidated revenue in the preceding financial year exceeds EUR 750 million (or equivalent in local currency).

For Singapore: threshold is S$1,125 million.

**2\. Type of Entity Required to File**

Ultimate Parent Entity (UPE):  
The entity at the top of the group hierarchy, resident in a jurisdiction that has implemented CbCR, must file the report.

Surrogate Parent Entity (SPE):  
If the UPE is in a jurisdiction that has not implemented CbCR, another group entity in a CbCR jurisdiction may be designated to file on behalf of the group.

Local Constituent Entity:  
May be required to file (secondary reporting obligation) if:

The UPE is in a non-CbCR jurisdiction, or

The jurisdiction does not have an information exchange agreement with the local tax authority.

**3\. Filing Deadlines**

Typically 12 months after the end of the reporting financial year (e.g., FY ending 31 Dec 2023 → CbCR due by 31 Dec 2024).

**4\. Information Required**

CbCR requires disclosure of, for each tax jurisdiction where the group operates:

Revenues (related-party and unrelated-party)

Profit (loss) before tax

Income tax paid and accrued

Stated capital and accumulated earnings

Number of employees

Tangible assets other than cash or equivalents

And a listing of:

All constituent entities resident in each tax jurisdiction

The main business activity(ies) of each entity

**5\. Key Triggers for Needing CbCR**

You need CbCR if:

Your MNE group exceeds the revenue threshold.

You are the UPE in a CbCR jurisdiction.

You are appointed as a Surrogate Parent Entity.

You are a local entity in a jurisdiction where the UPE is not reporting and no exchange mechanism exists.

### What is transfer pricing?

Transfer pricing is establishing appropriate prices between two or more related enterprises as if they are independent, unconnected third parties. This process is referred to as the arm’s length principle.

These enterprises are related when they have pre-existing connections that make them interdependent. These connections can be either direct or indirect. The two enterprises can be related as wholly-owned or majority-owned subsidiaries, or affiliates with shareholdings held by another group or company, or they can be commonly controlled by a larger, overarching enterprise.

 Between these related enterprises, they may exchange goods and services such as sales or purchase of goods, licensing of software rights or secondment of manpower services under each enterprise.

### Why is transfer pricing important?

[Transfer pricing rules](https://www.maxlewis.com.sg/changes-to-singapores-transfer-pricing-rules-from-ya-2026/)
 are meant to promote international trade and ensure a level playing field in the competition for tax revenue between countries while preventing tax evasion or abusive practices.

A business is liable to enter legal trouble or penalties if the matter of Transfer Pricing were to be ignored, no matter if one’s business is an SME or a multinational corporation.

### What are some of the guidelines of transfer pricing in Singapore?

Most tax authorities accept and recognise the five primary methods of transfer pricing outlined in the OECD Transfer Pricing Guidelines. These five methods are the comparable uncontrolled price method, the transactional profit split method, the resale price method, the cost plus method and the transactional net margin method. Businesses and authorities use these methods to determine transfer prices. Arm’s length transfer prices are calculated by comparing the profits of similar third-party organisations and comparable transactions.

### How often should taxpayers prepare TP documentation?

IRAS has made it clear that transfer pricing documentation reports should be contemporary, meaning taxpayers should do reports during the current time. Therefore, the transfer pricing report must be done for each financial year starting from 31 Dec 2018 or YA 2019, when the current transfer pricing guidelines came into effect.

### Is it true that IRAS allows taxpayers to produce simplified documentation, and if yes, what kind of conditions need to be fulfilled?

IRAS allows taxpayers to use the transfer pricing documentation they have prepared previously to support the transfer price if that past transfer pricing documentation is a qualifying past transfer pricing documentation.

Past transfer pricing documentation that qualifies as a “qualifying past TP documentation” includes documents that were prepared in the previous one or two years (i.e., TPD prepared in Year 1 can potentially be used for Years 2 and 3, whereas a major update/new TPD is required for Year 4), provided that the following conditions are also met:

a)  The transaction documented in the past transfer pricing documentation is the same type as the transaction in the current year;

b)   The transaction documented in the past transfer pricing documentations are undertaken with the same related parties;

c)   Previous was prepared correspondingly with the requirements under the Rules, properly prepared and dated in English.
