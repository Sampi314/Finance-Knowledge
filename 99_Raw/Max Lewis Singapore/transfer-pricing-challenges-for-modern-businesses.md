1.  **Digital Economy and Intangible Assets Valuation**
    ---------------------------------------------------
    

The [digital economy](https://www.maxlewis.com.sg/transfer-pricing-challenges-for-modern-businesses/)
 has turned transfer pricing on its head. Traditional models just don’t cut it anymore when you’re dealing with intangible assets like software, algorithms, and data.

Think about it—how do you value a piece of code that took thousands of development hours but costs virtually nothing to duplicate and distribute across your global operations? Or customer data that becomes exponentially more valuable when analyzed at scale?

Companies are struggling with these questions daily. A survey by [Deloitte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deloitte)
 found that 78% of multinational corporations identified intangible asset valuation as their top transfer pricing headache.

The real challenge lies in the unique characteristics of digital assets:

*   They can be used simultaneously in multiple locations
*   Their value often grows with use (unlike physical assets that depreciate)
*   The development costs rarely reflect true market value

1.  **Intra-Group Services: Establishing Commercial Rationality**
    -------------------------------------------------------------
    

Ever tried explaining to a tax auditor why your subsidiary paid $2 million for “management services” from the parent company? Not fun.

Intra-group services are under intense scrutiny worldwide. Tax authorities want proof that these services:

1.  Were actually provided (not just on paper)
2.  Delivered genuine value to the recipient
3.  Were priced as if between unrelated parties

The commercial rationality test is becoming standard practice. Would your company reasonably pay an unrelated third party for these same services at the same price?

Many businesses fail this test because they:

*   Can’t produce adequate documentation of service delivery
*   Use arbitrary allocation keys not reflecting actual benefit
*   Apply mark-ups without competitive benchmarking

1.  **Financial Transactions: Loans, Guarantees, and Cash Pooling Arrangements**
    ----------------------------------------------------------------------------
    

Money flowing between related entities creates massive transfer pricing risks. The 2017 OECD guidelines specifically targeted these transactions, and tax authorities followed suit.

Intercompany loans? Better have bulletproof documentation on:

*   Why the loan was commercially necessary
*   Whether the borrower had capacity to service the debt
*   How you determined the interest rate
*   What comparable market transactions support your pricing

Cash pooling arrangements offer efficiency but create complexity. Who gets the benefit of improved liquidity? How should interest be allocated among participants?

Parent company guarantees present similar challenges. If your UK parent guarantees a loan for your Brazilian subsidiary, reducing interest by 2%, what’s the arm’s length price for that guarantee?

1.  **Business Restructuring and Transfer Pricing Implications**
    ------------------------------------------------------------
    

Business restructuring isn’t just an operational headache—it’s a transfer pricing minefield.

When you move functions, assets, or risks between related entities, you’re essentially transferring value. Tax authorities expect compensation for that value, just as would happen between unrelated parties.

Common restructuring scenarios with major transfer pricing implications include:

*   Converting full-fledged manufacturers to contract manufacturers
*   Shifting intellectual property to low-tax jurisdictions
*   Centralizing procurement or R&D functions
*   Establishing regional principals or entrepreneur entities

The key question: who gave up what, and what compensation should they receive? Consider both immediate exit charges and ongoing impact on profit allocation.
