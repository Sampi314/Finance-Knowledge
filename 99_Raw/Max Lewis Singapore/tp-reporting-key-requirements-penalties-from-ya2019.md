[Transfer pricing](https://www.maxlewis.com.sg/our-services/transfer-pricing/ "Transfer Pricing Singapore")
 (TP) documentation plays a critical role in ensuring that transactions between related parties comply with the arm’s length principle, meaning that these transactions are conducted under market conditions. Starting from Year of Assessment (YA) 2019, the Inland Revenue Authority of Singapore (IRAS) has made it clear that companies must prepare transfer pricing documentation on a contemporaneous basis. This means the reports must reflect current data and information available at the time of the transactions.

Importance of Contemporaneous TP Documentation
----------------------------------------------

In order to comply with IRAS’s guidelines, TP reports must be prepared for each financial year, starting from 31 December 2018 (YA 2019). The documentation should demonstrate how the transfer prices for each transaction are determined or supported, using the most recent data available. Companies should ensure consistency in preparing these reports year after year.

When performing audits, IRAS typically starts from YA 2019 or earlier, examining whether the taxpayer has consistently prepared TP reports. Though companies are not required to submit these reports when filing their Income [Tax Returns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tax_return)
, they must be ready to provide them within 30 days of a request from IRAS.

Common Related Party Transactions That Require TP Documentation
---------------------------------------------------------------

For most companies, related-party transactions that require TP documentation include:

**1\. Intercompany loans to overseas entities**: These must be benchmarked to market rates, requiring a separate loan database to perform the necessary benchmarking and analysis.

**2\. Sales and purchases of goods**: Each type of transaction must be benchmarked separately to ensure that transfer pricing guidelines are met.

**3\. Service fees, royalties, license fees, and management fees**: These fees also require separate benchmarking analysis to demonstrate compliance with the arm’s length principle.

Financial Losses as a Potential TP Query Point
----------------------------------------------

For companies operating at a net loss before tax from FY2018 to FY2020, this could trigger an inquiry from the tax authorities. The company must be prepared to explain the reasons behind these losses from a transfer pricing perspective. This scrutiny is heightened by the fact that tax authorities in other countries can request information from Singapore’s IRAS under the Mutual Exchange of Information Agreement, without needing to go through the taxpayer.

Penalties for Non-Compliance with TP Documentation Requirements
---------------------------------------------------------------

Since YA2019, strict penalties have been imposed for non-compliance with transfer pricing documentation requirements. A company is liable to a fine not exceeding SGD 10,000 if it commits any of the following offenses:

*   Failure to prepare transfer pricing documentation in a timely manner or according to the required content;
*   Failure to submit transfer pricing documentation within 30 days of an IRAS request;
*   Failure to retain TP documentation for at least five years; or
*   Providing any false or misleading documentation.

It is essential for companies to remain compliant with these guidelines, as non-compliance can lead to significant penalties, financial costs, and reputational damage.

Conclusion
----------

Preparing accurate and [contemporaneous transfer pricing](https://www.maxlewis.com.sg/the-importance-of-contemporaneous-tp-documentation/ "The Importance Of Contemporaneous TP Documentation")
 documentation is crucial for maintaining compliance with IRAS guidelines. Failing to meet these requirements can result in significant penalties, while thorough and well-prepared documentation ensures that transactions between related parties are transparent and defensible. Businesses should stay vigilant by consistently preparing their TP reports, benchmarking related-party transactions, and being ready to respond to audit queries. In the end, maintaining compliance protects both the company’s financial standing and its reputation.
