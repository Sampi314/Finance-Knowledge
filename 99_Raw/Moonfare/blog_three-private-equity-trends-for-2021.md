Three Private Equity Trends For 2021

Investors hunt for returns and find private equity. The asset class continues to outperform public markets. And individuals become an increasingly important source of…

![Three Private Equity Trends For 2021](https://www.moonfare.com/cdn-cgi/image/width=1256)

January 6, 2021

**Investors hunt for returns and find private equity. The asset class continues to outperform public markets. And individuals become an increasingly important source of capital.**

Moonfare has identified three trends in the private equity industry that we expect to continue and accelerate in the new year.

For a more detailed look at private equity heading into the New Year, please take a moment to review our latest white paper, “[What Now](https://www.moonfare.com/docs/What_Now.pdf)
?”

##### 1\. Investors Hunt for Returns, Find Private Equity

The pandemic and its fallout will once again transform markets in 2021, as the combination of fiscal and monetary policy continues to affect returns in publicly traded assets like stocks and bonds.

In the case of stocks, valuations have [pulled away from the fundamental data presented on companies’ balance sheets and income statements](https://www.ft.com/content/26b4b77b-81c8-4cbb-a7ee-217b1d71e0dc)
 to focus far more on the narrative, decoupling market capitalizations from intrinsic value. Monetary policy encourages this trend. One result: stocks are [more expensive than ever](https://www.wsj.com/articles/the-median-s-p-stock-has-never-been-more-expensive-11598202000)
.

Bonds are no longer a good hedge, with yields bottoming out and a correlation to equity increasing substantially, removing the diversifying benefits of this lower risk asset class.

Smart investors will in 2021 continue looking for ways to outflank these dynamics: They will want to invest in fundamentals and intrinsic value while diversifying their portfolios and avoiding volatility. These investors will increasingly take advantage of new access to invest in private markets.

By the numbers, the retail demand for [alternative investments](https://www.moonfare.com/glossary/alternative-investments)
 is expected to be up by more than 15% in 2021 from 2019, [according to Blackstone](https://www.sec.gov/files/Panel2-John-Finley-Blackstone.pdf)
. Within alternatives, the global AUM for private equity is expected to overtake hedge fund AUM by 2023, to become the preferred alternative asset.

At Moonfare, we’ve seen the growing demand for private equity first hand. The average portfolio is around €500,000, with more than 50% of our clients investing in more than one fund and more than 25% holding three or more funds. Around half of the new AUM on Moonfare’s platform comes from repeat investors increasing their exposure, a trend we expect to continue this year.

##### 2\. Private Equity Outperforms Public Markets Over Time

Private equity has delivered [higher returns and lower volatility](https://www.sec.gov/files/Panel2-John-Finley-Blackstone.pdf)
 than public markets over the past two decades. Funds launched since the global financial crisis have been [strong and consistent](https://www.mckinsey.com/~/media/mckinsey/industries/private%20equity%20and%20principal%20investors/our%20insights/mckinseys%20private%20markets%20annual%20review/mckinsey-global-private-markets-review-2020-v4.ashx)
 in totality. Faced with a pandemic-driven economic dislocation, we are in prime time for new funds that could potentially set a new high watermark for fund performance.

Private equity investment is about finding fundamental value in good companies. But as we saw this year, even good companies can become distressed, with profits battered by Covid-19 and entire industries like hospitality, travel and some retail forced to largely shut down.

Investment is never just about buying low: It is also buying the right business at the best price and the right time. Some of the strongest performing private equity funds have launched [during economic downturns](https://www.mckinsey.com/~/media/mckinsey/industries/private%20equity%20and%20principal%20investors/our%20insights/mckinseys%20private%20markets%20annual%20review/mckinsey-global-private-markets-review-2020-v4.ashx)
, where advantageous pricing dynamics can emerge (e.g. fewer buyers in the market, distressed sellers). Take 2009, in the aftermath of the global financial crisis, when the average purchase price multiple for leveraged buyout transactions in the US was [lower than in any other year between 2006-2019](https://www.cambridgeassociates.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/09/Managing-Portfolios-Through-Equity-Market-Downturns.pdf)
.

More than ever, the management expertise of private equity will add value. Research by [McKinsey & Company](https://www.mckinsey.com/~/media/McKinsey/Industries/Private%20Equity%20and%20Principal%20Investors/Our%20Insights/Lessons%20for%20private%20equity%20from%20the%20last%20downturn/Lessons-for-private-equity-from-the-last-downturn-vF.pdf?shouldIndex=false)
 found that fund managers who operated a dedicated value-creation team outperformed in crisis years, with 5 percentage points more in terms of internal rates of return (23%) than fund managers without (18%).

##### 3\. Fund Managers Increasingly Raise Money from Individuals

The investors who once relied on private equity firms to manage their money are increasingly managing it themselves. We expect to see more pension funds [raising third-party capital](https://www.privateequityinternational.com/danish-pensions-fof-arm-seeks-up-to-e800m-for-debut-fund-as-it-turns-gp/?utm_source=sailthru&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=breaking:%20atp%20turns%20gp&utm_term=pei%20alert%20smart%20list)
 themselves and, as a result, the [influx of retail capital](https://www.privateequityinternational.com/is-private-equity-ready-for-a-retail-revolution/)
 into private equity to continue accelerating.

The shift towards a more hands-on investing role is nothing new for big institutional investors who traditionally allocated to private equity funds. Private equity allocators have been more involved in [co-investments](https://www.moonfare.com/blog/private-equity-co-investment)
 and building their own portfolios [for the past decade](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2018/hands-on-role-institutional-investors-private-equity)
, while the number of direct-investment deals grew [16% on average per year](https://pitchbook.com/news/articles/direct-and-co-investment-by-lps-on-the-rise)
 from 2009 to 2017.

This kind of active strategy profits from tailwinds in the industry. Fund managers are increasingly avoiding club deals — when multiple funds tag team an investment — and instead seeking exclusive control over their targets, while co-investment funds like [AlpInvest](https://www.alpinvest.com/strategies/co-investments)
 capitalize on the gap by offering capital and expertise without insisting on control.

Now, with the growing combination of [regulatory support](https://www.ssga.com/library-content/pdfs/official-institutions-/boom-in-private-markets-is-no-private-matter.pdf)
, [platform solutions](https://www.moonfare.com/)
 — like Moonfare — and return-seeking investors willing to face some illiquidity, the path for individuals to allocate to private equity funds and fill the funding gap left by institutional investors running their own portfolios is becoming clearer every day.
