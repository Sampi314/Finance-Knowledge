Why infrastructure transactions close or collapse: The role of strong advisory
==============================================================================

*   November 27, 2025

**By Gaurav Singh, Head of Infrastructure Advisory**

##### Table of Contents

In today’s capital markets – especially in infrastructure projects and renewable energy – deals don’t necessarily fail because the opportunity is no good. Often, it’s a result of poor advice.

Behind every successful transaction lies not just a viable project or a willing buyer, but a team of advisors with the technical depth, foresight, and steady negotiation skills to see it through to completion. In contrast, weak advisory can turn even the most promising opportunity into a costly collapse – something both public and private sector stakeholders increasingly understand.

Technical knowledge: The foundation of credibility
--------------------------------------------------

Modern infrastructure and energy transactions are complex ecosystems of engineering, regulation, and finance. Advisors who don’t understand the underlying assets often underestimate risk or overlook structural weaknesses. For instance, the collapse of several early-stage UK battery storage deals in 2023–24 came down not to a lack of investor appetite, but to flawed assumptions about grid connection timings and technical constraints – mistakes that could have been avoided with better [informed advisory services](https://www.gridlines.com/financial-advisory/)
.

The best advisors don’t just understand term sheets; their understanding of transformers, inverters, and interconnectors enable better context and foresight.

Experience: Knowing what’s around the corner
--------------------------------------------

Experience breeds intuition – the ability to see where things might derail long before they do. Seasoned advisors recognise red flags early: a counterparty overpromising on EPC delivery, a lender insisting on unworkable covenants, or a developer underestimating permitting risk.

A classic case can be seen in offshore wind transactions around 2010, when several projects in Northern Europe faced years of delay after investors relied on inexperienced counsel who failed to account for seabed rights and grid access complexities. The deals didn’t fail because the projects were bad, but rather, because the advice was blind to experience.

Foresight: Structuring for the future, not just the present
-----------------------------------------------------------

A strong advisor doesn’t just close today’s deal, they design it to stand the test of tomorrow’s regulation, inflation, and market volatility. Foresight is what distinguishes the best from the merely competent. It’s about having the attention to detail, building reliable financial models, stress-testing scenarios, running sensitivities, and anticipating what investors or lenders will question before they even ask.

In the renewables sector, where government policy and market rules evolve quickly, foresight can mean the difference between an asset that performs and one that underperforms. Deals structured without sufficient flexibility or contingency often become stranded when the market shifts.

Negotiation: Keeping calm when things go off script
---------------------------------------------------

Deals rarely proceed in a straight line. Negotiations stall, tempers flare, and external events from interest rate hikes to policy delays, can derail timelines. The art of good negotiation lies in maintaining composure, empathy, and balance. A skilled advisor knows how to bring parties back to the table, reframe risk, and preserve momentum even when things don’t go to plan.

Here at Gridlines, we have seen deals almost collapse at the eleventh hour due to a misinterpretation of specific clauses in a loan agreement. But our experience, particularly in [cross-border infrastructure transactions,](https://www.gridlines.com/blog/m-a-financial-modelling/)
 meant we were able to remain calm and measured to revive and ultimately close the deal.

Financial analysis and modelling: The backbone of decision-making
-----------------------------------------------------------------

Even the most charismatic advisor cannot compensate for weak numbers. [Financial modelling](https://www.gridlines.com/project-finance-modelling/)
 is the language of trust in any infrastructure investment — and it must be accurate, transparent, and robust. Specialist firms like Gridlines exemplify the importance of this discipline. A strong model not only reflects reality; it helps shape it by providing clarity to investors and lenders about what’s bankable, what’s risky, and where value can be created.

When models are flawed, everything downstream unravels. Deals can fall apart when returns are overstated, debt service coverage is miscalculated, or sensitivity tests are poorly constructed. One prominent renewable energy transaction in Southern Europe reportedly failed because a developer’s model omitted degradation impacts on solar yield — an error that wiped out projected returns once corrected.

In summary
----------

A deal succeeds due to a multitude of reasons. As well as the economics needing to stack up, the depth, breadth and experience of a good advisor will help maximise the probability of success. The right advisors combine technical knowledge, experience, foresight, negotiation skill, and analytical precision to bridge the gap between opportunity and execution.

In an environment where margins are tighter, [infrastructure valuations](https://www.gridlines.com/infrastructure-valuations/)
 are under pressure, and investor scrutiny is sharper than ever, the difference between closing and collapsing isn’t luck. It’s strong advisory.

For anyone involved in infrastructure or renewables transactions, it’s worth asking not just what you’re buying, but who is advising you. Because in the end, a good advisor doesn’t just close deals; they prevent them from falling apart!
