What project sponsors get wrong about financial advisory
========================================================

*   February 13, 2026

**By Gaurav Singh, Head of Infrastructure Advisory**

In project development, sponsors obsess over technology choices, EPC contracts, permitting, and commercial strategy. Yet many still underestimate – or fundamentally misunderstand – the strategic value of specialist debt and equity financial advisory.

The consequences can be expensive: delayed financial close, reduced valuations, mispriced risk, diluted ownership, or even project cancellation.

At **Gridlines**, we repeatedly see the same patterns. Here’s what project sponsors frequently get wrong.

![A low angle shot of two glass skyscrapers with a bright blue sky in the background.](https://www.gridlines.com/wp-content/uploads/pexels-brett-sayles-2599538-2-2048x1363.jpg "What project sponsors get wrong about financial advisory 1")

### 1\. Treating financial advisory as a “check-the-box” requirement

Many sponsors assume that financial advisory is simply about “raising the money.” In reality, high-quality advisory is about structuring the transaction to optimise cost of capital, negotiating lender requirements, and insulating the project from avoidable risk.

**What goes wrong when this is overlooked?**

Sponsors often hire advisors based solely on brand recognition or previous relationships – even if those advisors lack deep sector or jurisdictional experience.

In several major offshore wind and thermal power deals across Europe and Asia, sponsors initially engaged corporate finance teams with limited project-finance structuring experience. The result:

*   Debt packages misaligned with construction risk profiles
*   Prolonged lender due diligence
*   Value dilution caused by renegotiation late in the process

In several cases, sponsors were forced to replace advisors mid-process which is both expensive and time-consuming!

### 2\. Mandating advisors who don’t understand the project’s capital structure

Complex projects require nuanced structuring. Yet some sponsors still appoint advisors whose expertise lies in M&A or corporate equity placement, not project finance.

Consequences include:

*   Overly conservative or expensive capital stacks
*   Lender-driven restructurings
*   Unnecessary equity dilution

### 3\. Assuming “big bank = best advisor”

Global investment banks are outstanding institutions, but corporate-finance strength does not always translate into greenfield project finance expertise.

Sponsors often face:

*   Incomplete documentation packages
*   Misaligned risk allocation
*   Ineffective ECA coordination

### 4\. Overconfidence in in-house capabilities

Internal teams are essential – but rarely have the deal volume, lender relationships, or bandwidth to run a full financing process.

This results in:

*   Weak competitive tension
*   Suboptimal term sheets
*   Inflexible covenant packages
*   Avoidable equity dilution

### 5\. Underestimating the value of an independent financial model

Some sponsors rely on their internal teams for financial modelling, but lenders and investors alike. But without the rigorous model interrogation and independent reviews that come with a third party financial advisor, a relatively small upfront cost could lead to very costly issues.

Weak models lead to:

*   Prolonged due diligence
*   Late-stage model rebuilding
*   Collapsed negotiations

### What the right advisor brings

*   Market-tested structuring and pricing
*   Competitive tension among lenders
*   Risk articulation and mitigation
*   Independence
*   Efficient financial close
