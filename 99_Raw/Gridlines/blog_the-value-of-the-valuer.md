The value of the valuer
=======================

*   December 18, 2025

**By: Isha Gupta, Director of Valuation**

In infrastructure asset management, getting valuations right requires multiple layers of expertise working in concert. Yet we often encounter a misconception: “We have excellent financial modellers who built our asset models, and our auditors review them thoroughly. Why do we need a valuer?”  

The answer lies in understanding that these are complementary, not interchangeable, skill sets.

![3 elements blog](https://www.gridlines.com/wp-content/uploads/3-elements-blog.png "The value of the valuer 1")

The role of a valuer:
---------------------

While both model builders and model auditors are critical for model accuracy and reliability, a valuer’s role goes beyond crunching numbers or verifying model formulas. A valuer adds professional judgment that connects your technically sound model to market reality.

_Valuers specialise in:_

1.  Determining an appropriate discount rate that truly reflects the project’s risk profile, capital structure, and market conditions.
2.  Validating assumptions against the market reality by tracking market signals and investor expectations. This includes stress testing the assumptions, such as tariff projections, operational performance, policy changes, and refinancing options against comparable transactions and market data.
3.  Conducting sanity checks that connect model output to the market sentiment.

**Here’s a real example:**

A pension fund valued its renewable energy portfolio internally at €620M.

Their models were technically flawless.  
Their audits were thorough and complete.

Then an infrastructure valuer reviewed it and gave it a market-calibrated valuation of €755M.  
That’s a **€135M difference.**

So why the difference?

1.  The discount rate issue: The model used a 7.0% discount rate from three years ago. In this time, the market had shifted to 5.5-6.5% due to a fundamental shift in the investor base—large institutional investors and sovereign wealth funds had entered the market aggressively, compressing yields across the sector.  
    Additionally, the cost of debt had fallen, and the perception of regulatory risk in renewables had decreased materially.  
    
2.  The merchant price risk premium: The cash flow model included a “merchant risk” adjustment using post-PPA revenue forecast, which is 8% lower. This was reasonable when modelled.  
    However, the valuer identified that recent transactions of comparable assets showed buyers were now using post-PPA forecast which is only 0-3% lower.  
    Why? The market had watched these assets perform through years of volatile energy prices and had gained confidence in the revenue stability.
3.  The price curve differential: The energy price curve used in the model was lower than that from the leading consultants, and the investors would price it more conservatively.  
      
    

These weren’t modelling errors, and they weren’t an audit failure either. It was a commercial judgment gap.

Without someone continuously tracking market transactions, understanding investor appetite, and calibrating assumptions against live pricing data, the technically perfect model had delivered a materially understated valuation.

Unsurprisingly, the fund’s investment committee was initially sceptical—how on earth could their sophisticated models be so far off?

The answer is simply because models calculate what you tell them to calculate. Only valuers tell you whether what you’re calculating still reflects reality. And that’s the value of the valuer. 

**How they work together**

The most robust valuations emerge when all three disciplines work in together:

*   **Model builders** create the technical engine that can reliably process complex cash flows and sensitivities
*   **Model auditors** provide assurance that the engine works as intended and maintains integrity
*   **Valuers** check the key inputs against market evidence and validate that outputs align with how the market looks at these assets.

Remove any one of these elements, and you introduce material risk.

**The bottom line**
-------------------

[Infrastructure valuations](https://www.gridlines.com/infrastructure-valuations/)
 that stand up to scrutiny—from auditors, regulators, investors, and ultimately the market itself—require expertise across multiple dimensions.

Model builders, model auditors, and valuers each bring essential capabilities that the others don’t replicate.  
  
The big question isn’t whether you need a valuer when you already have modellers and auditors. It’s whether you can afford a valuation process that lacks any of these critical perspectives.

When reputations, and billions of dollars in asset values, hang on these judgments, having the right expertise in the right roles is fundamental risk management.
