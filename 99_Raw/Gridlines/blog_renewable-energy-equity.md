Why it’s so hard to raise renewable energy equity right now
===========================================================

*   October 23, 2025

By Gaurav Singh  
_Head of Infrastructure Advisory @ Gridlines_

If you’ve tried to raise equity for an infrastructure or renewable energy project recently, you’ll know it’s one of the toughest markets in recent memory. Deals are still getting done, but they’re taking longer, the bar for investment is higher, and investor nerves are running deep.

Portfolios trading below NAV: The confidence gap
------------------------------------------------

One of the biggest reasons for the slowdown in investment in renewables is the disconnect between market valuations and asset fundamentals. Many listed infrastructure and renewable funds are still trading well below their Net Asset Value (NAV) – in some cases 20–30% below.

  
That’s created a confidence problem. Why would institutional investors commit new equity at full value when they can buy into existing portfolios at a discount? Until that gap narrows, fresh fundraising will continue to lag. Investors are not short of capital, they’re short of conviction.

Political crosswinds: The UK and US in focus
--------------------------------------------

Policy uncertainty is also feeding that hesitation.  
In the UK, the new Labour government has been vocal about accelerating the energy transition, with plans to streamline planning, grid connections, and local energy partnerships.

But investors are still unsure about what this means in reality. Talk of public ownership in parts of the energy system, potential profit caps, and ongoing regulatory reform have introduced an element of “wait and see” caution – particularly for asset managers aligning their investment strategy with long-term government policy.

Across the Atlantic, the US’s Inflation Reduction Act has turbocharged renewable power, but it’s also created friction. Tax credit transfer rules are complex, costs are rising, and the looming presidential election has investors wondering whether today’s incentives will survive tomorrow’s politics.

The Gate 2 bottleneck: Battery M&A on pause
-------------------------------------------

In the UK, the Gate 2 grid connection process is becoming a critical bottleneck, particularly for the battery storage sector. Project developers and investors are holding their breath for the Gate 2 results due in December 2025, which will determine which projects secure connection slots. That uncertainty has stalled M&A activity across the sector.

Deals that were ready to move to exclusivity have been pushed back as buyers refuse to commit without clarity on grid connection timelines and costs. At the same time, smaller developers are at a critical juncture in deciding whether they sell at a discount to the recent market, or take a gamble and sell at a premium if their project makes the cut.

Investors expect that once Gate 2 results are announced, there will be a flurry of dealmaking. Strong, well-positioned projects with confirmed connection dates could see premiums, while others may struggle to attract capital or will need to be restructured. December could mark a major reset point for the [UK battery storage market](https://www.renewableuk.com/energypulse/blog/stacking-up-the-storage-where-the-uk-battery-market-stands-in-2025/)
 – and a test of patience for those pursuing investments in renewable energy.

Higher yields, slower closes
----------------------------

Even beyond policy, macroeconomics is doing its part to keep things sticky. With gilt and treasury yields hovering around 4-5%, the traditional risk-adjusted return of renewable energy projects – typically in the 6-8% range – doesn’t look as compelling as it once did.

As a result, investors are digging deeper into [risk modelling](https://www.gridlines.com/project-finance-modelling/)
, demanding stronger downside protection, and taking months rather than weeks to reach decisions. Deals that might have taken eight weeks to close in 2021 are now taking six months or more. This is the reality, and we are seeing this first-hand at Gridlines.

Cautious optimism for 2025/26
-----------------------------

Not all is doom and gloom. The long-term fundamentals for renewables remain unquestionable. Energy security, decarbonisation, electrification, and infrastructure modernisation are here to stay. What’s missing right now is confidence and clarity.

Once listed fund valuations stabilise, the UK government’s policy intentions become clearer, and the Gate 2 dust settles, the market could see a significant rebound in debt and equity appetite.

For now, the message is clear: deals are still happening, but the new reality is slower, more cautious, and far more selective. In this environment, transparency, patience, and discipline are the currencies that matter most.
