Auditing financial models: Common pitfalls explained
====================================================

*   June 3, 2025

**By Paul Myers, Director**.

You’ve heard it before – the devil is in the detail.

And so it is with auditing financial models. You might think that checking your model is a pretty straightforward task – your modeller sends us their model, maybe some additional items to do with the transaction or project finance structure, and off we go. If the model matches the transaction and any other bits of information, then all’s good, no?

Well, there’s a bit more to it…

Why audit in the first place?
-----------------------------

Typical infrastructure and energy transactions involve lenders and investors planning to put hundreds of millions of dollars into a project. That’s why it’s so common to get a model checked by an independent expert team before large amounts of money start moving.

Why can’t the customer build and check their own model?

Well a typical model has thousands of formulas in, all written by humans, and humans make mistakes. Every person with a car thinks they’re a great driver, but there’s a reason why wearing a seatbelt is the law in most places.

The financial model for a typical infrastructure transaction often has thousands of calculations, so it is almost guaranteed that there will be mistakes. The job of the model auditor is to find those.

And ok, the model auditor isn’t perfect either, so some gremlins might go unnoticed, but having that second pair of eyes (or team of eyes) gives an order of magnitude more confidence in the forecasts upon which the deal will be agreed. Here are a few classic pitfalls to beware of to make sure your money is well-spent.

Common Pitfalls when Auditing Financial Models
----------------------------------------------

### Pitfall #1: not having a clear scope

You want to know your model works. But what do you mean by “works”?

Do all the formulas do what they should? Does your modeller understand what the project is about?

Does the model accurately match your real-life project? If the model is to be checked against some key transaction contracts, how complete or reliable are they?

And once you have clarified what you’re wanting to get checked, how detailed do you want that check to be? Some situations may only need a light-touch review of the numbers – maybe the model has been used many times before, maybe the key numbers in the deal are driven by other factors and the audit is less critical – but some will require scrutiny and a detailed review to ensure that the debt is safe and the investment won’t suffer if the deal price has come from a broken model.

If the audit scope is not clear, there’s a risk that either the model auditor wastes time checking things nobody needs, or worse that the auditor didn’t check important pieces of the analysis.

### Pitfall #2: using the wrong tools for the job

We’re a service business, so the “tools” here are the processes we develop. But like any business in any industry, or even the weekend DIY enthusiast, having the right tools makes a huge difference. There are two main tools, or approaches to model review: shadow modelling or cell-by-cell review.

Shadow modelling uses a parallel model to aim for the same answers as the model under scrutiny, but without studying the innards of the model under test, on the basis that if you start in the same place (same model inputs) and get to the same place (same model results) via a different route, it is very likely that where you have got to is the right place.

The advantages are that the review is inherently focussed on whether the model achieves what it is trying to do, and isn’t distracted or delayed by checking parts of a model that don’t do anything in the current case.

The disadvantage of shadow modelling is that if the model under review has parts that come to life as the deal develops, there can suddenly be a whole load more model modelling to check.

The cell-by-cell review process examines each different formula in the model to check it is doing what one would expect and is connected to the right precedent and dependent cells. Advantages of this approach include the review of parts of the model that might be inactive right now but might spring to life later in the transaction development (which the shadow modelling review would implicitly ignore) and that some models or sections of models are easier and quicker to check by eye than to have to create formulas (as a shadow model would require).

The disadvantage of cell-by-cell is that it relies on the reviewer being laser-focussed on scrutinising the formula and concentrating 100% throughout, but humans are fallible (just like drivers).

Both have their advantages and trade-offs, so we have brought together people with backgrounds in each to maximise the advantages and minimise the trade-offs. Indeed for some audits we use a mixture of both approaches through the model.

### Pitfall #3: leaving it until the last minute

When the transaction is finalised and the deal about to be done, there are dozens of workstreams across the project teams all coming to a head.

So getting more of the audit done earlier means less to do in the last moments.

Pretty obvious, eh? But traditional model audit scopes put limits on how much reviewing or how many versions of the model the fee includes, so the modeller holds on to the model to save costs.

Removing those limits means the auditor can do their due diligence and get into the work earlier, raise queries sooner, and discuss those with you when they find them, rather than batching them up for a lengthy report a few weeks later.

This saves time overall compared to traditional model audit, while also taking out some of the stress of the last few days before your deadline.

### Pitfall #4: “just doing the same as last time”

At the start of every review, the auditor should have a call with you to make sure that scope is clear. It can be easy to slide into assuming this job is the same as the last one, but key details make big differences.

And whilst we auditors are analytical people are focussed on logic and structure, having some background on the project, knowing why it is happening now and here, makes our output more insightful.

### Pitfall #5: fighting it out or figuring it out - confrontation v cooperation

Sometimes it looks like the model auditor is paid to rip apart someone else’s work. Some auditors approach it on that basis, and some modellers take the comments personally.

Good auditors will be very clear that they’re here to help, in everyone’s interests. We’re not here to point-score.

If the model auditor feels like they need to justify their existence (and their fees) by finding hundreds of trivial points to raise, nobody wins. In complex forecasts such as these there will always be a few things to raise (remember, nobody is the perfect driver!) so keeping the conversation relevant to the things that matter is key.

The things that matter is usually obvious from the situation, but only if the auditor has taken time to understand the situation.

A one-off model that has been developed just for this transaction and will be thrown away after the deal is done can tolerate some items that might not be best-practice modelling.

Whereas a model which your team plans to use again and again may benefit from hearing about things that would make the model more robust to future changes, future transactions, even future modellers.

We mentioned scope earlier; if that is not clear, the interaction can become confrontational, then nobody enjoys the experience.
