Intrinsic vs. Relative Value: Two Sides of the Coin
---------------------------------------------------

Valuation methods generally fall into two categories: intrinsic and relative.

*   **Intrinsic Value (Absolute Valuation):** This approach, exemplified by the DCF model, seeks to calculate a company's value based on its own fundamental financial characteristics, primarily its ability to generate cash flow. It's an attempt to find a standalone, objective value.
*   **Relative Value (Comparable Analysis):** This approach, which includes P/E and EV/EBITDA multiples, values a company by comparing it to similar companies in the market. It's less about finding an absolute number and more about seeing how a stock is priced relative to its peers.

1\. Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Valuation
----------------------------------------

The DCF model is arguably the most comprehensive method for determining intrinsic value. It’s built on the principle that a company's value is equal to the sum of all its future cash flows, discounted back to their present value.

### How It Works

A DCF analysis involves three main steps:

*   **Forecast Free Cash Flow (FCF):** Estimate the company's unlevered [free cash flow](https://www.askatlantis.com/blog/what-is-free-cash-flow)
     over a forecast period, typically 5-10 years.
*   **Calculate Terminal Value:** Estimate the value of the company beyond the forecast period.
*   **Discount to Present Value:** Use the company's [Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC)](https://www.askatlantis.com/blog/what-is-wacc)
     to discount all future cash flows (both the forecast period and terminal value) back to today to arrive at the enterprise value.

### Pros:

*   **Comprehensive:** It’s a detailed, bottom-up approach that forces you to think critically about all the drivers of a business.
*   **Focus on Fundamentals:** It relies on a company's ability to generate cash, the ultimate source of value.
*   **Not Swayed by Market Sentiment:** It provides a value estimate independent of volatile market moods.

### Cons:

*   **Highly Sensitive to Assumptions:** Small changes in growth rates or the WACC can dramatically alter the final valuation.
*   **Difficult for Unpredictable Companies:** It’s less reliable for startups, cyclical companies, or businesses with no clear history of cash flow.

**Best for:** Valuing stable, mature companies with predictable cash flows, like Johnson & Johnson (JNJ) or Coca-Cola (KO). For a complete walkthrough, see our [DCF valuation guide](https://www.askatlantis.com/blog/what-is-dcf-valuation)
.

2\. Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio
---------------------------------

The P/E ratio is the most famous relative valuation multiple. It tells you how much investors are willing to pay for each dollar of a company's earnings.

### How It Works

The calculation is simple: \`P/E Ratio = Market Price per Share / Earnings per Share (EPS)\`.

A high P/E suggests that investors expect higher earnings growth in the future, while a low P/E might indicate a company is undervalued or has lower growth prospects.

### Pros:

*   **Simple and Widely Available:** It’s easy to calculate and readily available on any financial data platform.
*   **Good for Quick Comparisons:** It provides a fast way to compare the valuation of similar, profitable companies within the same industry.

### Cons:

*   **Can Be Misleading:** It can be distorted by different accounting policies, one-time charges, and negative earnings (in which case it's not meaningful).
*   **Ignores Debt:** P/E is an equity-level multiple, meaning it doesn't account for a company's capital structure. A company with a lot of debt might look cheaper on a P/E basis than it really is.

**Best for:** A quick screening tool for comparing profitable, established companies in the same sector, like comparing Apple (AAPL) with a P/E of around 33 to Microsoft (MSFT). 3\. Enterprise Value-to-EBITDA (EV/EBITDA)
------------------------------------------

The EV/EBITDA multiple is another relative valuation tool that is often considered more robust than the P/E ratio.

### How It Works

The formula is: \`EV/EBITDA = Enterprise Value / EBITDA\`.

*   **Enterprise Value (EV):** Represents the total value of a company, including both its equity and debt, minus cash. It's the theoretical takeover price.
*   **EBITDA:** Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. It's a proxy for operating cash flow.

By using EV, this multiple accounts for differences in capital structure (debt levels). By using EBITDA, it removes the effects of non-cash expenses and varying tax rates, making it excellent for comparing companies with different financial leverage or from different countries.

### Pros:

*   **Capital Structure Neutral:** It’s ideal for comparing companies with different levels of debt.
*   **Less Affected by Accounting Choices:** EBITDA is a cleaner measure of operating performance than net earnings.

### Cons:

*   **Ignores Capital Expenditures (CapEx):** EBITDA can overstate cash flow for capital-intensive industries (like manufacturing or airlines) because it ignores the cash needed to maintain and upgrade assets.

**Best for:** Comparing companies across different industries or with varying capital structures. For example, comparing Amazon (AMZN) with an EV/EBITDA of ~14x to a more traditional retailer like Walmart (WMT).

Comparison Summary: DCF vs. P/E vs. EV/EBITDA
---------------------------------------------

| Feature | DCF | P/E Ratio | EV/EBITDA |

| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |

| **Valuation Type** | Intrinsic (Absolute) | Relative | Relative |

| **Core Component** | Free Cash Flow | Earnings per Share | EBITDA |

| **Accounts for Debt?** | Yes | No | Yes |

| **Best Use Case** | Mature, stable companies | Quick comparison of profitable peers | Companies with different debt levels |

| **Biggest Weakness** | Sensitive to assumptions | Distorted by accounting/debt | Ignores capital expenditures |

The Power of Triangulation
--------------------------

No single stock valuation method tells the whole story. The most effective approach is to use them together in a process called "triangulation." By calculating a company's value using a DCF model and then comparing it to peers with P/E and EV/EBITDA multiples, you can build a much more robust and defensible investment thesis.

Frequently Asked Questions
--------------------------

**Q: Which valuation method is best for beginners?**

A: The P/E ratio is the simplest to understand and calculate, making it a great starting point for beginners. However, it's crucial to learn its limitations and quickly move on to using EV/EBITDA and eventually DCF for a more complete picture.

**Q: Can a stock have a negative P/E ratio?**

A: No. If a company has negative earnings (a net loss), the P/E ratio is not meaningful and is typically displayed as "N/A." In these cases, other metrics like EV/Sales or a DCF model are necessary.

**Q: Why is a lower EV/EBITDA multiple considered better?**

A: A lower EV/EBITDA multiple suggests that a company might be undervalued relative to its operating cash flow. It means you are paying less for each dollar of a company's earnings power, which can be an attractive investment opportunity.
