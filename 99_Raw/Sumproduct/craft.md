Consistency
-----------

Structured modeling offers a win-win for both model developers and users. By enforcing consistent formatting and formula application, it significantly reduces errors, leading to more reliable and trustworthy models. Users can then grasp the model’s logic faster due to its clear and organized structure.  
  
For developers, pre-defined styles and the ability to apply formulas uniformly across data sets streamline the building process, saving time and effort. This structured approach ensures both the accuracy and speed needed to build robust and dependable models.

Robustness
----------

For bulletproof models, think “simple, strong, auditable.” This philosophy prioritises clear structure, consistent formulas, and built-in safeguards.  
  
By eliminating hidden complexities like macros, the model becomes readily auditable and mathematically accurate. Inherent error checks and sensitivity analyses further enhance robustness. This approach ensures models are not just error-free, but also transparent and reliable, fostering trust in their results.

Flexibility
-----------

Traditional models offer a single answer, leaving you wondering “what if?” Structured models break this mold with a built-in “what if?” engine.

**Flexibility for Exploration:** These models allow you to adjust key assumptions within defined boundaries. Imagine adjusting variables like interest rates or sales forecasts – all within a controlled environment.

**Unlocking Deeper Insights:** This flexibility isn’t passive. You can actively explore different scenarios and see how the model responds. This goes beyond calculations, helping you identify potential risks and opportunities for informed decision-making.

Transparency
------------

Many modellers often forget that key decision makers base their choices on printed material.

Models need to be clear, concise and cohesive.  Assumptions, calculations and outputs need to be recognisable instantly and user-friendly bearing in mind:

*   the logical flow and dynamic linking of the model
*   the number of categories required for particular calculations
*   the nature of the assumptions together with their assumption entry methods at the design stage
