Higher education institutions face mounting financial challenges. Declining enrollments have led to a staggering loss of tuition revenue, with U.S. colleges seeing a significant drop in student enrollment between 2022-2024. Specifically, first-year domestic enrollments of 18-year-olds in four-year colleges [declined by over 6% from fall 2023 to fall 2024](https://monitor.icef.com/2024/12/first-year-college-students-in-the-us-down-by-more-than-6-ahead-of-projected-enrolment-cliff/)
. Major contributing factors include systemic financial aid issues, rising education costs, and shifting educational preferences.

Simultaneously, funding has become increasingly volatile, with unpredictable fluctuations in grants, government aid, and endowments.

Adding to the strain, inflation has driven up costs across the board—from faculty salaries to campus operations and technology investments. In this uncertain environment, the traditional budgeting process in higher education often falls short. Rigid, linear approaches fail to keep pace with fluctuating enrollments, inconsistent funding, and rising operational expenses.

According to StrataDecision.com, [66% of higher education finance professionals](https://www.syntellis.com/blog/future-higher-ed-depends-moving-past-financial-guesswork)
 believe their current business models are unsustainable over the next five to 10 years. As competition for students intensifies and tuition revenue declines, institutions must embrace financial agility—dynamic forecasting, scenario planning, and rolling budgets—to stay ahead. Without the ability to pivot in real-time, they risk falling behind. 

This guide equips leaders with best practices for higher education budgeting to navigate uncertainty while ensuring institutional sustainability and growth.

Challenges of Budgeting and Forecasting in Higher Education
-----------------------------------------------------------

[Budget forecasting](https://www.golimelight.com/blog/how-to-do-budget-forecasting)
 in universities comes with core challenges that increase risk and restrict growth. Let's discuss them.

### Static annual budgets limit flexibility

Many higher education institutions often rely on rigid annual budgets, making it difficult to adapt to changing financial conditions. With inflation outpacing revenue growth, [many universities face reduced purchasing power](https://www.claconnect.com/en/resources/articles/2023/trends-in-higher-education-and-2024-outlook)
, impacting operations and innovation. 

Inside Higher Ed reported that DePaul University [projected a $56 million deficit](https://www.insidehighered.com/news/business/financial-health/2023/05/05/what-do-depauls-budget-woes-mean-catholic-higher-ed)
 for 2023-24. The university faced a significant shortfall due to a delayed budget evaluation. The Strategic Resource Allocation Committee (SRAC) was unable to assess the budget gap and develop timely strategies.

### Tuition-dependent institutions face revenue risks

Declining enrollments have strained tuition-dependent colleges. Inside Higher Ed reported that Penn State announced [$94 million in budget cuts for 2025-26](https://www.insidehighered.com/opinion/views/2024/02/05/most-colleges-finances-are-biggest-challenge-opinion)
. Heavy reliance on tuition makes institutions vulnerable to demographic shifts and economic downturns.

### Uncertainty in government and donor funding

Government funding remains unpredictable, with [many institutions losing COVID-19 relief funds](https://www.insidehighered.com/opinion/views/2024/02/05/most-colleges-finances-are-biggest-challenge-opinion)
 that had temporarily bridged financial gaps. Donor contributions also fluctuate due to economic and political factors, complicating long-term financial planning.

### Complexity in managing multiple cost centers

Universities manage diverse cost centers like research, housing, and healthcare. In 2022, Henderson State University projected a [budget shortfall of $12.5 million](https://www.hsu.edu/site/assets/files/3277/henderson_financial_report.pdf)
. Its academic portfolio was misaligned with financial realities, causing all academic units to operate at a loss. The costs associated with faculty and programs were unsustainable given the declining student enrollment. This necessitated drastic measures, such as program cuts and restructuring, to align spending with actual revenues and workforce needs.

These complexities pose significant challenges for resource allocation and [financial forecasting](https://www.golimelight.com/blog/best-financial-forecasting-software)
 accuracy.

Modern Budgeting & Forecasting Approaches for Higher Education
--------------------------------------------------------------

Institutions can better manage risks and achieve sustainable growth while fulfilling their mission. Here are four effective approaches:

### 1\. Zero-based budgeting

Zero-based budgeting in higher education requires institutions to justify all expenses annually, ensuring alignment with priorities like student success and research. For example, the [University of Kentucky implemented zero-based budgeting](https://uknowledge.uky.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1440&context=mpampp_etds)
 to allocate resources toward high-impact programs, improving operational efficiency.

### 2\. Rolling forecasts

Rolling forecasts enable institutions to adapt to changing conditions by updating budgets quarterly or monthly. [Rutgers University shifted to rolling three-year averages](https://www.insidehighered.com/sites/default/files/2024-03/IHE_Best%20Practices%20in%20Multi-Year%20Budgeting.pdf)
 for cost metrics, enhancing financial stability and responsiveness.

### 3\. Predictive analytics

Predictive models identify patterns in faculty workload, course demand, and facility utilization, helping institutions allocate resources efficiently while reducing waste. [The University of Arizona adopted predictive analytics](https://budget.arizona.edu/sites/default/files/2024-09/university_of_arizona_fy25_unrestricted_operating_budget_book.pdf?)
 to assess its deficit and take immediate corrective actions—such as hiring freezes, compensation freezes, and procurement restrictions—reducing its FY 2024 budget deficit from [more than $162](https://budget.arizona.edu/sites/default/files/2024-09/university_of_arizona_fy25_unrestricted_operating_budget_book.pdf)
 million down to approximately [$63 million](https://budget.arizona.edu/sites/default/files/2024-09/university_of_arizona_fy25_unrestricted_operating_budget_book.pdf)
.

### 4\. Decentralized budgeting  

Decentralized models empower individual units to manage budgets independently. This approach fosters more efficient resource allocation, as those closest to the operations have a clearer understanding of cost drivers, revenue opportunities, and program-specific financial challenges. The [University of Colorado, Colorado Springs adopted an incentive-based budgeting system](https://www.insidehighered.com/sites/default/files/2024-03/IHE_Best%20Practices%20in%20Multi-Year%20Budgeting.pdf)
, aligning departmental decisions with institutional goals.

Tips to Implement a Future-Proof Budgeting Strategy
---------------------------------------------------

Here are key strategies that help higher education institutions achieve sustainability while managing evolving challenges.

### 1\. Adopt a hybrid budget model

Combining multiple budgeting approaches can balance financial stability and performance-driven funding. For example, pairing incremental budgeting with performance-based budgeting ensures both consistency and accountability.

[Dean Elaine Carey](https://www.linkedin.com/in/elaine-carey-3037928)
 of Oakland University [emphasizes](https://www.hanoverresearch.com/insights-blog/higher-education/6-alternative-budget-models-for-colleges-and-universities/)
 the need to balance financial urgency with institutional values. She suggests that blending budget models helps address challenges like enrollment declines and rising costs.

### 2\. Align professional development opportunities with institutional strategic objectives 

The 2022-24 Strategic Plan and Budget by the National Education Association [emphasizes aligning professional development opportunities with strategic objectives](https://www.nea.org/sites/default/files/2022-10/2022-24-strategic-plan-and-budget-10-26-22.pdf)
 to enhance decision-making and resource allocation. Equip your workforce with the skills necessary to achieve broader goals, reducing inefficiencies and boosting productivity. Train faculty in advanced educational technologies and allocate resources based on measurable outcomes, such as increased student engagement or reduced administrative workload.

### 3\. Develop more targeted, in-demand academic offerings

According to [Bain & Company’s 2023 report](https://www.bain.com/insights/financially-resilient-university/)
, institutions must rethink academic and innovation models. This can include launching new programs or adopting integrated, multidisciplinary curriculums and faculty structures. Johns Hopkins, for example, adopted this approach to expand its health-research faculty and create interdisciplinary research programs. By investing in research infrastructure, the university enabled growth in sponsored research, leading to a [7% annual increase in enrollment](https://www.bain.com/insights/financially-resilient-university/)
.

Integrate technology and automation to enhance operational efficiency. Optimize your operations by creating more flexible cost structures. Implement [collaborative budgeting tools like Limelight](https://www.golimelight.com/)
 for real-time feedback and seamless execution of innovative academic models.

### 4\. Invest in cost control without blanket cuts

Blanket budget cuts often harm essential programs disproportionately. Instead, target inefficiencies and prioritize high-impact areas like online learning and mission-critical programs. [Jake Nelson](https://www.linkedin.com/in/jake-nelson-48b3ab13b)
 from EAB [advises](https://eab.com/resources/blog/strategy-blog/cost-control-strategies-rightsize-your-university/)
 proactive strategies such as rightsizing operations and managing long-term cost growth rather than applying uniform cuts.

FAQ's
-----

### 1\. What are the biggest challenges higher education institutions face in budgeting and forecasting?

Institutions often struggle with rigid annual budgets that don’t adapt well to changing conditions, dependency on tuition revenue, unpredictable government or donor funding, and managing multiple cost centers like research, facilities, housing etc.

### 2\. What is zero-based budgeting and how can universities benefit from it?

Zero-based budgeting involves starting from a “zero base” each year so that every expense must be justified, rather than just adjusting last year’s budget. Universities using this method can better align spending with priorities, reduce waste, and ensure resources are allocated where they deliver the most value.

### 3\. How do rolling forecasts improve financial agility for colleges and universities?

Rolling forecasts allow institutions to update projections regularly (quarterly or monthly) rather than sticking to a fixed annual forecast. This lets them respond to emerging trends — like enrollment shifts, funding changes, or rising costs — more dynamically and make course corrections earlier.

### 4\. Why is predictive analytics important in higher education budgeting?

Predictive analytics helps institutions anticipate trends like course demand, faculty workload, facility usage, or revenue fluctuations. It enables more accurate forecasting, so decisions like hiring, program expansion, or cost reduction can be made proactively instead of reactively.

### 5\. How can FP&A software help higher education institutions streamline Budgeting & Forecasting?

FP&A tools help by centralizing data from different units, supporting scenario modelling (what-ifs), enabling decentralized planning with centralized consolidation, automating routine tasks, and providing real-time reporting & dashboards. All of this helps reduce errors, speed up processes, and improve decision-making.
