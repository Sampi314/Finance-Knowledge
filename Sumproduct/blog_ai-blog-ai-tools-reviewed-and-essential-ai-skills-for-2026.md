AI Blog: AI Tools Reviewed and Essential AI Skills for 2026
===========================================================

_Welcome back to our AI blog series.  Over the past year, we have explored a wide range of artificial intelligence tools, from AI copilots embedded directly in Excel to more advanced, agent-style systems capable of automating entire workflows.  These tools are no longer experimental.  They are increasingly becoming part of everyday work._

Writing all these blogs on artificial intelligence (AI), one clear theme has emerged across all these reviews: tools alone are not enough.  As AI becomes more powerful and more accessible, the real differentiator is how professionals use it and augment it in their work.

In this blog, we step back to review the AI tools we have covered so far, compare them and identify the core AI skills that will matter most in 2026.

**_Review of AI Tools Covered_**

Rather than listing every feature in detail, the table below summarises the tools and capabilities we have discussed across recent AI blogs, focusing on where they add the most value in real-world use.  Please bear in mind this matrix is arguably subjective!

![](https://sumproduct.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/image-80.png)

According to this summary table, we can find that no single tool is “the best”.  Each excels in a different context.  The most effective users are not those who chase every new release, but those who understand which tool fits which task.

**_What These Tools Have Taught Us_**

AI amplifies good workflows, but it does not fix poor ones.  If inputs are unclear or processes are broken, AI will simply scale the problem.  What’s more, prompt quality matters more than model choice.  The same tool can produce vastly different results depending on how instructions are framed.

Do not forget that human judgment remains essential.  AI can draft, calculate and summarise, but it cannot replace professional accountability.

These lessons lead naturally to a broader question: what skills should professionals focus on next?

**_The Essential AI Skills for 2026_**

As AI becomes embedded in everyday tools, technical knowledge alone is no longer sufficient.  The following skills are increasingly becoming baseline expectations rather than “nice-to-haves”.

![](<Base64-Image-Removed>)

**1\. Prompt Design as a Professional Skill**

By 2026, writing effective prompts will be as fundamental as writing clear emails or building structured spreadsheets.  Strong prompts typically:

*   set a clear role or perspective for the AI
*   define the task precisely
*   specify the desired output format
*   apply sensible constraints.

**2\. Tool Fluency, Not Tool Obsession**

It is unrealistic and unnecessary to master every AI platform.  Instead, professionals should aim to:

*   maintain a small, trusted set of tools
*   understand their strengths and weaknesses
*   use them consistently within defined workflows
*   depth of understanding beats breadth of experimentation.

This is not about tricking the model but clear communication.

**3\. Critical Evaluation of AI Output**

AI outputs often sound confident, even when they are incomplete or incorrect.  A key skill for 2026 is the ability to:

*   validate assumptions,
*   cross-check numbers and logic
*   identify when an answer “sounds right” but is wrong.

This skill is especially important in finance, modelling and advisory work.

**4\. AI-Enabled Workflow Design**

The biggest productivity gains come not from single prompts, but from embedding AI into processes.  This might include:

*   automating repetitive reporting steps
*   using AI to pre-draft analysis for review
*   combining AI with Excel, Power BI or databases.

Understanding where AI fits in a workflow is more valuable than knowing how to use it in isolation.

**5\. Ethics, Ownership and Responsible Use**

As AI becomes more powerful, professionals must remain conscious of:

*   data privacy
*   bias in generated outputs
*   whether intellectual property is generated by AI; if so, it may not be owned?
*   regulatory and compliance implications.

Responsible AI use is no longer optional; it is now part of companies’ risk management.

**_From Tools to Capability_**

The most important shift we are seeing is a move from tool adoption to capability building.  AI is no longer just software you “try out”; it is becoming infrastructure.  Those who invest time now in building strong AI habits including clear prompting, structured workflows and critical review will be best placed for 2026 and beyond.

At SumProduct, our focus remains on practical, real-world use of AI, not hype.  We will continue testing tools, sharing lessons learned and showing how AI fits into workflows that still rely upon sound judgement and domain expertise.

**_Word to the Wise_**

AI tools will continue to change rapidly.  Skills, however, compound over time.  Focus less on chasing every new model and more on learning how to work effectively with the tools already in front of you.
