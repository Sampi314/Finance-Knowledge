M&A transactions, financial modelling and cross-border opportunities
====================================================================

*   July 10, 2025

**By Diana Capetillo, Associate Director**

![A man in the office speaking on the phone to a client](https://www.gridlines.com/wp-content/uploads/gridlines_delhi_129_1I3A6569-2048x1365.jpg "M&A transactions, financial modelling and cross-border opportunities 1")

Periods of economic turmoil often trigger a surge in mergers and acquisitions (M&A) activity. While decision-makers become more cautious, turbulence also exposes opportunity. Companies that pair high growth potential with weak management can fail to weather a crisis, creating rare openings for well-run firms whose own growth has plateaued.  
  
[M&A financial modelling](https://www.gridlines.com/transaction-modelling/)
 helps to evaluate the potential financial impact.

Opportunities are identified with M&A modelling
-----------------------------------------------

But how can an M&A professional clearly identify an opportunity?  

The answer is easier said than done. It’s not just about comparing financial statements, highlighting technological aspects and finding the common mistakes.

The real answer is a disciplined assessment of the target that layers in the improvements, efficiencies, and economies of scale the acquirer can deliver. A robust M&A modelling approach translates those ideas into numbers, revealing the target’s potential and the upside for the buyer.

Amongst the main efficiency changes that a new management could bring are:  

*   *   Substantial cost savings (overheads, supply-chain, logistics, etc.).

*   *   Enhancing existing capacity through the refurbishment of capital expenditure

*   *   Accelerating growth with additional capital expenditure

*   *   Increasing revenue with a larger, better-known brand.

*   *   Implementation of more aggressive market penetration strategies.

All these key strategies should be modelled and discounted at each strategy’s discount rate to obtain the after-tax present value derived from the upsides and growth expectations. 

Of course, the present value derived from the expected acquisition, including a terminal value, must exceed the price paid for the target company. That’s how a real opportunity can be identified, with supporting figures from a comprehensive and thorough financial model. 

When considering a merger or consolidation, the performance of the combined company, its combined rating, and the value of the target company should all be assessed. It is also worth noting that purchase accounting is normally used in mergers, meaning assets and liabilities are measured at fair value rather than historical cost, and common equity is eliminated.

All the above can only apply when there is a full acquisition of the target companies. If the acquisition is partial, the following applies:

*   *   Consolidation (controlling position) – A line-by-line consolidation of financial statements is performed. All inter-company transactions are eliminated. The minority interest must also be accounted for. 

*   *   Equity Method (non-controlling, but more than 20%) – The investment in the target company is stated at net asset value. The dividends flowing to the acquirer should be treated as an adjustment to the investment. The earnings are accounted for as a percent of the target company’s earnings. 

*   *   Fair Value Method (less than 20%) – The dividends flowing to the acquirer should be treated as income.

M&A financial modelling overseas
--------------------------------

And, what happens when the opportunity lies in another country?

Well, it can all get a little bit more complex! A cross-border M&A transaction implies dealing with different legal systems, tax rules, business idiosyncrasies, corporate regulations, FX risk, impactful market conditions and consumer cultures.

These factors might affect the opportunity’s expected figures and attractiveness.

Each factor might have several implications, for instance: 

*   *   **Legal systems** – failing to understand and navigate the intricacies of a new legal system may require the aid of specialised legal advisers and can expose the acquirer to fines and reputational damage, potentially jeopardising its position in the new market.

*   *   **Tax rules** – The target may already be profitable, or have the potential to be, even under high tax rates. Yet any distributions to the parent could attract withholding tax. The target’s jurisdiction might also prohibit a step‑up in tax basis, denying the acquirer the uplift in asset value. Moreover, capital gains may be taxed at a high rate.

*   *   **Business idiosyncrasies** – Each country or region has its own way of doing business. If these nuances are overlooked, the transaction may stall. Even if a deal completes, failing to grasp local practices can hamper the target’s post‑acquisition performance, as relationships with suppliers and customers become far more challenging.

*   *   **Corporate regulations** – An M&A opportunity might also be supported by carrying forward and inheriting tax losses. However, local corporate finance regulations may prevent the acquirer from fully benefiting from tax losses, since there might be a cap on the loss that can be utilised or possibly dividends will not be allowed unless the fiscal retained earnings are positive.

*   *   **FX risk and wider market conditions** – An opportunity in a soft‑currency market may look attractive—cheaper labour, raw materials and utility bills. Yet such currencies are often highly volatile, bringing windfall gains in some periods but heavy losses in others.
        
        Other market forces can also affect the target’s performance: inflation may lift prices beyond what consumers can bear, and higher local‑currency interest rates can squeeze returns where the business is financed in that currency.
        

*   *   **Consumer cultures** – Certain countries/regions have very strong local-production consumption cultures, making market penetration difficult if the acquirer is looking for a rebranding of the target company.

Enjoy the advantages of robust M&A modelling
--------------------------------------------

In conclusion, M&A transactions can add massive organisational value. However, they are not easily executed. A proper M&A financial model will provide the numeric support required to materialise the transaction.

Thus, a comprehensive M&A financial model should include:

*   *   The corporate models for the acquirer and target company. 

*   *   The calculation of the market value of the acquired balance sheet. 

*   *   Multi-currency and FX modules, if any. 

*   *   Tax benefits, if any, such as a step-up in tax basis, inherited tax losses. 

*   *   Tax downsides, if any, such as withholding tax. 

*   *   The purchase value for the target company and additional refurbishment and investment capex deployments. 

*   *   The method under which the transaction would be financed (equity, LBO, etc.) 

*   *   Upsides and growth expectations. 

*   *   New financial performance of the merged or acquired company. 

*   *   New rating of the merged or acquired company.
        
        Professionals such as investment banks, private equity firms, and advisors often rely on various types of financial models, including those focused on building a merger model, to make data-driven decisions.
