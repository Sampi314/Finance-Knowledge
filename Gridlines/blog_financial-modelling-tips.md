Financial modelling is a core skill in finance. It involves building an abstract but realistic representation of a real-world financial situation using Excel or a similar tool.

These financial models [forecast a business’s financial performance](https://www.gridlines.com/business-modelling-and-forecasting/)
, providing direction and context for decision-making under uncertainty. In other words, a well-built financial model turns key assumptions (e.g., growth rates, costs) into projected outcomes (e.g., revenues, cash flows), serving as a powerful tool for data-driven decision-making and strategic planning.

However, financial models are only as useful as the care put into designing them. This post outlines nine essential financial modelling tips that every professional modeller should know to create accurate, robust, and user-friendly models.

Understanding financial modelling
---------------------------------

#### What is financial modelling?

At its core, financial modelling is the process of building a mathematical model to represent the financial performance of a project or business. A simple three-statement model will link the income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement using integrated formulas.

The model is typically built in Excel and integrates a company’s financial statements, key assumptions, and calculations to forecast future results.

Think of a financial model as a hypothesis-testing machine: you input hard-coded inputs like revenue growth, costs, and interest rates, and the model produces output projections like future cash flows, profits, and financial ratios.

Adjusting the inputs enables scenario planning to examine how outcomes change in different situations. Cash flow projections, in particular, are key to understanding business viability and cash flow modelling helps translate [operational](https://www.gridlines.com/operational-modelling/)
 and financing assumptions into expected results.

Why financial models matter
---------------------------

Models are crucial in business planning, investment banking, and strategic decision-making.

A well-structured financial statement model provides direction on where a company is headed by revealing its main business drivers and potential risk areas. For example, management can use the model to test how changes in market conditions or strategy would impact the company’s financial health.

Importantly, robust forecasting models also signal credibility; investors and investment banks often view a solid model as evidence that the team truly understands the business and has thought through various scenarios.

Companies use financial models for various purposes, including valuing investment opportunities, budgeting and forecasting, raising capital, and evaluating [mergers and acquisitions](https://www.gridlines.com/transaction-modelling/)
 (M&A), which may involve detailed merger models and leveraged buyout models.

In short, financial models matter because they transform uncertainty into informed decision-making, enabling stakeholders to make data-oriented decisions with confidence.

9 financial modelling tips
--------------------------

**Building a great model requires more than technical Excel skills – it demands careful planning, consistency, and an eye for detail. Here are nine financial modelling best practices that will help ensure that your models are robust, error-free, and easy to use.**

Tip 1. Have a clear model structure
-----------------------------------

Outline your financial model before building.
---------------------------------------------

Before opening Excel, take the time to plan the model’s purpose, scope, and structure. Define the questions the model needs to answer and the most important outputs it should provide. Clarifying the model’s purpose and agreeing on key content and functionality upfront will save time in the long run.

In practice, this means sketching an outline of the model on paper or a whiteboard: list the major sections (inputs, calculations, outputs) and how they will flow into each other. A bit of upfront planning acts as a blueprint, ensuring that once you start building, the model’s layout is logical and focused on the objectives. 

Clarifying your model’s purpose, key content and functionality before you start helps avoid scope creep and unnecessary complexity. With a solid outline, you’ll build a model with a clear story that is easier to follow.

#### Use appropriate sections

Structuring your model into well-defined sections is a hallmark of best practice. A robust financial model typically has separate sheets or tabs for inputs/assumptions, calculations, and outputs/reports. For example, you might have an Assumptions or Input tab (for all dynamic input sections), one or more Calculation tabs (for interim computations and financial statement outputs), and a Summary tab (for outputs and sensitivity modelling).

Having a cover sheet describing the business unit, purpose, and versioning is also good practice. This structure improves readability, especially when you’re building or reviewing cash flow analysis or sensitivity tables.

Tip 2. Use consistent formatting
--------------------------------

#### Colour-coding

A consistent colour-coding convention in your spreadsheets will significantly enhance the model’s readability. Different modellers have different opinions about which colours to use. The colours themselves don’t matter much. What matters is that you adopt a standardised approach within your team and stick to it. 

The goal is for anyone reviewing the model to be able to instantly distinguish between an input (which can be changed) and a calculated cell (which should not be overwritten).

For instance, **consistency** is key – pick a scheme and apply it uniformly across the entire workbook. Differentiating inputs from outputs with clear colours or highlights makes them easily identifiable and distinguishable. This reduces confusion and prevents accidental edits to formulas.

#### Labelling

Think of labelling as adding signposts throughout your model. They guide the user and document what each part of the model represents.

Clear labelling is essential for a self-explanatory model. Label types of cells, units, and data context clearly (e.g., “Capital Expenditures (USD)”, “Debt Balance (End of Year)”). Good labelling minimises the chance of user error, especially in large or complicated formula structures.

Use consistent naming conventions and avoid abbreviations that aren’t obvious. If necessary, add brief comments or a notes section to explain complex calculations.

Tip 3. Validate your data
-------------------------

#### Use reliable sources

With financial modelling, your model is only as good as the integrity of its inputs. So, always source your input data and assumptions from reliable, up-to-date sources.

If you’re modelling a company’s financials, use the official financial statements or audited reports for historical figures. For market assumptions (like growth rates or commodity prices), rely on reputable industry reports, market research, or government data. Using credible sources improves accuracy and builds trust in your model’s outputs. 

Whenever possible, document the source of key inputs (for example, in a footnote or a separate assumptions documentation tab). In practice, this could involve cross-checking a revenue figure against the company’s annual report or ensuring that your economic forecasts align with central bank data. You avoid basing decisions on faulty or outdated information by grounding your model in reality.

#### Cross-check for accuracy

In addition to sourcing data carefully, it’s vital to cross-check and sense-check your model’s calculations. Mistakes can happen. A pasted value might be off, or a formula could reference the wrong cell. 

Implement processes to verify that the model’s outputs make sense. For instance, reconcile your model’s summary figures against known benchmarks: 

*   Does Year 1 total revenue in the model match the actual reported revenue?
*   Do the cash flow projections roughly align with historical trends or industry peers?

If you have alternate calculations or back-of-the-envelope estimates, compare them to the model’s results as a gut check.

It’s also helpful to use Excel’s built-in tools for error checking, such as tracing precedents and dependents, to ensure formulas are pulling the correct inputs. 

Ultimately, verifying accuracy is about **being sceptical of your own financial model** and testing it from different angles.

Run extreme scenarios (what if sales drop 50%?) to see if outputs still behave logically. This rigorous cross-checking will catch errors early and give end-users confidence that the model has been thoroughly vetted.

Tip 4. Financial modelling error checks
---------------------------------------

#### Use error flags 

#### Even with careful building, errors or inconsistencies can creep into a financial model. That’s why professional models include dedicated error checks to flag issues immediately.

If a check fails, the model can display an obvious flag (like the word “ERROR” or a cell highlighted in red). It’s good practice to aggregate these checks in one place, such as a “Checks” tab or a summary section, so you can quickly review if the model integrity is intact.

Scanning your error check indicators lets you know immediately if something has gone off. For instance, include checks for balance sheet balancing, cash flow statement consistency (opening cash + net cash flow = closing cash), or that key calculated totals match between schedules and main statements.

Building in these safeguards will protect you from presenting flawed outputs. If an error flag does show up, investigate and fix it before relying on the model’s results.

**However, a word of caution:** just because all of your error checks are green does not mean your financial model is correct; it just means that none of the errors you have planned for have occurred. The model could still be complete nonsense, though. Check, and check again. Then have somebody else check. 

Tip 5. Make your assumptions transparent
----------------------------------------

#### Document and explain assumptions

#### Assumptions are the foundation of any financial model – they drive the calculations and outputs. Given their importance, always document your assumptions clearly and make them transparent to the model’s users. State each key assumption in plain language (growth rates, cost estimates, discount rate, etc.), and if not self-evident, accompany them with a brief explanation or source reference. For example, if you assume a 5% annual revenue growth, you might add a note: “5% based on historical CAGR and market trend analysis”.

Transparency builds trust: stakeholders can see exactly what you’ve assumed and can challenge or change those inputs if needed. One best practice is to use an **Inputs** or **Assumptions** tab, where all major inputs are listed in a well-organised manner (perhaps grouped by category, such as revenue drivers, expense assumptions, and financing assumptions, etc.), each with comments or sources noted.

By clearly documenting all assumptions and methodologies in one place, you make it easy for someone reviewing the model to understand its scenario. It also helps you remember months later why you chose a particular number.

Remember, readers often focus on assumptions, so shine a light on them with thorough documentation.

#### Centralise key assumptions

In line with the above, it’s important to **centralise your key assumptions** rather than scattering them throughout the model.

All inputs that a user may need to adjust should be easy to find, ideally on one sheet (or a few dedicated sheets), clearly labelled as input/assumption sheets.

This makes the model more user-friendly and reduces errors – you won’t accidentally hardcode a number in a formula if you discipline yourself always to pull inputs from the assumptions section. 

Centralising assumptions means, for example, you don’t enter a tax rate or growth percentage multiple times on different pages; you enter it once on the Inputs page and reference that cell wherever needed.

Professionals often structure models such that the first worksheet(s) are the input sheets, followed by calculation sheets and output sheets. Doing so allows a user to go directly to the input tab, modify a few key drivers, and see results flow through without having to hunt across multiple pages.

Additionally, centralising assumptions makes scenario analysis easier (more on that below) – you can swap out one set of inputs for another in a single location.

In summary, keep your financial model assumptions **in one central hub** and clearly signal: “These are the variables you can change.” It makes the model cleaner, more transparent, and less prone to errors.

Tip 6. Design for flexibility
-----------------------------

#### Enable easy scenario testing

#### One powerful advantage of financial modelling is its ability to test different scenarios.

Your model should be built with flexibility so that you (or stakeholders) can easily explore “what if” questions. To enable easy scenario testing, consider setting up your model to handle **multiple cases** – for instance, a base case, an upside case, and a downside case.

There are a few ways to do this. One approach is to have a scenario selector (perhaps a dropdown or an input cell) that switches the model’s assumptions between predefined sets (e.g., Scenario 1: high growth, Scenario 2: medium, Scenario 3: low).

Another method is to maintain a separate **Scenarios and Sensitivities** sheet, where you lay out different assumption sets side by side with corresponding outputs. Whichever approach you choose, the idea is to avoid having to rebuild or heavily edit the model to answer a new question – structure it to flex quickly.

By constructing a model that can run a range of scenarios and sensitivities, you’ll give management a comprehensive view of possible outcomes without requiring extensive manual work for each case. This flexibility is crucial for making decisions under uncertainty.

Tip 7. Focus on clear outputs
-----------------------------

#### Summarise results

After all the heavy lifting in the model’s calculations, make sure you present the outputs clearly and concisely.

Busy executives or clients may not have time to wade through spreadsheet tabs – they want a snapshot of the results. That’s why a **summary output** section or dashboard is so valuable.

A good practice is to have an Outputs tab that pulls together the most important results of the model: key financial metrics, charts, and perhaps a high-level financial statement summary. This page should be neatly formatted and digestible at a glance.

For instance, you might include a table of annual cash flows, a few key headline ratios (like IRR, ROI, or EBITDA margin), and perhaps a chart showing revenue and profit over time. The outputs should directly answer the model’s original purpose (e.g., “Is this project financially viable?” or “How much funding do we need?”). In modelling conventions, the output sheet is the polished product of the model – it should read almost like an executive summary.

A one-page summary with the right figures can convey the essence of the model far more effectively than a 20-page workbook. So, spend time designing the output presentation: use proper formatting, consider adding conditional formatting to highlight positives and negatives, and ensure it prints nicely if needed. 

A clear output not only aids understanding but also builds credibility, as it shows you know which results matter.

#### Highlight key metrics

Within your outputs, highlight the **key metrics** that drive decision-making. Not all numbers are equally important – identify the 5–10 metrics that a reader should definitely notice. These could include metrics such as Net Profit, Free Cash Flow, NPV, IRR, Debt Service Coverage Ratio, or Customer Lifetime Value, depending on the model’s context.

Use visual or typographical emphasis for these metrics; you might bold them, use a larger font, or employ colour coding (for instance, green for positive outcomes and red for warnings). Another idea is to include a brief commentary or call-out box for each key metric explaining what it means (“e.g. _IRR of 18% indicates the project returns 18% annually, exceeding our 10% hurdle rate_“).

If your model is for external use (such as pitching to investors), consider adding a simple **dashboard** or chart section that graphically highlights the KPIs – charts can often make trends clearer than tables. The same principle applies generally: accentuate the metrics that tell the story. For example, if the key question is “When does the business break even?” clearly shows the break-even year and revenue level. If the primary concern is cash flow, highlight the ending cash balance or any period of negative cash flow.

By prominently surfacing the critical metrics, you ensure the model’s audience quickly grasps the key takeaway insights.

Tip 8. Keep your financial model user-friendly
----------------------------------------------

#### Avoid complexity

“Simplicity is the ultimate sophistication” – this adage applies to financial models too.

So, strive to keep your model as simple as possible while still capturing the necessary detail. Overly complex or convoluted models are difficult to understand, audit, and prone to errors. 

Avoid gigantic formulas that try to do everything at once; it’s often better to break complex calculations into step-by-step components (perhaps across several rows) that are easier to follow. Use intermediate calculations and helper columns if needed to make the logic transparent. Likewise, avoid using exotic or obscure Excel tricks when a plain formula will do – remember that the model might be handed to someone else who isn’t as Excel-savvy.

A good practice is to follow the KISS principle (Keep It Simple and Short): a model isn’t a programming contest but a communication tool.

The best financial modelling advice we can give is that _a good financial model should be simple and easy to understand._ Avoid using overly complicated formulas. Instead, break down complex calculations into smaller, manageable steps. In practical terms, this might mean using separate lines for each component of a calculation (e.g., calculate subtotal A, subtotal B, then final result = A+B) instead of one monster formula. It also means avoiding unnecessary flourishes – if part of the model isn’t adding insight, consider removing it. By keeping the model logically simple, you make it far more user-friendly and reduce the learning curve for anyone new who needs to work with it.

#### Provide user instructions

#### A professional model often comes with a brief “how-to” guide or, at the very least, clear instructions embedded within it. Remember that the end user of your model might not be the person who built it – they could be a colleague, a client, or even _you_ six months from now (when you’ve forgotten the details!).

To make life easier, include a tab or section with **user instructions**. This could be a short note on the cover sheet that explains how the model is structured and how to operate it (e.g., “Step 1: Update the yellow cells in the Assumptions tab with the latest data. Step 2: Select a scenario from the dropdown on the Dashboard tab. Step 3: Review the updated outputs.”). Some models even have an ‘Instructions’ tab that walks new users through the model’s layout. Additionally, within the model, use consistent visual cues: for instance, clearly mark input cells (with a specific colour or border) and consider including a legend that reminds users what that formatting means.

The overall idea is to **design the model for an inexperienced user** – don’t assume they know what you know. Provide guidance wherever necessary. Ultimately, a user-friendly model is one that someone can pick up and use without needing an hour-long explanation. If you hand your model to a colleague and they can find their way around it smoothly, you’ve done a great job making it user-friendly.

Tip 9. Review, test, and update your financial model regularly
--------------------------------------------------------------

#### Check your formulas

#### Building the model is only part of the journey – you also need to review and test it rigorously. This means reviewing your formulas to ensure they are accurate and robust.

A helpful technique is to use Excel’s formula auditing tools (like Trace Precedents/Dependents and Evaluate Formula) to step through complex calculations.

Another best practice is to have a fresh pair of eyes review the model if possible – often, a colleague can spot issues you’ve overlooked. You should test the model under various scenarios to see if it behaves as expected. For instance, try zeroing out a major revenue stream; does the model’s output adjust logically, or does it break somewhere? If you increase an expense assumption dramatically, do related ratios and cash flows respond correctly? This kind of stress-testing will help ensure the model doesn’t have hidden errors that only appear in edge cases.

It’s also wise to build in some **self-auditing checks** (as discussed earlier) and verify that all those check cells are indeed showing no errors each time you run the model. Always keep in mind the question, _“Am I making this model easily auditable?”_ throughout the building process, which essentially forces you to structure things clearly and check as you go. After completing the model, conduct a thorough review pass: reconcile totals, ensure consistency across sheets, and confirm that all references point where they should. This meticulous review process is what separates a polished, professional model from one that is merely okay.

Remember, a financial model might be used to make significant decisions – it’s worth the extra effort to double-check every calculation.

#### Update with new data and feedback

A financial model is a living document. As new actual data becomes available or when assumptions change, you should update your model to keep it current and relevant.

Make it a habit to regularly revisit your model – for example, update it with the latest quarterly results or any significant changes in the business environment.

Regular updates will keep the model accurate and useful for ongoing decision-making. Additionally, you should incorporate feedback from model users.

If executives or team members find certain outputs unclear, refine the model’s presentation to ensure clarity. If they ask, “What about X scenario?” consider adding that flexibility if it’s within scope. Over time, models can also become bloated or convoluted as changes layer on; don’t be afraid to refactor or simplify sections during updates to maintain clarity. The best models evolve with the business – they are not one-and-done.

In practical terms, updating might mean adding a new column for the next year’s forecast once the year ends and last year’s actuals are known, or it could mean revising the cost assumptions after negotiating a new supplier contract. By keeping the model current, you ensure it remains a reliable tool rather than a static snapshot. Also, stay vigilant for any errors that might creep in with new data – re-run your checks and tests after each significant update.

In summary, treat your model as an ongoing project: regularly revisit, test, and refine it to reflect the latest data and insights. This way, it will continue to serve its purpose and provide value over the long term.

In summary: key tips for financial modelling
--------------------------------------------

In conclusion, here are the key takeaways to remember when building or refining a financial model:

*   **Plan and structure ahead:** Start with a clear purpose and outline. Organise the model into logical sections (inputs, calculations, outputs) for clarity.
*   **Be consistent in formatting:** Apply consistent colour codes and uniform styles. Clearly label every item so the model is easy to read.
*   **Verify data and assumptions:** Use credible sources for all inputs and double-check everything. Cross-verify key outputs and ensure the model’s calculations align with reality and expectations.
*   **Build-in error checks:** Include automatic checks (balance sheet balances, etc.) and flags to catch errors early. Protect important formula cells or sheets to prevent accidental changes.
*   **Transparency is key:** Document your assumptions and make them accessible. Centralise inputs on a dedicated sheet and explain the rationale behind them for full transparency.
*   **Stay flexible:** Design the model to handle various scenarios. Avoid hard-coded values so you can easily tweak assumptions. Use scenario analysis tools or a scenario manager approach to quickly test different cases.
*   **Emphasise outputs:** Provide a clear summary of results. Highlight the key metrics that matter for decisions, using visuals or emphasis to draw attention to those numbers.
*   **User-friendliness:** Keep the model as simple as possible. Minimise complexity in formulas and layout. Include instructions and guides within the model, and clearly distinguish input cells so that a new user can navigate it confidently.
*   **Continuous review:** Finally, always test your model and update it regularly. Review formulas with a fresh perspective or through peer review, and continually update the model to incorporate new data and feedback over time.

By adhering to these principles, you’ll create financial models that are robust, reliable, and respected by all who use them. Good modelling practice is as much about discipline and clarity as it is about technical skill.

FAQ’s
-----

### How to improve a financial model?

Start by ensuring consistency in formatting, such as using standard colour codes for inputs and formulas. Centralise assumptions in one tab, label all inputs clearly, and avoid hard-coded numbers within formulas. Add error checks, balance confirmations, and scenario toggles to test how sensitive your model is to changes in key assumptions.

### How do I prepare for financial modelling?

To prepare for financial modelling, begin by building a solid understanding of financial statements, particularly the income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement, and how they link together in a three-statement model. Consider taking a reputable [financial modelling course](https://www.gridlines.com/financial-modelling-course/)
 or working through templates to build hands-on experience.

### What is 3-way financial modelling?

A 3-way financial model is a type of financial model that integrates a company’s income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement into a single, connected model. These three statements are interlinked through formulas, so changes in one statement automatically update the others.
