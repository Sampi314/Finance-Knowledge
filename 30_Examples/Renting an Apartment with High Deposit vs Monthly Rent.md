# Renting an Apartment with High Deposit vs Monthly Rent

## Setup

We are deciding between two rental options for a $200,000 apartment over a 2-year period. One option involves a large upfront deposit with no monthly rent (similar to the Jeon-se system in South Korea), while the other requires a smaller deposit but charges monthly rent. We need to evaluate which option is truly cheaper by accounting for the time value of money (our opportunity cost).

## Assumptions

- **Apartment Value:** $200,000
- **Lease Term:** 2 years
- **Opportunity Cost:** 8.2% annual return on investments
- **Option 1 (High Deposit, No Rent):** $150,000 deposit upfront, $0 monthly rent. Deposit returned at the end of Year 2.
- **Option 2 (Low Deposit, Monthly Rent):** $10,000 deposit upfront, $1,000 monthly rent ($12,000/year). Deposit returned at the end of Year 2.

## Walk-through

1. **Calculate nominal (unadjusted) costs:**
   - With Option 1, you pay $150,000 upfront and get $150,000 back later. On paper, it looks like you lose $0.
   - With Option 2, you pay a $10,000 deposit and $24,000 in total rent over two years, then get the $10,000 back. On paper, it looks like you lose $24,000.

2. **Identify the difference in upfront capital:**
   - Under Option 1, you are paying $140,000 *more* upfront compared to Option 2 ($150,000 - $10,000 = $140,000).
   - This $140,000 represents capital that is tied up and cannot be invested elsewhere.

3. **Calculate the opportunity cost of tied-up capital:**
   - **Year 1 Lost Earnings:** The $140,000 could have earned 8.2% over the first year. $140,000 * 8.2% = ~$11,480 (rounded to $12,000 for simplicity).
   - **Year 2 Lost Earnings:** The $140,000 plus the Year 1 earnings could have earned another 8.2% in the second year. ($140,000 + ~$12,000) * 8.2% = ~$12,464.
   - Total lost earnings over two years = ~$24,000.

## Result

Once you account for the opportunity cost of tying up an extra $140,000 in deposit, Option 1 actually "costs" you the same ~$24,000 in lost potential returns as the outright rental cost of Option 2.

## What this teaches

- **A zero-rent option isn't free:** Even if money is returned fully later, giving up liquidity has a real cost.
- **Always compare similar cash flow timings:** You cannot directly compare cash paid today with cash returned years later without discounting.
- **Opportunity cost drives the decision:** If your personal investment return (opportunity cost) is higher than 8.2%, Option 1 becomes even more expensive than paying monthly rent. If it is lower, Option 1 becomes the better deal.
