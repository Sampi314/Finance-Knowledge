# Double-entry journal example

## Setup

We will look at two basic transactions to illustrate how debits and credits balance in the general ledger.

## Assumptions

- **Scenario 1:** A company purchases $250,000 in equipment using cash.
- **Scenario 2:** A company makes a $20,000 sale to a customer on credit (the customer hasn't paid cash yet).

## Walk-through

**Scenario 1: $250,000 Cash Purchase of Equipment**
1. The company receives a new asset: Equipment. Since equipment is an asset, and assets increase with debits, we debit Equipment for $250,000.
2. The company pays for the asset using cash. Cash is an asset, and it is decreasing. Assets decrease with credits, so we credit Cash for $250,000.

*Journal Entry:*
- DR Equipment: $250,000
- CR Cash: $250,000

**Scenario 2: $20,000 Credit Sale to Customer**
1. The company completes a sale but has not yet received cash. Instead, it receives an IOU from the customer, known as Accounts Receivable. Accounts Receivable is an asset, and it is increasing, so we debit Accounts Receivable for $20,000.
2. The company has earned revenue from the sale. Revenue accounts naturally have a credit balance and increase with credits. Therefore, we credit Sales Revenue for $20,000.

*Journal Entry:*
- DR Accounts Receivable: $20,000
- CR Sales Revenue: $20,000

## Result

In both scenarios, the total debits exactly equal the total credits, keeping the accounting equation (Assets = Liabilities + Equity) in balance.

## What this teaches

- Every transaction has at least one debit and one credit.
- Debits increase assets and decrease other accounts, while credits increase revenue and decrease assets.
- Total debits must always equal total credits.
