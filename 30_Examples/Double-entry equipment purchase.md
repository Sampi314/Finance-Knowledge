# Double-entry equipment purchase

## Setup

We are tracking a corporate purchase to illustrate how a single business transaction affects two different accounts under the double-entry bookkeeping system.

## Assumptions

- Alpha Company purchases new furniture for its office.
- The total cost of the furniture is $5,000.
- Alpha Company pays for the furniture immediately using cash.

## Walk-through

1. Alpha Company acquires a new asset: office furniture. Because furniture is an asset account, it must be increased. Asset accounts are increased with a debit. Therefore, we record a $5,000 debit to the Furniture (or Equipment) account.
2. Alpha Company pays for the furniture with cash. Because cash is also an asset account, and the company's cash balance is going down, it must be decreased. Asset accounts are decreased with a credit. Therefore, we record a $5,000 credit to the Cash account.

## Result

The resulting journal entry is:
- **Debit:** Furniture ($5,000)
- **Credit:** Cash ($5,000)

Because the debit amount equals the credit amount, the accounting equation remains in perfect balance. Total assets remain unchanged because one asset (Furniture) went up by $5,000 while another asset (Cash) went down by $5,000.

## What this teaches

- Every financial transaction impacts at least two accounts.
- A debit entry is required to increase an asset account, while a credit entry is required to decrease an asset account.
- The total debits must always equal the total credits after a transaction is recorded.
