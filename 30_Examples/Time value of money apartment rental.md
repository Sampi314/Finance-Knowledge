# Time value of money apartment rental

## Setup

We are comparing two ways to rent an apartment to understand how the time value of money and opportunity cost affect real-world decisions. Many people assume paying a larger refundable deposit to avoid monthly rent is a better deal because "you get your money back," but this ignores the opportunity cost of capital.

## Assumptions

- **Apartment Value:** $200,000
- **Opportunity Cost of Capital:** 8.2% per year
- **Holding Period:** 2 years
- **Option 1 (Large Deposit, No Rent):** $150,000 deposit upfront, $0 monthly rent. Deposit is returned after 2 years.
- **Option 2 (Small Deposit, Monthly Rent):** $10,000 deposit upfront, $12,000 annual rent ($1,000/month). Deposit is returned after 2 years.

## Walk-through

1. **Calculate the cash outlay difference.**
   Option 1 requires a $150,000 deposit, while Option 2 requires a $10,000 deposit. The difference is $140,000. Under Option 1, this $140,000 is tied up for 2 years.
2. **Calculate the opportunity cost for Year 1.**
   By tying up the extra $140,000, you miss out on earning a return on it.
   Year 1 Lost Earnings = $140,000 * 8.2% = ~$11,480 (rounded to ~$12,000 for simplicity).
3. **Calculate the opportunity cost for Year 2.**
   You miss out on returns for both the initial $140,000 and the compounded returns you would have earned in Year 1.
   Year 2 Lost Earnings = ($140,000 + ~$12,000) * 8.2% = ~$12,464.
   Total Lost Earnings over 2 years is roughly ~$24,000.
4. **Compare the true costs.**
   - Option 1 has no rent but costs you ~$24,000 in lost potential investment earnings.
   - Option 2 costs you exactly $24,000 in rent ($12,000/year * 2 years).

## Result

Both options cost essentially the same at an 8.2% opportunity cost. While Option 2 "looks" worse on paper because you are paying rent that you don't get back, Option 1 costs the same in terms of the time value of money because of the earnings you forfeit by tying up a larger deposit.

## What this teaches

- Money tied up in a deposit has an opportunity cost, which represents the time value of money.
- You must always consider what you *could have earned* with capital when comparing upfront payments to ongoing expenses.
- $150,000 received back in two years is worth less than $150,000 today.
